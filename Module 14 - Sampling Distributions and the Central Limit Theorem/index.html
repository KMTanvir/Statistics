Sampling Distributions and the Central Limit Theorem

Module 14  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Explain the difference between a population distribution and a sampling distribution
  • Describe the sampling distribution of the sample mean and calculate its standard error
  • State the Central Limit Theorem and identify when it applies
  • Explain how sample size affects the spread of the sampling distribution
  • Apply the Central Limit Theorem to compute probabilities about sample means and sample proportions
  • Recognize the practical importance of this theorem for the inference methods covered in later modules
Why this module matters: nearly every statistical method from Module 15 onward, confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, regression inference, rests on the ideas introduced here. This is the conceptual bridge between probability theory (Modules 8 through 13) and statistical inference (Modules 15 through 17).

Three Distinct Distributions

Before this module, "distribution" referred to how individual data values are spread out. This module introduces a second, distinct kind of distribution, one that describes a summary statistic rather than individual values.

Population distribution
The distribution of individual values across an entire population, with parameters μ and σ.
Sample distribution
The distribution of individual values within one particular sample of size n, summarized by the sample mean x̄ and sample standard deviation s.
Sampling distribution (the new idea)
The distribution of a statistic, such as the sample mean x̄, across all possible samples of size n that could be drawn from the population.
The most common confusion in this module: the sampling distribution is not about one sample's data values. It is about how the sample mean itself would vary if the sampling process were repeated many times.

Building Intuition: Repeated Sampling

Imagine a population of rice farmers in a district with a true mean landholding of μ=1.2 hectares and σ=0.6 hectares. Suppose 1,000 different researchers each independently survey a random sample of 30 farmers and calculate the sample mean landholding.

What happens across those 1,000 sample means?
• They will not all equal 1.2 hectares exactly, sample means vary from sample to sample.
• Most will cluster fairly close to 1.2 hectares.
• A few, by chance, will land further away.
• If plotted as a histogram, the 1,000 sample means form a distribution of their own: the sampling distribution of the mean.
In practice, only one sample is ever actually collected. The sampling distribution is a theoretical construct describing what would happen across all possible samples, which allows conclusions to be drawn from just the one sample in hand.

The Center of the Sampling Distribution

A remarkable and useful fact: the mean of the sampling distribution of x̄ equals the population mean μ, regardless of sample size.

\[ \mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu \]

This property is called unbiasedness: the sample mean does not systematically overestimate or underestimate the population mean. On average, across many repeated samples, the sample means center exactly on the true population value.

Unbiasedness is about the long-run average across repeated sampling, not about any single sample. Any individual sample mean can still land above or below μ.

The Standard Error

The spread of the sampling distribution of x̄ is measured by its standard deviation, given a special name: the standard error of the mean.

\[ \sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

Here σ is the population standard deviation of individual values, and n is the sample size. The standard error measures how much sample means are expected to vary from sample to sample, which is conceptually distinct from σ, which measures how much individual values vary within the population.

A frequent terminology mix-up: "standard deviation" (of individual values, σ) and "standard error" (of the sample mean, σ/√n) are not interchangeable terms. The standard error is always smaller than σ for any sample size greater than 1.

How Sample Size Affects the Standard Error

Continuing the landholding example, σ=0.6 hectares. The standard error shrinks as sample size grows, but not in direct proportion, it shrinks with the square root of n.

Sample size (n)Standard error (σ/√n)
100.6/√10 ≈ 0.190
300.6/√30 ≈ 0.110
1000.6/√100 = 0.060
4000.6/√400 = 0.030
Diminishing returns: quadrupling the sample size from 100 to 400 only halves the standard error (from 0.060 to 0.030). This square-root relationship is why dramatically improving precision requires disproportionately larger samples, an important practical consideration in survey budgeting.

The Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is one of the most powerful results in statistics. It states that, for a sufficiently large sample size n, the sampling distribution of the sample mean x̄ is approximately normal, regardless of the shape of the population distribution from which the sample was drawn.

\[ \bar{X} \ \dot\sim\ N\left(\mu, \ \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \quad \text{as } n \text{ grows large} \]
The remarkable part: the population itself can be skewed, bimodal, uniform, or any other shape. The theorem says nothing about the population's shape needing to be normal, only about the shape of the distribution of sample means becoming approximately normal as n increases.

When Does the CLT Apply?

If the population is already normal
The sampling distribution of x̄ is exactly normal for any sample size, even n=2.
If the population is not normal
The sampling distribution of x̄ becomes approximately normal as n increases. A common rule of thumb is n ≥ 30, though more skewed populations may require larger n.
Important caveat: samples must be independent and identically distributed (drawn randomly from the same population) for the theorem to apply. Severely skewed populations, or populations with extreme outliers, may require sample sizes well beyond 30 before the normal approximation becomes reliable.

Watching the Theorem Work

Picture a strongly right-skewed population, such as household income in a district, where most households earn modestly but a small number earn very high incomes, pulling the mean above the median.

As sample size n increases:
• n=2: the sampling distribution of x̄ is still noticeably skewed, resembling the population.
• n=10: the skew softens, the distribution starts rounding into a bell-like shape.
• n=30 or more: the sampling distribution is approximately symmetric and bell-shaped, even though individual incomes remain heavily skewed.
This progressive smoothing toward normality, regardless of the original skew, is exactly what the Central Limit Theorem guarantees, and is the reason normal-based methods remain usable for analyzing averages of skewed data.

Worked Example: Probability for a Sample Mean

Birth weights in a district have μ=3.1 kg and σ=0.5 kg (population shape unspecified). A clinic records a random sample of n=49 births. What is the probability that the sample mean birth weight is below 3.0 kg?

\[ \sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{0.5}{\sqrt{49}} = \frac{0.5}{7} \approx 0.0714 \]
\[ z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma_{\bar{x}}} = \frac{3.0-3.1}{0.0714} \approx -1.40 \]
From the standard normal table, Φ(-1.40) ≈ 0.0808. There is about an 8.1% chance that this sample's mean birth weight falls below 3.0 kg.
Notice the z-score formula looks identical to Module 12's z-score for individual values, but here σ is replaced by the standard error σ/√n, since the variable being standardized is a sample mean, not a single observation.

A Critical Distinction: One Observation vs. a Sample Mean

Using the same birth weight population (μ=3.1 kg, σ=0.5 kg), compare two different questions.

P(one randomly chosen baby weighs below 3.0 kg)
z = (3.0-3.1)/0.5 = -0.20. Φ(-0.20) ≈ 0.4207, about 42.1%. Uses σ directly, requires the population itself to be normal for this calculation to be exact.
P(mean of 49 babies weighs below 3.0 kg)
z = (3.0-3.1)/0.0714 ≈ -1.40. Φ(-1.40) ≈ 0.0808, about 8.1%. Uses the standard error σ/√n, and is approximately valid by the CLT even if the population is not normal.
The error to avoid: using σ instead of σ/√n (or vice versa) produces a probability for the wrong question entirely. Sample means vary far less than individual values, which is exactly why averages are more precise estimates than single observations.

The Central Limit Theorem for Proportions

The same logic extends to sample proportions. If p is the true population proportion and n is the sample size, the sampling distribution of the sample proportion p̂ is approximately normal when n is large enough.

\[ \hat{p} \ \dot\sim\ N\left(p, \ \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\right), \quad \text{when } np \geq 10 \text{ and } n(1-p) \geq 10 \]
Worked example: a survey finds that 40% of households in a district (p=0.40) use mobile banking. For a random sample of n=200 households, the standard error of p̂ is √(0.40 × 0.60 / 200) = √0.0012 ≈ 0.0346.
This is the same condition encountered in Module 12's normal approximation to the binomial: a sample proportion is simply a rescaled count of successes, so the same large-sample conditions apply.

Anchor Case: Monitoring Average Daily Wage

A labor ministry monitors the average daily wage of informal sector workers, with a historical population mean of μ=450 taka and σ=80 taka. This month's random sample of n=64 workers produces a sample mean of 430 taka. Is this a meaningfully low reading, or plausible random sampling variability?

\[ \sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{80}{\sqrt{64}} = \frac{80}{8} = 10 \]
\[ z = \frac{430-450}{10} = -2.0 \]
Φ(-2.0) ≈ 0.0228. A sample mean this low or lower would occur only about 2.3% of the time if the true average wage were still 450 taka, suggesting this month's reading is unusually low and worth investigating rather than dismissing as ordinary sampling variation.

Common Pitfalls

Confusing σ with σ/√n
Using the population standard deviation when the question is about a sample mean (or vice versa) gives the wrong probability entirely.
Assuming small samples are automatically safe
For strongly skewed populations, n=30 may not be large enough for the normal approximation to be reliable; the appropriate threshold depends on how non-normal the population is.
Non-random sampling breaks everything: the CLT assumes independent, randomly drawn samples. If a sample is biased (for example, convenience sampling that systematically over-represents certain groups), the sampling distribution theory described in this module does not protect against that bias. The CLT addresses random variability, not systematic bias.

Module 14 Recap

  • 1A sampling distribution describes how a statistic, such as the sample mean, would vary across repeated random samples.
  • 2The mean of the sampling distribution of x̄ equals the population mean μ (unbiasedness).
  • 3The standard error, σ/√n, measures the spread of the sampling distribution and shrinks as sample size grows.
  • 4The Central Limit Theorem states that the sampling distribution of x̄ becomes approximately normal as n grows, regardless of the population's shape.
  • 5A common rule of thumb is n ≥ 30, though more skewed populations require larger samples.
  • 6The same logic applies to sample proportions, with standard error √(p(1-p)/n).
  • 7The CLT corrects for random sampling variability, not for bias introduced by non-random sampling methods.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: "The Survey Looks Wrong Because the Average Changed"

A district health office reports that this quarter's sample mean clinic visit time (from a random sample of n=36 patients) is 22 minutes, compared to a long-standing historical mean of μ=20 minutes with σ=6 minutes. A supervisor immediately concludes that something has changed at the clinic.

Before concluding something changed, what should be calculated?

Any difference between this quarter's sample mean and the historical mean can look like meaningful evidence of change, especially when reported as a single headline number.
The standard error is σ/√n = 6/√36 = 1.0 minute, giving z=(22-20)/1.0=2.0. Φ(2.0)≈0.9772, so P(sample mean ≥ 22)≈1-0.9772=0.0228, about 2.3%. This is a fairly uncommon outcome under the historical assumption, so it is reasonable to suspect a real change, but the supervisor should base that conclusion on this calculated probability rather than on the raw two-minute difference alone, since sampling variability alone could occasionally produce a difference this large.

Case 2: Two Surveys, Two Different Standard Errors

Two separate research teams survey household electricity expenditure in the same district. Team A samples n=25 households; Team B samples n=400 households. Both report sample means close to the true population mean, but a stakeholder asks which team's result should be trusted "more."

How should sample size factor into the comparison?

If both sample means landed close to the true value, it might seem like both surveys are equally reliable and the sample size difference does not matter much.
Team B's standard error is √(25/400)=1/4 as large as Team A's (since σ/√400 = (σ/√25)/4), meaning Team B's sample mean is drawn from a much tighter sampling distribution and is far less likely to have landed close to μ purely by chance. Team A's result, even though it happened to land close this time, carries substantially more sampling uncertainty, which would show up clearly if either survey were repeated multiple times.

Case 3: Applying the Wrong Standard Deviation

A student calculates the probability that a sample mean of 40 test scores falls below 65, using μ=70 and σ=12, but plugs σ=12 directly into the z-score formula instead of the standard error.

What is wrong with this calculation, and how would correcting it change the answer?

Since the z-score formula in Module 12 used σ directly, it is an easy habit to carry that same formula over to sample mean problems without adjusting it.
The student's incorrect calculation gives z=(65-70)/12≈-0.417, Φ(-0.417)≈0.338. The correct calculation uses the standard error: σx̄=12/√40≈1.897, giving z=(65-70)/1.897≈-2.636, Φ(-2.636)≈0.0042. The two answers differ by nearly a factor of 80, illustrating how serious this common error is: using σ instead of the standard error treats a sample mean as if it were a single observation, drastically overstating how much sample means actually vary.

Case 4: A Skewed Population, A Trusted Average

A microfinance institution tracks loan sizes, which are strongly right-skewed (many small loans, a few very large ones). An analyst wants to test whether this month's average loan size, based on a sample of n=80 loans, differs from the historical average, and a colleague objects: "You can't use normal-based methods, the data isn't normal."

Is the colleague's objection valid?

It is easy to conflate "the individual data is skewed" with "the sampling distribution of the mean must also be skewed," since both involve the same underlying numbers.
The colleague is conflating the population distribution (which is indeed skewed) with the sampling distribution of the sample mean (which, by the Central Limit Theorem, becomes approximately normal for a sufficiently large n, such as 80). Normal-based methods are not being applied to the individual skewed loan amounts; they are applied to the sampling distribution of the mean, which the CLT justifies. The objection would be valid only if n were too small for the CLT to kick in given how strongly skewed the loan-size distribution is.
