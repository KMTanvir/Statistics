Estimation and Confidence Intervals

Module 15  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Distinguish a point estimate from an interval estimate
  • Construct and interpret a confidence interval for a population mean, both when σ is known and when it must be estimated
  • Construct and interpret a confidence interval for a population proportion
  • Explain what a confidence level actually means, and correct common misinterpretations
  • Describe how confidence level and sample size each affect interval width
  • Determine the sample size needed to achieve a target margin of error
From theory to practice: Module 14 established that sample statistics vary from sample to sample, following a predictable sampling distribution. This module uses that knowledge to turn a single sample's result into a defensible estimate of an unknown population value, complete with a built-in measure of uncertainty.

Point Estimates and Interval Estimates

Point estimate
A single number, calculated from sample data, used as a best guess for an unknown population parameter. The sample mean x̄ estimates μ; the sample proportion p̂ estimates p.
Interval estimate
A range of plausible values for the population parameter, built around the point estimate, that also communicates how much uncertainty surrounds that estimate.
Why a single number is not enough: reporting "the average monthly income is 18,500 taka" gives no sense of how precise that figure is. An interval estimate, such as "between 17,800 and 19,200 taka," conveys both the estimate and its precision in one statement.

The Margin of Error

A confidence interval is built by adding and subtracting a margin of error from the point estimate.

\[ \text{Confidence interval} = \text{point estimate} \pm \text{margin of error} \]

The margin of error itself is built from two pieces: a critical value (reflecting the desired confidence level) multiplied by the standard error (reflecting the sampling distribution's spread, from Module 14).

\[ \text{Margin of error} = (\text{critical value}) \times (\text{standard error}) \]
Everything in this module is really an application of one repeated idea: take a point estimate, attach a margin of error built from the standard error, and report the resulting range.

Confidence Interval for a Mean (σ Known)

When the population standard deviation σ is known, or the sample is large enough that the sample standard deviation s is a good stand-in, the confidence interval for μ uses the standard normal distribution.

\[ \bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

Here zα/2 is the critical value from the standard normal table corresponding to the chosen confidence level, and σ/√n is the standard error introduced in Module 14.

Confidence levelzα/2
90%1.645
95%1.96
99%2.576

Worked Example: A 95% Confidence Interval for a Mean

A random sample of n=64 rural households reports a sample mean monthly remittance income of x̄=8,200 taka, with a known population standard deviation of σ=1,600 taka. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true mean remittance income.

\[ \text{SE} = \frac{1600}{\sqrt{64}} = \frac{1600}{8} = 200 \]
\[ 8200 \pm 1.96(200) = 8200 \pm 392 \]
The 95% confidence interval is approximately (7,808 taka, 8,592 taka). This range of plausible values for the true population mean is reported alongside the point estimate of 8,200 taka.

What "95% Confidence" Actually Means

The confidence level describes the long-run behavior of the method used to build the interval, not the probability that this specific interval contains the true parameter.

Incorrect interpretation: "There is a 95% probability that the true mean remittance income lies between 7,808 and 8,592 taka." Once calculated, this specific interval either does or does not contain μ; there is no remaining randomness to assign a probability to.
Correct interpretation: "If this same sampling and interval-construction procedure were repeated many times, approximately 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true population mean." The 95% describes the reliability of the method, applied before the data is collected, not a probability statement about this one realized interval.

Confidence Level and Interval Width

Using the same remittance example (x̄=8200, SE=200), higher confidence levels require a larger critical value, producing a wider interval.

Confidence levelMargin of errorInterval
90%1.645(200)=329(7871, 8529)
95%1.96(200)=392(7808, 8592)
99%2.576(200)=515(7685, 8715)
The fundamental trade-off: greater confidence requires a wider, less precise interval. There is no way to simultaneously increase confidence and narrow the interval without collecting more data.

Sample Size and Interval Width

Holding confidence level fixed at 95% and σ=1600, increasing sample size shrinks the standard error (Module 14's square-root law) and therefore narrows the interval.

nSE = σ/√nMargin of error (95%)
164001.96(400)=784
642001.96(200)=392
2561001.96(100)=196
Quadrupling the sample size halves the margin of error, the same square-root relationship from Module 14, now seen in its direct effect on confidence interval precision.

When σ Is Unknown: The t-Distribution

In practice, the population standard deviation σ is almost never known. It must be estimated from the sample using the sample standard deviation s. This extra estimation step introduces additional uncertainty, which is accounted for by using the t-distribution instead of the standard normal distribution.

\[ \bar{x} \pm t_{\alpha/2, \ n-1} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]
The t-distribution's shape
Bell-shaped and symmetric like the normal distribution, but with heavier tails, reflecting greater uncertainty from estimating σ with s.
Degrees of freedom (n-1)
As n grows, the t-distribution's tails thin and it converges toward the standard normal distribution.

Worked Example: A t-Based Confidence Interval

A nutrition study measures hemoglobin levels in n=16 pregnant women, finding a sample mean of x̄=11.2 g/dL and a sample standard deviation of s=1.4 g/dL. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true mean hemoglobin level.

With n-1=15 degrees of freedom, the t-table gives t0.025,15 ≈ 2.131.
\[ \text{SE} = \frac{1.4}{\sqrt{16}} = 0.35, \qquad 11.2 \pm 2.131(0.35) = 11.2 \pm 0.746 \]
The 95% confidence interval is approximately (10.45, 11.95) g/dL.
Notice that t0.025,15≈2.131 is larger than the corresponding z-value of 1.96, reflecting the extra uncertainty from estimating σ with s when the sample size is modest.

Confidence Interval for a Proportion

Using the sampling distribution of the proportion from Module 14, a confidence interval for a population proportion p is built the same way, with the standard error of p̂ in place of the standard error of x̄.

\[ \hat{p} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} \]

This formula requires the same large-sample condition from Module 14: np̂ ≥ 10 and n(1-p̂) ≥ 10, for the normal approximation to be reliable.

Worked Example: "54% Support, Plus or Minus 3%"

A survey of n=900 randomly selected voters finds that 54% support a proposed policy. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true level of support.

\[ \text{SE} = \sqrt{\frac{0.54(0.46)}{900}} = \sqrt{0.000276} \approx 0.0166 \]
\[ 0.54 \pm 1.96(0.0166) = 0.54 \pm 0.0325 \]
The 95% confidence interval is approximately (50.75%, 57.25%), commonly rounded and reported as "54%, plus or minus about 3 percentage points."
This is exactly the familiar margin-of-error language seen in election polling. The "plus or minus 3%" is the margin of error from this formula, almost always reported at the 95% confidence level by convention, even when the confidence level itself is not stated explicitly.

Anchor Case: Estimating Vaccination Coverage

A health survey samples n=500 children in a district and finds that 410 have received a particular vaccine. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true district-wide vaccination coverage rate.

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{410}{500} = 0.82, \qquad \text{SE} = \sqrt{\frac{0.82(0.18)}{500}} \approx 0.0172 \]
\[ 0.82 \pm 1.96(0.0172) = 0.82 \pm 0.0337 \]
The 95% confidence interval is approximately (78.6%, 85.4%). A health official aiming for 90% coverage can see directly from this interval that the true rate is very likely below the 90% target, since the entire interval falls under that threshold.

Determining the Required Sample Size

Before a survey is conducted, the margin-of-error formula can be rearranged to solve for the sample size needed to achieve a target margin of error E.

\[ n = \left(\frac{z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sigma}{E}\right)^2 \qquad \text{(for a mean)} \qquad n = \frac{z_{\alpha/2}^2 \, p(1-p)}{E^2} \qquad \text{(for a proportion)} \]
Worked example: to estimate a proportion within a margin of error of 3 percentage points at 95% confidence, with no prior estimate of p (using the conservative value p=0.5 that maximizes p(1-p)):
n = (1.96)2(0.5)(0.5) / (0.03)2 = 0.9604/0.0009 ≈ 1,068 respondents.
This is why national opinion polls commonly survey around 1,000 to 1,100 people: it is close to the sample size needed for a roughly 3 percentage point margin of error at 95% confidence.

Common Misinterpretations to Avoid

"95% of the data falls in this interval"
Incorrect. The confidence interval estimates the population mean or proportion, not the spread of individual data values (that is what the empirical rule from Module 12 describes).
"There's a 95% chance the true value is in this specific interval"
Incorrect, as established on Slide 6. The 95% describes the method's long-run reliability, not a probability about this one fixed interval.
A narrow interval is not automatically a good interval: a narrow confidence interval built from a biased or non-random sample is still misleading, no matter how small its margin of error looks. Confidence intervals address sampling variability, not bias, exactly as the CLT did in Module 14.

Module 15 Recap

  • 1A point estimate is a single best guess; an interval estimate adds a margin of error to communicate precision.
  • 2A confidence interval has the form point estimate ± (critical value × standard error).
  • 3The confidence level describes the long-run reliability of the method, not the probability that one specific interval contains the parameter.
  • 4Higher confidence widens the interval; larger sample size narrows it, following the square-root law from Module 14.
  • 5Use the t-distribution instead of the normal distribution when σ is unknown and must be estimated by s.
  • 6Confidence intervals for proportions follow the same logic, using √(p̂(1-p̂)/n) as the standard error.
  • 7Required sample size can be calculated in advance to target a desired margin of error.

The standard error of a sample proportion is √(p̂(1-p̂)/n), mirroring the standard error of a mean but using p̂(1-p̂) in place of σ2.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: "There's a 95% Chance the Real Number Is in Here"

A news report states a 95% confidence interval for unemployment of (6.2%, 7.8%) and explains it as "there is a 95% chance the true unemployment rate falls between 6.2% and 7.8%." A reader asks whether this explanation is accurate.

Is the news report's explanation correct?

This phrasing is extremely common in media reporting and sounds intuitively reasonable, since "95% confidence" seems to invite a probability interpretation about the specific numbers given.
Not quite. The true unemployment rate is a fixed (if unknown) number; it either does or does not lie in (6.2%, 7.8%), with no remaining randomness once the interval is calculated. The accurate interpretation is that the procedure used to construct this interval succeeds in capturing the true rate about 95% of the time it is used across repeated samples. The 95% is a property of the method, not a probability statement about this one already-computed interval.

Case 2: A Suspiciously Narrow Interval

An online poll of 50,000 self-selected respondents (people who chose to click a link and answer) reports a 95% confidence interval for support of a policy as (61.8%, 62.2%), an extremely narrow range. A campaign manager is impressed by how "precise" this estimate looks.

Should the narrowness of this interval inspire confidence in its accuracy?

A narrow interval is naturally associated with precision, and a large sample size of 50,000 reinforces the impression that this estimate must be highly reliable.
No. The interval's narrowness only reflects the large sample size; it says nothing about whether the sample represents the actual population of interest. A self-selected online sample is not a random sample, and the confidence interval formula assumes random sampling. The interval can be narrow and still centered on the wrong value entirely if the sample is systematically unrepresentative, illustrating that confidence intervals quantify sampling variability, not the separate problem of sampling bias.

Case 3: Planning a Survey Budget

A research team wants to estimate the proportion of farmers using a particular irrigation technique, targeting a margin of error of no more than 4 percentage points at 95% confidence. A team member proposes simply using last year's sample size of 200, since "it worked fine before."

Is reusing last year's sample size of 200 an adequate plan?

If a particular sample size was used successfully before, it can seem reasonable to assume it will again satisfy whatever precision target is set this time.
The required sample size should be calculated directly from the target margin of error: using the conservative p=0.5, n=(1.96)2(0.5)(0.5)/(0.04)2=0.9604/0.0016≈601. A sample of 200 falls well short of this requirement and would produce a margin of error closer to √(1.962(0.25)/200)≈0.069, about 6.9 percentage points, nearly double the 4-point target. The required sample size should be calculated from the target precision, not assumed from past convenience.

Case 4: Comparing Two Reported Estimates

Two separate studies estimate average daily wage among day laborers in the same region. Study A reports 480 taka with a 95% CI of (450, 510). Study B reports 520 taka with a 95% CI of (500, 540). A policymaker wants to know whether these two studies actually disagree.

Do these two confidence intervals suggest a real disagreement between the studies?

Comparing only the two point estimates, 480 versus 520, makes the studies look meaningfully different, a 40 taka gap.
The two intervals, (450, 510) and (500, 540), overlap between 500 and 510. This overlap suggests the difference between the two studies' point estimates is plausibly explained by ordinary sampling variability rather than a genuine underlying difference in true average wage. Overlapping confidence intervals do not prove the underlying values are equal, but they do mean the data does not provide strong evidence that the two populations actually differ, a preview of the logic behind the hypothesis tests in Module 16.
