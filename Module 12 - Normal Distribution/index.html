Normal Distribution

Module 12  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Describe the key properties of the normal distribution and explain why it appears so often in real data
  • Apply the empirical rule to estimate proportions within 1, 2, and 3 standard deviations of the mean
  • Calculate and interpret a z-score
  • Use the standard normal table to find probabilities and to solve inverse (percentile) problems
  • Apply the normal approximation to the binomial distribution, including the continuity correction
  • Recognize signs that real data does not follow a normal distribution
A change in kind, not just degree: every distribution since Module 9 (binomial, negative binomial, Poisson) has been discrete, counting whole numbers of successes or events. The normal distribution is continuous: the variable can take any value along a number line, and probabilities correspond to areas under a curve rather than heights of bars.

Properties of the Normal Distribution

The normal distribution, often called the bell curve, is the single most important continuous distribution in statistics. It is defined completely by two parameters: the mean μ and the standard deviation σ.

Symmetric and bell-shaped
The curve is a perfect mirror image around its center, with the highest point at the mean.
Mean = median = mode
All three measures of central tendency from Module 3 coincide at the same central value.
Total area equals 1
As with any continuous probability distribution, the entire area under the curve represents a total probability of 1, or 100%.
Asymptotic tails
The curve approaches, but never quite touches, the horizontal axis, extending infinitely in both directions.
Why it shows up so often: many real-world measurements, such as height, blood pressure, and measurement error, arise from the combined effect of many small, independent influences. This tendency is formalized later in the Central Limit Theorem (Module 14), which explains why averages in particular gravitate toward this exact shape.

The Empirical Rule

For any normal distribution, fixed proportions of the data fall within 1, 2, and 3 standard deviations of the mean. This is known as the empirical rule, or the 68-95-99.7 rule.

\[ P(\mu-\sigma < X < \mu+\sigma) \approx 68\%, \quad P(\mu-2\sigma < X < \mu+2\sigma) \approx 95\%, \quad P(\mu-3\sigma < X < \mu+3\sigma) \approx 99.7\% \]
Worked example: the height of adult women in Bangladesh is approximately normal with μ = 151 cm and σ = 6 cm.
• About 68% fall between 145 cm and 157 cm (μ ± 1σ).
• About 95% fall between 139 cm and 163 cm (μ ± 2σ).
• About 99.7% fall between 133 cm and 169 cm (μ ± 3σ).
Quick diagnostic use: a value beyond 3 standard deviations from the mean is rare enough (about 0.3% of cases, split between both tails) that it is often worth a second look, whether as a genuine outlier or a data entry error.

Standardizing: the Z-Score

A z-score measures how many standard deviations a value lies above or below the mean. It converts any normal distribution into a common scale for comparison.

\[ z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} \]

A positive z-score means the value lies above the mean; a negative z-score means it lies below the mean. A z-score of 0 means the value equals the mean exactly.

Worked example: a woman is 160 cm tall, in a population with μ = 151 cm and σ = 6 cm.
z = (160 − 151) / 6 = 9 / 6 = 1.5.
This woman's height is 1.5 standard deviations above the average.

The Standard Normal Distribution

Every normal distribution, once converted to z-scores, becomes the same standard normal distribution: a normal distribution with μ = 0 and σ = 1. This is what makes a single reference table useful for any normal distribution, regardless of its original mean and standard deviation.

Reading the standard normal table: the table gives Φ(z), the cumulative probability P(Z ≤ z), the area under the curve to the left of a given z-score. For example, Φ(1.5) = 0.9332 means 93.32% of the standard normal distribution lies at or below z = 1.5.
Symmetry shortcut: because the standard normal curve is symmetric around 0, Φ(−z) = 1 − Φ(z). This means a table of only positive z-values is enough to answer questions about negative z-values as well.

Three Kinds of Probability Questions

"Less than" — P(X < x)
Convert to a z-score, then read Φ(z) directly from the table.
"Greater than" — P(X > x)
Use the complement rule: P(X > x) = 1 − Φ(z).
"Between" two values — P(a < X < b)
Convert both endpoints to z-scores, za and zb, then compute Φ(zb) − Φ(za).
This mirrors the complement rule introduced in Module 8 and reused throughout Modules 9 through 11: it remains the fastest route to "greater than" or "at least" questions for the normal distribution too.

Worked Example: "Greater Than"

Exam scores for a large class follow a normal distribution with μ = 70 and σ = 10. What proportion of students scored above 85?

\[ z = \frac{85 - 70}{10} = 1.5 \]
From the standard normal table, Φ(1.5) = 0.9332.
P(X > 85) = 1 − Φ(1.5) = 1 − 0.9332 = 0.0668, about 6.7% of students scored above 85.
Notice this is the same z-score and the same probability as the height example on Slide 4: once standardized, the underlying units (centimeters or exam points) no longer matter to the calculation.

Worked Example: "Between"

Returning to the height distribution (μ = 151 cm, σ = 6 cm), what proportion of women have heights between 145 cm and 157 cm?

\[ z_1 = \frac{145-151}{6} = -1, \qquad z_2 = \frac{157-151}{6} = 1 \]
Φ(1) = 0.8413 and Φ(−1) = 1 − 0.8413 = 0.1587.
P(145 < X < 157) = Φ(1) − Φ(−1) = 0.8413 − 0.1587 = 0.6826, about 68.3%.
Confirming the empirical rule: 145 cm and 157 cm are exactly μ − 1σ and μ + 1σ. The exact calculation, 68.3%, matches the approximate 68% from the empirical rule on Slide 3, showing the rule is simply a convenient rounding of the exact standard normal areas.

Inverse Problems: Finding a Value from a Percentile

Sometimes the probability is known and the corresponding value of x is the unknown. This reverses the standard table lookup: find the z-score for the target probability first, then convert back to the original scale.

\[ x = \mu + z\sigma \]
Worked example: for the exam scores (μ=70, σ=10), what score marks the 90th percentile, the score that 90% of students scored at or below?
From the standard normal table, the z-score with Φ(z) = 0.90 is z ≈ 1.2816.
x = 70 + (1.2816)(10) = 70 + 12.816 ≈ 82.8.
A score of about 82.8 marks the 90th percentile of this exam.

The Normal Approximation to the Binomial

Recall from Module 9 that for large n, with np and n(1−p) both reasonably large (a common rule of thumb is at least 5, though 10 is often preferred), the binomial distribution is well approximated by a normal distribution with the same mean and standard deviation.

\[ \text{Binomial}(n,p) \approx \text{Normal}(\mu = np, \ \sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}) \]
The continuity correction: the binomial distribution is discrete (whole numbers only) while the normal distribution is continuous, so each whole number x is treated as covering the interval from x − 0.5 to x + 0.5. "P(X ≤ 55)" in the binomial becomes "P(X < 55.5)" when approximated by the normal, a small adjustment that meaningfully improves accuracy.

Worked Example: Normal Approximation with Continuity Correction

A fair coin (p = 0.5) is flipped 100 times (n = 100). What is the approximate probability of 55 or fewer heads?

\[ \mu = np = 50, \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \]
Applying the continuity correction, P(X ≤ 55) becomes P(X < 55.5) in the normal approximation.
z = (55.5 − 50) / 5 = 5.5 / 5 = 1.1.
Φ(1.1) = 0.8643, so P(X ≤ 55) ≈ 0.8643.
This approximate value (0.8643) is very close to the exact binomial probability, illustrating why the normal approximation is so widely used when n is large enough to make direct binomial calculation by hand impractical.

Recognizing Non-Normal Data

Skewness
If mean, median, and mode clearly differ (Module 3), or if a histogram has a long tail on one side, the data is skewed, not normal. Income and rare-event counts are commonly right-skewed.
Bounded data
Data with a hard floor or ceiling, such as percentages (0 to 100) or counts (0 and above), often cannot be normal, especially when the mean sits close to that boundary.
A common mistake: applying the empirical rule or z-score lookups to data that is visibly skewed or bounded produces probability estimates that can be badly wrong, since both tools assume the symmetric, unbounded shape of the true normal curve.
Practical check: before relying on normal-based methods, examine a histogram for symmetry, compare the mean and median, and consider whether the variable has a natural boundary that the normal distribution's infinite tails would violate.

Looking Ahead: Why Averages Tend to Be Normal

Even when individual measurements are not normal, something remarkable happens to their averages. If many independent samples are drawn from almost any distribution and their means are calculated, the distribution of those sample means becomes approximately normal as the sample size grows, regardless of the shape of the original data.

This is the Central Limit Theorem, explored in full in Module 14. It explains why normal-based methods remain useful even for studying sample averages drawn from skewed or unusual populations, and it is the foundation for nearly all of the estimation and hypothesis-testing methods later in this course.

Bringing It Together: Blood Pressure Screening

A community health program measures systolic blood pressure in a large adult population, finding an approximately normal distribution with μ = 120 mmHg and σ = 12 mmHg. The program flags anyone above 140 mmHg for follow-up.

What proportion of the population gets flagged?
z = (140 − 120) / 12 = 20/12 ≈ 1.667.
Φ(1.667) ≈ 0.9522, so P(X > 140) ≈ 1 − 0.9522 = 0.0478, about 4.8% of the population.
Planning implication: if the program expects to screen 5,000 people, it should budget follow-up capacity for roughly 5,000 × 0.0478 ≈ 239 individuals, a concrete operational number derived directly from the normal model rather than guesswork.

Module 12 Recap

  • 1The normal distribution is continuous, symmetric, and bell-shaped, fully described by μ and σ.
  • 2The empirical rule gives quick estimates: about 68%, 95%, and 99.7% of data fall within 1, 2, and 3 standard deviations of the mean.
  • 3The z-score, z=(x−μ)/σ, converts any normal distribution to the standard normal distribution for table lookups.
  • 4The standard normal table answers "less than," "greater than" (via the complement rule), and "between" probability questions.
  • 5Inverse problems reverse the lookup: find z for a target probability, then solve x=μ+zσ.
  • 6The normal distribution approximates the binomial well for large n, using the continuity correction for accuracy.
  • 7Skewed or boundary-limited data violates the assumptions behind the empirical rule and z-score lookups.
  • 8The Central Limit Theorem (Module 14) explains why sample averages tend toward normality even when individual data does not.

15 questions on the empirical rule, z-scores, the standard normal table, inverse percentile problems, and the normal approximation to the binomial.

1.Which of the following is NOT a property of the normal distribution?

The normal distribution's tails extend infinitely in both directions; it is not bounded between 0 and 1.

2.According to the empirical rule, approximately what percentage of normally distributed data falls within 2 standard deviations of the mean?

About 95% of data in a normal distribution falls within 2 standard deviations of the mean.

3.A value has z = -2. This means:

A negative z-score indicates the value lies below the mean, specifically by that many standard deviations.

4.Heights are normal with μ=151cm, σ=6cm. What is the z-score for a height of 163cm?

z = (163-151)/6 = 12/6 = 2.0.

5.If Φ(1.5) = 0.9332, what is P(Z > 1.5)?

P(Z > 1.5) = 1 - Φ(1.5) = 1 - 0.9332 = 0.0668, by the complement rule.

6.Why does a single standard normal table work for any normal distribution, regardless of its original μ and σ?

Standardizing via the z-score formula converts any normal distribution to the standard normal distribution (μ=0, σ=1), which is what the table tabulates.

7.Exam scores are normal with μ=70, σ=10. What proportion of students scored above 85?

z=(85-70)/10=1.5, so P(X>85)=1-Φ(1.5)=1-0.9332=0.0668.

8.For heights N(151,6), what is P(145 < X < 157)?

145 and 157 are μ±1σ, so P(145<X<157)=Φ(1)-Φ(-1)=0.8413-0.1587=0.6826, matching the empirical rule's 68%.

9.To find the exam score marking the 90th percentile (μ=70, σ=10, z≈1.2816 for the 90th percentile), which formula is used?

Inverse problems use x=μ+zσ, solving for x once the target z-score is known: x=70+1.2816(10)≈82.8.

10.The normal approximation to the binomial works well when:

The approximation is reliable when n is large enough that np and n(1-p) are both reasonably large, commonly at least 5 or 10.

11.The continuity correction adjusts P(X ≤ 55) in a discrete binomial to which value before applying the normal approximation?

Each discrete value x is treated as covering x±0.5, so P(X≤55) becomes P(X<55.5) for the continuous normal approximation.

12.For n=100 coin flips (p=0.5), what are μ and σ for the normal approximation?

μ=np=50, σ=√(np(1-p))=√25=5.

13.A variable's histogram shows a long right tail, and its mean is noticeably larger than its median. This indicates:

A long right tail with mean exceeding median is a classic sign of right skew, violating the symmetry assumption behind the empirical rule and z-score lookups.

14.Why might it be inappropriate to apply normal-distribution methods directly to a variable bounded between 0 and 100 (such as a percentage) when its mean is close to 95?

With the mean near a hard boundary, the symmetric, unbounded normal curve would assign meaningful probability beyond the boundary (above 100), which is impossible for the real variable.

15.The Central Limit Theorem, previewed in this module and covered fully in Module 14, states that:

The Central Limit Theorem concerns the distribution of sample means (not individual values), which approaches normality as sample size increases, regardless of the shape of the underlying population.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: Setting a Blood Pressure Screening Threshold

A health program models systolic blood pressure as normal with μ=120 mmHg, σ=12 mmHg, and flags anyone above 140 mmHg for follow-up. A colleague suggests lowering the threshold to 130 mmHg to "catch more at-risk people," without first checking how many more that would flag.

What should be calculated before deciding whether to lower the threshold?

Lowering a threshold to "catch more people" sounds purely beneficial, since it seems to only add coverage without any downside worth quantifying.
At 140 mmHg, z=(140-120)/12≈1.667, giving P(X>140)≈4.78% flagged. At 130 mmHg, z=(130-120)/12≈0.833, giving P(X>130)≈1-Φ(0.833)≈20.2% flagged, more than four times as many people. Before changing the threshold, the program should calculate exactly how many more people the new cutoff would flag and weigh that against available follow-up capacity, rather than adjusting the number on intuition alone.

Case 2: Misapplying the Empirical Rule to Income

An analyst calculates the mean and standard deviation of household income in a district and uses the empirical rule to state that "about 95% of households earn between μ-2σ and μ+2σ," even though the lower bound calculated this way is a negative income.

What does the negative lower bound reveal about this application of the empirical rule?

Since the empirical rule is a standard, well-known tool, it is tempting to apply it routinely to any numeric variable with a calculated mean and standard deviation.
A negative lower bound is impossible for income, which is clear evidence that household income is not normally distributed here, almost certainly because income is right-skewed (a long tail of high earners pulls the mean above the median, common income patterns described in Module 3). The empirical rule assumes symmetry and unbounded tails; applying it to a skewed, non-negative variable like income produces nonsensical results and should be a signal to use the actual shape of the income distribution instead.

Case 3: A Factory Calibrating Two Machines

A factory produces steel rods with two machines. Machine A produces rods with μ=50.0mm, σ=0.2mm. Machine B produces rods with μ=50.0mm, σ=0.5mm. A specification requires rods between 49.5mm and 50.5mm.

Even though both machines target the same mean, do they perform equally well against the specification?

Since both machines are centered exactly on the target of 50.0mm, it is tempting to assume they perform identically against the specification.
No. For Machine A, z=(50.5-50.0)/0.2=2.5, giving P(49.5<X<50.5)=Φ(2.5)-Φ(-2.5)≈0.9876, about 98.8% within spec. For Machine B, z=(50.5-50.0)/0.5=1.0, giving P(49.5<X<50.5)=Φ(1)-Φ(-1)≈0.6826, only about 68.3% within spec. Despite an identical mean, Machine B's larger standard deviation produces far more out-of-spec rods, illustrating why spread, not just central tendency, determines quality outcomes.

Case 4: A Survey Result That "Cannot Be Normal"

A researcher surveys satisfaction ratings on a 1-to-5 scale and finds nearly all respondents answering either 1 or 5, with very few in between. A statistics-savvy colleague proposes applying the empirical rule and z-scores to summarize the spread of these ratings.

Is the normal distribution an appropriate model here?

Since the normal distribution and z-scores are familiar, general-purpose tools, it can seem reasonable to apply them to any dataset with a calculable mean and standard deviation, including this one.
No. A distribution with most responses clustered at the two extremes (1 and 5) and almost none in the middle is bimodal, the opposite of the single-peaked, symmetric bell shape the normal distribution requires. Calculating a mean and standard deviation here may even produce a value (such as 3) that almost no actual respondent gave, and the empirical rule's 68-95-99.7 percentages would not describe this data's true spread at all. A histogram should always be examined before assuming normality, regardless of how convenient normal-based tools are.
