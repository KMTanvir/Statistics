Why Not Just Run Several t-Tests?

A development economist wants to compare average household income across four districts. A t-test compares two means at a time, so comparing four districts would require six separate pairwise t-tests.

The multiple testing problem returns: each individual t-test carries a 5% Type I error risk at α=0.05. Running six such tests on the same data, as seen in Module 16, inflates the true overall false-positive risk well above 5%, since chance alone makes at least one false "significant" difference increasingly likely as more comparisons are made.
Analysis of variance (ANOVA) solves this by testing all group means in a single test, controlling the overall Type I error rate at the chosen α.

One-Way ANOVA: Hypotheses

One-way ANOVA compares the means of k groups, defined by a single categorical factor (here, district).

Null hypothesis (H0)
μ1 = μ2 = ... = μk. All group population means are equal.
Alternative hypothesis (H1)
At least one μi differs from the others. H1 does not specify which group, or how many groups, differ.
A significant ANOVA result tells you that some difference exists among the groups, not which specific groups differ. That question is answered afterward, by post-hoc tests (Slide 11).

The Logic: Decomposing Total Variability

ANOVA compares two sources of variability in the data: how much scores vary between the group means, and how much scores vary within each group around its own mean.

Between-group variability
How far each group's mean is from the overall (grand) mean. Large if the groups truly differ.
Within-group variability
How much individual observations vary around their own group's mean. Reflects random, ordinary variation unrelated to group membership.
The core idea: if between-group variability is large relative to within-group variability, the differences among group means are unlikely to be explained by random chance alone.

Sums of Squares

This decomposition is formalized through sums of squares, which partition total variability into its two sources.

\[ SST = SSB + SSW \]

SST (total sum of squares) measures total variability of all observations around the grand mean. SSB (between-groups sum of squares) measures variability of the group means around the grand mean, weighted by group size. SSW (within-groups sum of squares, also called SSE, error sum of squares) measures variability of observations around their own group mean.

\[ SSB = \sum_{i=1}^{k} n_i(\bar{x}_i - \bar{x})^2 \qquad SSW = \sum_{i=1}^{k}\sum_{j=1}^{n_i}(x_{ij}-\bar{x}_i)^2 \]

Mean Squares and the F-Statistic

Each sum of squares is converted into a mean square by dividing by its degrees of freedom, producing an average variability per source.

\[ MSB = \frac{SSB}{k-1} \qquad MSW = \frac{SSW}{N-k} \]

Here k is the number of groups and N is the total number of observations across all groups. The F-statistic is the ratio of these two mean squares.

\[ F = \frac{MSB}{MSW} \]
F is always non-negative. A value of F near 1 suggests between-group variability is no larger than ordinary within-group noise, consistent with H0. A large F suggests the group means differ more than chance alone would explain.

The F-Distribution and the Decision Rule

Under H0, the F-statistic follows an F-distribution with (k-1) numerator degrees of freedom and (N-k) denominator degrees of freedom. Unlike the symmetric normal and t-distributions, the F-distribution is right-skewed and takes only non-negative values.

\[ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if the p-value} \leq \alpha \]
The decision rule is the same logic as every hypothesis test in Module 16: compute a test statistic, find the corresponding p-value (here, the area to the right of the observed F, since large F values are the only evidence against H0), and compare to α.

Worked Example: Comparing Three Fertilizer Types

An agricultural extension office compares average rice yield (tons/hectare) across three fertilizer types, with 5 plots tested per type (N=15, k=3).

Fertilizer AFertilizer BFertilizer C
4.1, 4.3, 4.0, 4.4, 4.2 (mean 4.2)4.6, 4.8, 4.5, 4.9, 4.7 (mean 4.7)4.0, 3.9, 4.2, 3.8, 4.1 (mean 4.0)

The grand mean across all 15 plots is 4.3. Calculation (carried out by software or by hand using the sums of squares formulas) yields SSB≈1.633, SSW≈0.328, giving:

\[ MSB = \frac{1.633}{2} \approx 0.817 \qquad MSW = \frac{0.328}{12} \approx 0.027 \qquad F = \frac{0.817}{0.027} \approx 30.1 \]

Interpreting the F-Test Result

With dfbetween=2 and dfwithin=12, an F-statistic of 30.1 falls far into the upper tail of the F-distribution, producing a p-value well below 0.001.

With H0: μA=μB=μC and α=0.05, since the p-value is far smaller than 0.05, the extension office rejects H0: at least one fertilizer type produces a different average yield than the others.
The F-test alone does not say which fertilizer differs from which. Fertilizer B's mean (4.7) looks visibly higher than A (4.2) and C (4.0), but confirming this formally requires a post-hoc test, introduced next.

Post-Hoc Tests: Which Groups Differ?

Once ANOVA establishes that some difference exists, post-hoc tests identify which specific pairs of group means differ, while still controlling the overall Type I error rate across all the pairwise comparisons being made.

Tukey's HSD (Honestly Significant Difference)
The most common post-hoc method for one-way ANOVA. It compares every pair of group means using a single adjusted critical value, so the overall error rate across all pairwise comparisons stays near the chosen α.
In the fertilizer example: Tukey's HSD would likely confirm that B differs significantly from both A and C, while A and C may or may not differ significantly from each other, a distinction the F-test alone cannot make.

Assumptions Behind ANOVA

Independence
Observations within and across groups are independent of one another, typically satisfied by random sampling or random assignment.
Normality
Within each group, the data are approximately normally distributed, or sample sizes are large enough for the Central Limit Theorem (Module 14) to apply.
Equal variances (homogeneity of variance)
The population variance is assumed equal across all groups. ANOVA is moderately robust to mild violations, especially with similar group sizes, but severe violations distort the F-test's error rates.
When variances differ sharply across groups, or sample sizes are small and badly skewed, alternative methods (such as Welch's ANOVA or non-parametric tests) are more appropriate than the standard F-test.

Effect Size: Eta Squared

As with hypothesis testing generally (Module 16, Slide 16), statistical significance does not indicate how large or practically important a difference is. Eta squared (η²) measures the proportion of total variability explained by group membership.

\[ \eta^2 = \frac{SSB}{SST} \]
Fertilizer example: η² = 1.633/(1.633+0.328) ≈ 1.633/1.961 ≈ 0.833. About 83% of the total variability in yield is explained by fertilizer type, a very large effect, consistent with the dramatic F-statistic observed.

Anchor Case: Comparing Teaching Methods Across Sections

A university department, building on Module 16's pilot of a new teaching method (Module 16, Slide 15), now compares average exam scores across four course sections taught by four different methods: lecture-only, flipped classroom, blended, and the new pilot method. Each section has 20 students (N=80, k=4).

Running one-way ANOVA gives F(3,76)=6.42, p=0.0006. At α=0.05, since the p-value is far below 0.05, the department rejects H0: at least one teaching method produces a different average exam score.
A Tukey HSD post-hoc test follows up to identify exactly which methods differ, before the department commits to department-wide changes, echoing the practical-significance caution already raised in Module 16.

Common Pitfalls

"A significant F-test means every group differs from every other group"
Incorrect. It only means at least one group differs from at least one other. Post-hoc tests are required to identify which specific pairs differ.
"A non-significant F-test proves all groups are identical"
Incorrect, for the same reason failing to reject H0 never proves H0 true (Module 16, Slide 8). It only means the evidence was not strong enough to detect a difference.
Skipping the assumption check: applying one-way ANOVA to heavily skewed data, very unequal variances across groups, or non-independent observations (such as repeated measurements on the same subjects) can produce misleading p-values, regardless of how the F-statistic itself is calculated.

Work through each scenario, then reveal the analysis.

Case 1: Six Tests Instead of One
A market researcher wants to compare average monthly spending across four customer segments. Rather than using ANOVA, the researcher runs all six pairwise t-tests at α=0.05 and finds one pair significant at p=0.04. Is this a sound basis for concluding those two segments truly differ?
Recall Module 16's multiple testing problem: each of the six tests carries its own 5% false-positive risk.
Not necessarily. Running six separate t-tests, each with a 5% Type I error risk, inflates the overall chance of at least one false "significant" result well above 5%. A single p=0.04 result among six tests is exactly the kind of finding that could arise from chance alone. The researcher should instead run one-way ANOVA first, and only follow up with a properly controlled post-hoc test (like Tukey's HSD) if the overall F-test is significant.
Case 2: A Significant F-Test, But Which Group?
A garment factory compares defect rates across three production shifts using one-way ANOVA and obtains F(2,57)=4.85, p=0.011. The factory manager announces, "the morning shift is clearly the problem." Is this conclusion justified by the ANOVA result alone?
Think about exactly what the alternative hypothesis in ANOVA does and does not claim.
No. The significant F-test only establishes that at least one shift's mean defect rate differs from the others; it does not identify which shift, or whether the morning shift specifically is the outlier. The manager's claim requires a post-hoc test, such as Tukey's HSD, comparing each pair of shifts directly before singling out the morning shift.
Case 3: A Tiny Effect Size Despite Significance
A large national study (N=4,000) compares average test scores across five regions using ANOVA and finds F(4,3995)=3.71, p=0.005, a statistically significant result. The calculated eta squared is 0.004. How should this result be reported?
Compare the p-value's message to the eta squared's message; they answer different questions.
The result should be reported with both numbers, not the p-value alone. Statistical significance (p=0.005) only reflects that, with a sample this large, even a very small true difference among regions is detectable. Eta squared of 0.004 means region explains less than half a percent of the variability in test scores, a practically negligible effect. Reporting only "statistically significant" without the effect size would overstate the finding's real-world importance, exactly the pitfall raised on Slide 15.
Case 4: Checking Assumptions Before Trusting the F-Test
A small clinical pilot compares recovery time across three treatment groups with only 5 patients per group. One group's data is heavily skewed by a single unusually slow recovery, and that group's spread of recovery times looks far wider than the other two groups. Should the standard one-way ANOVA result be trusted as reported?
Check this scenario against the three standard ANOVA assumptions from Slide 12.
Caution is warranted. Small sample sizes per group make it hard to assess normality reliably, and a visibly wider spread in one group suggests the equal-variance assumption may be violated. With both small, skewed samples and unequal variances, the standard F-test's p-value may not be trustworthy. An alternative such as Welch's ANOVA, which does not assume equal variances, or a non-parametric alternative, would be a more defensible choice here.
