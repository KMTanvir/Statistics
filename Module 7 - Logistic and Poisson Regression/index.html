Logistic and Poisson Regression

Module 7  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Explain why an outcome that is binary (yes/no) or a count (0, 1, 2, ...) needs a different regression model than a continuous outcome
  • Write and interpret the logistic regression equation, including the logit and odds ratio
  • Use a fitted logistic model to compute a predicted probability and turn it into a yes/no decision
  • Write and interpret the Poisson regression equation, including the rate ratio
  • Use a fitted Poisson model to compute a predicted count
  • Recognize overdispersion in count data and know what a negative binomial regression offers instead
  • Compare linear, logistic, and Poisson regression as three members of the same family of models
The question this module answers: Module 6 fit a straight line to a continuous outcome, such as an exam score or a yield in tons per hectare. But many outcomes that matter in practice are not continuous numbers at all: a loan either defaults or it does not, a patient either is or is not hospitalized, and the number of dengue cases in a week is a count that cannot be negative. This module extends regression to handle exactly these two outcome types.

Why Not Just Use Linear Regression?

Fitting an ordinary straight line to a binary outcome or a count outcome runs into real problems, not just stylistic ones.

Binary outcome problem
A binary outcome only takes the values 0 and 1. A straight line has no upper or lower bound, so it can predict negative probabilities or probabilities above 1, both of which are meaningless.
Count outcome problem
A count outcome can only be a non-negative whole number: 0, 1, 2, and so on. A straight line can still predict a negative count, and counts are typically right-skewed rather than evenly scattered around the line as linear regression assumes.

Both logistic and Poisson regression solve this the same way: instead of modeling the outcome directly with a straight line, they model a transformed version of the outcome that has no such boundary problem, then translate the result back to a probability or a count at the end.

Same family: linear, logistic, and Poisson regression are all members of a broader family called generalized linear models. Each pairs a type of outcome with a transformation, called a link function, that connects the straight-line part of the model to that outcome.

Logistic Regression: Predicting Yes/No

Logistic regression is used when the outcome is binary: default or no default, hospitalized or not, passed or failed. Rather than predicting the outcome itself, it predicts the probability that the outcome equals 1.

The fitted curve is S-shaped rather than straight. It stays between 0 and 1 for every possible value of x, which is exactly what a probability must do.

The trick behind the curve: the S-shape is what you get when a perfectly ordinary straight line, written on the log-odds scale, is converted back to the probability scale. The next slide shows that line.

The Logit: A Straight Line on the Log-Odds Scale

Logistic regression still fits a straight line, just not to the probability itself. It fits a line to the log-odds, also called the logit, of the probability:

\[ \text{logit}(p) = \ln\left(\frac{p}{1-p}\right) = b_0 + b_1 x \]

Here \(p\) is the probability that the outcome equals 1, and \(\frac{p}{1-p}\) is the odds of the outcome occurring. The right-hand side looks exactly like the simple linear regression equation from Module 6, because it is one, just written on a different scale.

Odds
The ratio of the probability of an event happening to the probability of it not happening. Odds of 0.25 mean the event is one-quarter as likely to happen as not to happen.
Why the log?
Odds range from 0 to infinity, still a boundary problem at zero. Taking the natural log of the odds removes that lower boundary, leaving a scale that can take any value, positive or negative, exactly what a straight line needs.
Going back to a probability: once \(b_0 + b_1 x\) is calculated, it can be converted back to a probability with \( p = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(b_0 + b_1 x)}} \), which is exactly the S-shaped curve from the previous slide.

Interpreting Coefficients: The Odds Ratio

A logistic coefficient \(b_1\) is the change in the log-odds for a one-unit increase in x. Log-odds are not an intuitive scale for most audiences, so coefficients are usually translated into an odds ratio:

\[ \text{odds ratio} = e^{b_1} \]
Odds ratioInterpretation
Greater than 1Each one-unit increase in x multiplies the odds of the outcome, making it more likely
Equal to 1x has no association with the odds of the outcome
Less than 1Each one-unit increase in x multiplies the odds by a fraction, making the outcome less likely
Odds ratio is not a probability ratio: an odds ratio of 2 does not mean the probability doubles. It means the odds, the ratio of probability to its complement, double. When the baseline probability is small, the two are close; when it is not small, they can differ noticeably.

Worked Example: Predicting Loan Default

A microfinance lender models whether a small business loan defaults (1 = default, 0 = no default) using the borrower's monthly business revenue, x, in thousand taka. The fitted model is:

\[ \text{logit}(p) = -1 - 0.03x \]
Borrower with revenue = 20: logit(p) = −1 − 0.03(20) = −1.6. Odds = e−1.6 = 0.202. Probability p = 0.202 ÷ (1 + 0.202) = 0.168, about a 16.8 percent chance of default.

Borrower with revenue = 80: logit(p) = −1 − 0.03(80) = −3.4. Odds = e−3.4 = 0.033. Probability p = 0.033 ÷ (1 + 0.033) = 0.032, about a 3.2 percent chance of default.

In plain English: each additional thousand taka of monthly revenue is associated with the odds of default being multiplied by e−0.03 ≈ 0.970, roughly a 3 percent reduction in the odds of default, holding everything else fixed.

From Probability to Decision: The Classification Threshold

A predicted probability is often turned into a yes/no decision by comparing it to a threshold, commonly 0.5: predict the outcome will occur if \(p\) exceeds the threshold, and predict it will not otherwise.

In the loan example, the borrower with revenue 20 has predicted probability 0.168, below 0.5, so the model predicts no default. Both borrowers in the worked example would be predicted as non-defaulters at this threshold, even though their estimated risk differs by a factor of five.
The threshold is a choice, not a law of nature: a lender worried about missing risky borrowers might lower the threshold to 0.2, catching more true defaulters at the cost of also flagging more borrowers who would actually have repaid. There is no single correct threshold; it depends on the relative cost of the two kinds of mistakes.

Assessing a Logistic Model

Logistic regression does not have an R-squared in the same sense as linear regression, since residuals are not defined the same way for a binary outcome. Two common alternatives are used instead.

Classification accuracy
The proportion of cases where the predicted yes/no decision, using a chosen threshold, matches the actual outcome. Simple to explain, but can be misleading when one outcome is rare.
Pseudo R-squared
Several measures exist that mimic the spirit of R-squared for logistic models, summarizing how much the predictors improve on simply guessing the overall proportion. They do not have the same direct percentage-of-variation interpretation as ordinary R-squared.
Beware of class imbalance: if only 2 percent of loans actually default, a model that predicts "no default" for every single borrower would already be 98 percent accurate while being completely useless for identifying risk. Accuracy alone can hide this kind of failure.

Poisson Regression: Predicting Counts

Poisson regression is used when the outcome is a count: the number of events occurring in a fixed period of time or area of space, such as weekly dengue cases in a district, monthly customer complaints, or the number of road accidents at an intersection per year.

This connects directly back to the Poisson distribution from Module 11: that module described the probability of observing a given count when events happen randomly at a constant average rate. Poisson regression lets that average rate itself depend on explanatory variables.

The core idea: instead of assuming every observation shares one fixed average rate, Poisson regression allows the expected count to rise or fall with x, for instance allowing the expected number of dengue cases to depend on weekly rainfall.

The Poisson Regression Equation

Counts cannot be negative, so Poisson regression fits a straight line to the log of the expected count, \(\lambda\), rather than to the count itself:

\[ \ln(\lambda) = b_0 + b_1 x \]

Equivalently, the expected count itself is:

\[ \lambda = e^{b_0 + b_1 x} \]

Because \(e\) raised to any power is always positive, \(\lambda\) can never come out negative, no matter what values x and the coefficients take. This is the same logic as the logit link in logistic regression: transform the outcome to a scale with no boundary problem, fit a straight line there, then transform back.

Interpreting Coefficients: The Rate Ratio

As with logistic regression, the raw coefficient \(b_1\) is most easily interpreted after exponentiating it, this time producing a rate ratio:

\[ \text{rate ratio} = e^{b_1} \]
Rate ratioInterpretation
Greater than 1Each one-unit increase in x multiplies the expected count upward
Equal to 1x has no association with the expected count
Less than 1Each one-unit increase in x multiplies the expected count downward
Plain-English template: "For each additional [one unit of x], the expected count of [event] is multiplied by [rate ratio], holding other predictors fixed." This is the count-data equivalent of the slope sentence used for linear regression in Module 6.

Worked Example: Predicting Dengue Case Counts

A district health office models the expected number of dengue cases per week, λ, using average weekly rainfall, x, in millimeters. The fitted model is:

\[ \ln(\lambda) = 0.5 + 0.004x \]
Week with rainfall = 100mm: ln(λ) = 0.5 + 0.004(100) = 0.9. λ = e0.9 = 2.46 cases per week.

Week with rainfall = 300mm: ln(λ) = 0.5 + 0.004(300) = 1.7. λ = e1.7 = 5.47 cases per week.

In plain English: each additional millimeter of weekly rainfall is associated with the expected number of dengue cases being multiplied by e0.004 ≈ 1.004, about a 0.4 percent increase in expected cases per millimeter, holding other factors fixed. Over 200 extra millimeters this compounds into more than double the expected case count.

Overdispersion: When the Poisson Assumption Breaks

The Poisson distribution makes a specific assumption: the variance of the counts equals the mean. Real count data, such as disease counts driven by outbreaks or clustering, very often have a variance considerably larger than the mean, a problem called overdispersion.

Why it matters: fitting an ordinary Poisson model to overdispersed data still gives reasonable estimates of the coefficients themselves, but understates how uncertain those estimates are, making results look more precise and more statistically significant than they really are.
The fix: when overdispersion is present, a negative binomial regression, built on the negative binomial distribution introduced in Module 10, replaces the Poisson assumption with one that allows variance to exceed the mean, giving more honest uncertainty estimates while keeping the same log-link structure and rate-ratio interpretation.

Three Members of One Family

Linear, logistic, and Poisson regression share the same basic structure: a straight line in the predictors, connected to the outcome through a link function suited to that outcome's type.

ModelOutcome typeLink functionCoefficient becomes
Linear regressionContinuous (any value)Identity (none needed)A direct change in y
Logistic regressionBinary (0 or 1)Logit (log-odds)An odds ratio
Poisson regressionCount (0, 1, 2, ...)LogA rate ratio
One mental model, three outcomes: in every case, the model finds the best-fitting straight line on a transformed scale, and the choice of scale is dictated by what the outcome is physically allowed to be.

Limitations and Cautions

  • Both models still assume the chosen predictors are correctly specified; a relationship that is curved on the logit or log scale in some more complex way will be fit poorly
  • Both inherit the correlation-versus-causation caution from every other regression model in this course: a significant coefficient describes an association, not proof that x causes the outcome
  • Logistic regression's classification accuracy can be misleading under class imbalance, where one outcome is rare
  • Poisson regression's standard errors are understated when overdispersion is present and left uncorrected
  • Both require a reasonably large sample to produce stable coefficient estimates, more so than simple linear regression with a continuous outcome
  • Multiple predictors can be added to either model, in which case multicollinearity, the same complication from Module 6, can again make individual coefficients unstable
Takeaway: these models extend regression's reach to outcomes that linear regression cannot honestly handle, but they do not remove the need for the same careful habits: checking assumptions, being cautious about causal claims, and reporting uncertainty honestly.

Anchor Case: A District Dengue Outbreak

During a dengue outbreak, a district health office uses both models from this module. A logistic regression predicts the probability that an individual patient will require hospitalization, using the patient's age and the day of illness onset. A separate Poisson regression predicts the expected number of new dengue cases per week across the district, using weekly rainfall.

The logistic model gives officials a patient-level triage tool: a patient with a high predicted probability of hospitalization can be prioritized for closer monitoring. The Poisson model gives a district-level planning tool: a forecast of next week's expected caseload, used to allocate hospital beds and staff.
Why both are needed, and why neither is enough alone: the logistic model says nothing about how many new patients to expect in total, and the Poisson model says nothing about which specific patients are at higher risk. Combining them, while still remembering that both are associations estimated from past outbreaks and may not hold exactly in a new and different outbreak, gives a fuller and more honest picture than either model alone.

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: A Hospital Readmission Model

A hospital fits a logistic regression predicting whether a discharged patient will be readmitted within 30 days (1 = readmitted, 0 = not), using the patient's age and number of prior admissions. The model reports an odds ratio of 1.8 for prior admissions, and a classification accuracy of 91 percent. Only 9 percent of patients in the dataset are actually readmitted.

Is the 91 percent accuracy figure, by itself, good evidence that the model is clinically useful?

A 91 percent accuracy figure sounds impressive on its own, and it is easy to stop reading right there.
Because only 9 percent of patients are actually readmitted, a model that simply predicted "not readmitted" for every patient would already be about 91 percent accurate, while being completely useless for the hospital's actual goal of identifying at-risk patients. The odds ratio of 1.8 for prior admissions is informative on its own terms, but accuracy alone, under this much class imbalance, says almost nothing about whether the model actually catches the patients who go on to be readmitted.

Case 2: Reading an Odds Ratio in a News Report

A news article reports that a study found smokers have "2.5 times the odds" of developing a certain respiratory condition compared to non-smokers, based on a logistic regression. A reader concludes that smokers are "2.5 times more likely" to develop the condition.

Is restating "2.5 times the odds" as "2.5 times more likely" an accurate translation?

Odds and probability sound close enough in everyday language that swapping one phrase for the other feels harmless.
An odds ratio of 2.5 means the odds, probability divided by its complement, are multiplied by 2.5, not the probability itself. If the baseline probability of the condition among non-smokers is small, say 2 percent, an odds ratio of 2.5 corresponds to a probability among smokers of roughly 4.8 percent, which is close to, but not exactly, 2.5 times higher. If the baseline probability were larger, say 40 percent, the same odds ratio would correspond to a probability increase that is noticeably less than a 2.5-fold jump. The two phrases are only approximately interchangeable when the outcome is rare.

Case 3: Forecasting Call Center Volume

A telecom company fits a Poisson regression predicting the expected number of customer complaint calls per day during a billing cycle, using the number of billing errors flagged that day, x. The fitted model is ln(λ) = 1.2 + 0.15x, and a manager wants to use it to staff the call center for a day with 40 flagged billing errors, far more than the 0 to 10 range seen in the data used to fit the model.

What should the manager be cautious about before staffing based on this forecast?

A formula that exponentiates cleanly to a specific number of expected calls can feel precise and trustworthy, regardless of how far the input strays from the data that produced it.
A value of 40 flagged errors is far outside the observed range of 0 to 10, so this is extrapolation, the same risk that applied to linear regression in Module 6. Because the model is on the log scale, errors compound multiplicatively: a small misspecification near the edge of the data becomes a very large one when extrapolated to x = 40, since e raised to a large number grows extremely fast. The manager should treat any forecast this far outside the observed range as a rough guess at best, not a staffing guarantee.

Case 4: Overdispersion in Outbreak Data

A health office fits a Poisson regression for weekly dengue cases across upazilas. The mean weekly case count is 4.2, but the variance of weekly case counts is 31.6, more than seven times the mean. The office reports the rainfall coefficient as highly statistically significant.

Given the relationship between the mean and the variance here, should the office trust the reported statistical significance at face value?

A statistically significant coefficient from standard software output looks like a finished, trustworthy result, with no obvious sign that anything is wrong.
A variance more than seven times the mean is a clear sign of overdispersion, which violates the Poisson model's core assumption that variance equals the mean. Under overdispersion, a plain Poisson model understates the true uncertainty in its coefficients, which can make a result look statistically significant when a model that properly accounts for the extra variance, such as a negative binomial regression, would show much wider uncertainty around the same rainfall coefficient. The office should refit the model as a negative binomial regression before relying on the significance claim.
