  • Classify variables by type and explain why classification determines which analytical tools are appropriate
  • Differentiate a parameter from a statistic, and describe the inferential relationship between them
  • Evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of common sampling designs
  • Identify sources of sampling bias in real-world studies and news reports
Note: This module lays the conceptual foundation for everything that follows. The vocabulary introduced here, population, sample, parameter, statistic, and variable, will appear in every subsequent module.

What Is Statistics?

Every day, consequential decisions are made on the basis of incomplete information. A physician cannot test every drug on every patient before prescribing. A government cannot survey every citizen before drafting policy. A company cannot interview every customer before designing a product.

Statistics is the discipline that makes incomplete information rigorous. It provides a principled framework for collecting data, quantifying uncertainty, and drawing defensible conclusions from what we can observe about what we cannot fully observe.

Statistics operates at two levels. Descriptive statistics summarizes and displays what the data show. Inferential statistics uses sample data to draw conclusions about a larger population, always with an honest acknowledgment of uncertainty.

Example: Bangladesh's Directorate General of Health Services cannot measure the blood pressure of every adult in the country. Instead, it draws a carefully designed sample of several thousand adults and uses those measurements to estimate the national prevalence of hypertension, complete with a margin of error.

A Researcher's Problem

A public health researcher at a Bangladeshi university wants to estimate the average Body Mass Index (BMI) of university students nationwide. There are approximately four million enrolled students across more than 150 universities.

Measuring all four million is financially impossible, logistically impractical, and unnecessary, provided the sampling is done correctly. The researcher instead collects precise measurements from 2,000 carefully selected students. If the selection method is sound, those 2,000 observations can reliably represent the full four million.

Central promise of statistics: A well-chosen sample of manageable size can yield reliable conclusions about a population that is far too large to observe directly. The critical word is well-chosen.

Population and Sample

Population
The complete collection of all units relevant to a study. A population can be finite (all registered voters in Dhaka) or conceptually infinite (all possible readings from a measuring instrument). What defines the population is determined by the research question, not by convenience.
Sample
A subset of the population selected for actual observation or measurement. The quality of any statistical conclusion depends critically on whether the sample was chosen in a principled way that avoids systematic bias.
Back to the BMI study: The population is all approximately four million university students currently enrolled in Bangladesh. The sample is the 2,000 students whose BMI is actually measured. The researcher will use the sample to make statements about the population.

Why Sampling Works

The idea of inferring population characteristics from a subset can seem presumptuous. In practice, it rests on a straightforward principle: a representative sample shares the essential distributional properties of the population it was drawn from.

Analogy: When a physician orders a blood test, she draws a few millilitres from a vein. She does not drain the patient's entire blood supply. Those few millilitres, drawn correctly from the circulatory system, are chemically representative of what is circulating throughout the body. The key is that blood is well-mixed, so any small portion reflects the whole. Statistics requires us to create the equivalent of a "well-mixed" condition artificially, through careful sampling design.

The Critical Requirement: Representativeness

A sample is useful only if it accurately reflects the composition and variation of the full population. This is not guaranteed by having a large sample. A biased sample of 100,000 observations can yield worse conclusions than an unbiased sample of 1,000.

Common misconception: "The larger the sample, the more reliable the result." Sample size reduces random error, but it cannot correct for systematic bias introduced by a flawed selection process. A poll conducted exclusively among smartphone users will misrepresent a population where smartphone ownership is unequal across age or income groups, regardless of how many smartphone users respond.

Types of Variables

A variable is any characteristic that can take on different values across observations. Identifying a variable's type is not a formality. It determines which summary measures are meaningful and which statistical methods are valid.

In the BMI study: BMI (kg/m²) is continuous quantitative. Number of meals per day is discrete quantitative. A student's department is nominal qualitative. Year of study (Year 1, 2, 3, 4) is ordinal qualitative.

Parameters and Statistics

This distinction is fundamental to statistical inference and is worth understanding precisely.

Parameter
A numerical characteristic of the population. It is a fixed value, but it is typically unknown because we cannot observe the entire population. Denoted by Greek letters: the population mean is \(\mu\), the standard deviation is \(\sigma\).
Statistic
A numerical characteristic of the sample. It is calculated from observed data and used to estimate the corresponding parameter. Denoted by Roman letters: the sample mean is \(\bar{x}\), the sample standard deviation is \(s\).
In the BMI study: The true average BMI of all four million students is a parameter, \(\mu\). We will never know its exact value. The average BMI computed from the 2,000-student sample is a statistic, \(\bar{x}\). We use \(\bar{x}\) as our best available estimate of \(\mu\), and we will learn in later modules how to quantify the uncertainty in that estimate.

Methods of Data Collection

The choice of data collection method determines what kinds of conclusions are defensible. Three broad approaches are used in practice.

Sample Survey

A structured instrument, questionnaire or interview, is administered to a selected sample. Well-suited for measuring attitudes, self-reported behaviors, and demographic characteristics. Conclusions are limited by the honesty and recall accuracy of respondents.

Observational Study

The researcher observes and records outcomes without intervening or manipulating any variable. Useful when experimentation is infeasible or unethical, but limited in its ability to establish cause and effect because measured and unmeasured variables may be confounded.

Controlled Experiment

The researcher deliberately assigns treatments to subjects (ideally at random) and measures the resulting effect. Randomized controlled experiments are the only design capable of establishing a causal relationship between a treatment and an outcome.

Key distinction: Observational studies can identify associations; only controlled experiments can establish causation. This difference is not a technicality. It is why the observation that "people who carry lighters get more lung cancer" does not mean lighters cause cancer.
In the BMI study: A survey could ask students to self-report their weight and height. An observational study could examine university health records. A controlled experiment could randomize students into different meal-plan conditions and measure BMI change over a semester. Each approach yields a different type of conclusion.

Sampling Techniques

Probability sampling methods assign known, non-zero probabilities of selection to every unit in the population. This is what makes inference from a sample to a population mathematically justifiable.

When Sampling Goes Wrong

Bias in sampling is not merely an academic concern. It has led to consequential errors in public health research, political polling, and market surveys. Recognizing these failure modes is essential for any informed reader of quantitative evidence.

Convenience Sampling

Selecting whoever is easiest to reach. A study on mobile phone usage conducted in a university computer lab selects a sample that systematically overrepresents digitally engaged students. The results cannot be generalized to all students.

Self-Selection Bias

When participation is voluntary, those who opt in typically differ from those who do not. Online polls and call-in surveys are structurally self-selected. The people motivated to respond hold stronger opinions than the silent majority, skewing results in a predictable direction.

Non-Response Bias

When a significant fraction of a selected sample does not respond, and non-respondents differ systematically from respondents. A survey mailed to alumni will be disproportionately completed by those who feel positively about their institution.

A cautionary case: In the 1936 U.S. presidential election, Literary Digest mailed ten million ballots to people drawn from telephone directories and automobile registrations, and predicted that Alf Landon would defeat Franklin Roosevelt by a wide margin. Roosevelt won in a landslide. The sampling frame excluded low-income voters who were less likely to own telephones or cars and who voted heavily for Roosevelt. Large sample size did not compensate for a biased frame.

Module 1 Recap

Five principles to carry forward from this module:

  • 1Statistics provides a principled framework for drawing reliable conclusions from incomplete information. The goal is not certainty, but defensible inference.
  • 2The population is what you care about; the sample is what you observe. Always clarify both before interpreting a result.
  • 3A parameter describes the population (usually unknown). A statistic describes the sample (what you calculate). The entire enterprise of inference is about using the latter to estimate the former.
  • 4Variable type is not administrative bookkeeping. It determines which summary measures are meaningful and which statistical methods are valid.
  • 5Before trusting any quantitative claim, ask: Who was in the sample? How were they selected? Could non-participation or self-selection have distorted the result?

1.A hospital wants to estimate the average recovery time for all patients who underwent a particular surgery last year. The hospital's records show 3,400 such patients, but the research team analyzes a random selection of 200 records. The 3,400 patients represent the:

The population is the complete set of all units relevant to the research question: all 3,400 patients who had the surgery. The 200 records examined form the sample, which is the subset actually studied.

2.The true average height of all first-year students at a university is best described as:

A parameter is a numerical characteristic of the population. The true average height of all first-year students (the full population of interest) is a parameter, typically denoted \(\mu\). It is unknown unless every student is measured.

3.Which of the following is a continuous quantitative variable?

Body weight is continuous because it can take any value on a numerical scale within a range, including fractional values. Number of siblings is discrete (countable). Department is nominal qualitative. Year of study is ordinal qualitative.

4.A researcher divides all university students into four groups by region (Dhaka, Chittagong, Rajshahi, Khulna) and then randomly selects students from within each regional group. This is best described as:

Stratified sampling divides the population into homogeneous subgroups (strata) and draws random samples from each. Here, the four regions are the strata. This ensures all regions are represented, which simple random sampling might not guarantee.

5.A television channel invites viewers to text in their vote on whether a new policy should be implemented. 45,000 responses are received. Which of the following is the most serious concern about this data?

When participation is entirely voluntary, people with strong opinions are far more likely to respond than those who are indifferent. The 45,000 respondents are self-selected and do not represent the population. The large number makes this bias more misleading, not less.

6.A student's letter grade in a course (A, B, C, D, F) is best classified as:

Letter grades are qualitative because they are categories, not measurements. They are ordinal because the categories have a meaningful order (A is better than B, which is better than C). However, the differences between categories are not necessarily equal in magnitude, so they are not quantitative.

7.An average BMI of 22.4 calculated from the 2,000-student sample is an example of:

Any numerical summary computed from a sample is a statistic. The value 22.4 was calculated from the 2,000 observed measurements, not from the full population of four million students. It serves as an estimate of the unknown population parameter \(\mu\).

8.A researcher wants to study the health outcomes of a new drug. She randomly assigns 500 patients to receive the drug and 500 to receive a placebo, then measures outcomes after six months. This is:

The defining feature of a controlled experiment is that the researcher deliberately assigns treatments (drug vs. placebo) to participants, ideally at random. This is the only design that can establish a causal relationship between the treatment and the measured outcome.

9.A study on employee satisfaction is conducted by emailing a questionnaire to 800 randomly selected employees. Only 210 respond. The researchers publish results based on these 210 responses. The primary concern is:

Non-response bias arises when those who do not participate in a study differ in relevant ways from those who do. Employees who are dissatisfied may be more or less likely to respond than satisfied employees, making the 210 responses an unrepresentative subset of the 800 selected.

10.Which of the following statements about sample size is most accurate?

This is perhaps the most important practical lesson in sampling. Increasing sample size reduces random (chance) variation but does nothing to correct systematic bias. The Literary Digest poll of 1936 had over two million responses and still produced a badly wrong prediction, because the sampling frame was systematically biased.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: The Online Poll

A major Bangladeshi news portal runs an online poll for 24 hours asking: "Do you support the government's proposal to increase university tuition fees?" By the end of the day, 18,600 responses have been collected. The portal headlines its story: "Survey shows 81% of Bangladeshis oppose tuition fee hike."

What would you conclude from this poll? How much confidence should a policymaker place in this figure?

Most readers are impressed by the large sample size (18,600 responses) and take the 81% figure as broadly representative. They treat it as meaningful evidence of public opinion.
This is a self-selected, opt-in sample with no probability mechanism. The people who visited this particular news portal, saw the poll, and felt strongly enough to click a button are systematically different from the general population. People with strong opinions (in this case, those opposed to fee increases) are far more likely to participate than those who are indifferent or support the proposal. The 18,600 responses do not represent 18,600 independent, randomly selected opinions. A policymaker who treats this as evidence of national sentiment is making a category error: confusing a large self-selected response with a representative sample.

Case 2: Nine Out of Ten

An advertisement for a dietary supplement states: "9 out of 10 nutritionists recommend NutriBoost for daily energy support." No further information about the study is provided. The product is priced at 1,200 taka per month.

How much evidential weight should you give this claim? What information would you need before treating it as meaningful?

Many consumers interpret "9 out of 10 nutritionists" as evidence that a large, independent, representative survey of the professional community was conducted, and that the overwhelming majority endorsed the product.
Without knowing the sample size, sampling method, and who conducted the study, this statistic carries no evidential weight. "9 out of 10" could mean 9 out of 10 nutritionists employed by the company, or 9 out of 10 consultants paid to review the product, or literally 10 people at a focus group. The claim also says "recommend for daily energy support," not that the product is effective. A statistic without a disclosed methodology is not evidence; it is marketing. The questions to ask: How many nutritionists? How were they selected? Who funded the study? What exactly were they asked?

Case 3: The University Average

A university publishes its annual report with the headline: "Average graduate CGPA: 3.51." A faculty member cites this figure in a grant application, arguing that the university produces academically high-performing graduates. A rival faculty member dismisses it as "meaningless."

Who is more right? What would a statistically careful interpretation of this figure require?

Most readers accept the average as a straightforward, unambiguous summary of graduate performance, and use it directly to compare the university against others or against a benchmark.
Several questions must be answered before this figure can be meaningfully interpreted. First, is 3.51 the mean or the median? If a small number of students have very high CGPAs, the mean could be pulled upward, making it unrepresentative of the typical student. Second, does it cover all graduates or only those with complete records? Students who dropped out or whose records are incomplete may be excluded, introducing survivorship bias. Third, CGPA reflects the grading standards of that particular institution and its departments. A 3.51 at one university may represent different academic attainment than a 3.51 at another, making cross-institutional comparison invalid without context. The figure is not meaningless, but it requires several layers of contextual interpretation before it can support the claim made in the grant application.

Case 4: The Volunteer Health Study

A research team publishes a 5-year study: "Regular physical exercise reduces the risk of Type 2 diabetes by 38%." The study enrolled 4,000 volunteers recruited through advertisements placed at gyms, fitness centers, and university health clinics. All participants were between 20 and 40 years old.

Can this conclusion be generalized to the broader adult population? What limitations should be stated alongside this finding?

The 5-year duration, the large sample size, and the precise-sounding percentage (38%) create an impression of rigor. Most readers generalize the finding to all adults.
The sample was recruited from gyms and health clinics, meaning participants were almost certainly more health-conscious, more physically active at baseline, and more attentive to nutrition than the general adult population. This is a well-documented problem called "healthy volunteer bias." The finding may be entirely valid within this subpopulation, but it cannot be straightforwardly applied to sedentary adults, older populations, or those with pre-existing conditions, because those groups were systematically excluded by the recruitment method. Additionally, this appears to be an observational study (volunteers chose to exercise; they were not randomly assigned to do so), which means confounding factors, such as diet, socioeconomic status, and access to healthcare, may partly explain the association. The 38% figure describes an association, not a guaranteed causal effect applicable to all adults.
