Organizing and Visualizing Data

Module 2  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Construct and interpret a frequency distribution table including relative and cumulative frequencies
  • Select the appropriate chart type: bar, grouped bar, stacked bar, histogram, scatter plot, or box plot
  • Distinguish bar charts (categorical) from histograms (quantitative continuous) and explain why the difference matters
  • Read a scatter plot to assess direction, strength, and linearity of a bivariate relationship
  • Extract the five-number summary from a box plot and apply the 1.5 × IQR rule to identify potential outliers
  • Recognize chart design choices that distort or mislead readers
Continuing from Module 1: The BMI research team has 2,000 measurements in a spreadsheet column. This module turns that raw list into structures and pictures that reveal the data's actual shape.

Convention: Classes are left-closed, right-open: a BMI of exactly 18.0 belongs to [18.0, 21.0), not [15.0, 18.0), so each observation falls into exactly one class.

Bar Charts

A bar chart is used for qualitative (categorical) variables. Each bar represents one category; bar length is proportional to frequency or relative frequency. The bars are separated by gaps to signal that the categories are discrete: there are no intermediate values between, say, "Engineering" and "Business."

Number of students by academic department (n = 2,000)

Key rules for bar charts

  • The vertical axis must start at zero. Truncating it exaggerates differences between bars.
  • For nominal variables (no natural order), bars are often sorted from tallest to shortest to help the reader identify the most and least common categories at a glance.
  • Horizontal bar charts work better when category labels are long and would overlap on a vertical chart.
Do not use a bar chart for quantitative continuous data. Drawing a separate bar for each numerical value (BMI = 22, 23, 24 ...) implies the values are discrete categories. For continuous data, use a histogram, which is the subject of the next slide.

Histograms

A histogram is the correct display for a quantitative continuous variable. Each bar covers one class interval, and the bars touch each other because there are no gaps in the underlying number line. The height of each bar represents the frequency (or relative frequency) of observations in that interval.

BMI distribution among 2,000 university students

The histogram immediately reveals what the table could only hint at: the data is concentrated between BMI 21 and 30, with a gentle rightward tail. This right-skewed shape means most students have moderate BMI values, but a minority with notably high values stretch the distribution toward the right.

Grouped and Stacked Bar Charts

When a categorical variable interacts with a second categorical variable (a grouping factor), two variants of the bar chart become useful. Both charts below display exactly the same data: the number of students in each BMI category across four departments.

Grouped (side-by-side) bar chart
Stacked bar chart
Grouped bar chart
Places bars for each subgroup side by side. Best for comparing individual subgroup values across categories. The eye can judge bar heights directly from a shared baseline.
Stacked bar chart
Places subgroup bars on top of one another. Shows totals and compositional shares. The 100% variant scales all bars to the same height, emphasizing proportions rather than counts.
When to switch to 100% stacked: If department sizes differ substantially, the 100% view lets you compare proportional composition without the distraction of unequal group sizes dominating the visual.

Scatter Plots

A scatter plot displays the relationship between two quantitative variables simultaneously. Each observation becomes a single point: its horizontal position encodes one variable, its vertical position the other.

Hours of physical activity per week vs. BMI (sample of 120 students)

What to look for

  • Direction: Upward slope = positive association. Downward slope = negative association.
  • Strength: Tight clustering around an imaginary line = strong. Diffuse spread = weak.
  • Linearity: Is the trend approximately straight, or does it curve?
  • Outliers: Points far from the main cluster may warrant investigation.
Reading this chart: Students who report more weekly physical activity tend to have lower BMI values (moderate negative association, broadly linear). This is an observational association, not proof of causation. Active students may differ from sedentary ones in diet, sleep, and many other unmeasured ways.

Box Plots

A box plot (box-and-whisker plot) summarizes a distribution using five values: minimum, Q1, median, Q3, and maximum. It simultaneously reveals center, spread, skewness, and potential outliers.

Interquartile Range (IQR)
IQR = Q3 − Q1 = 28.5 − 21.5 = 7.0. Measures spread of the middle 50% of data. Resistant to outliers. The box width represents the IQR.
Outlier detection (1.5 × IQR rule)
Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5 × 7.0 = 39.0. Lower fence = Q1 − 1.5 × 7.0 = 11.0. The value 39.5 exceeds the upper fence, so it is flagged as an outlier (open circle beyond the whisker).

✏ Quick Check

In the box plot above, what percentage of students have a BMI between Q1 (21.5) and Q3 (28.5)?

Comparing Groups with Box Plots

Side-by-side box plots placed on a common axis are one of the most efficient ways to compare the distribution of a variable across several groups. A single glance reveals differences in center, spread, and skewness that would take several tables to communicate.

Box plots vs. histograms for comparison: A histogram gives the richest picture of one group's shape. Side-by-side box plots sacrifice some shape detail but enable efficient comparison across many groups simultaneously, answering "which department has the widest spread?" or "which has the highest median?" in a single view.

Choosing the Right Chart

Chart selection is not an aesthetic decision. Each chart type is designed to answer a specific kind of question. Using the wrong type produces displays that are at best uninformative and at worst misleading.

The most common error: Applying a bar chart to a quantitative continuous variable (drawing separate bars for BMI = 22, 23, 24 ...). The gaps between bars falsely imply that the values between them are impossible. A histogram with appropriate class intervals is the correct choice.

When Charts Mislead

A well-designed chart reveals truth; a poorly designed or deliberately manipulative one conceals it. Recognizing these techniques is an essential skill for any quantitatively literate reader.

Truncated Y-Axis

Starting the vertical axis at a non-zero value exaggerates differences between bars. A change from 44% to 46% appears to triple when the axis runs from 43% to 47% rather than 0% to 100%.

Inconsistent Class Widths in Histograms

If class widths are unequal, bar height must represent frequency density (frequency ÷ class width), not raw frequency. Using raw frequency with unequal widths makes wider classes appear artificially larger.

Overloaded Grouped Bar Charts

More than four subgroups per cluster produces bars too narrow to read and a legend too crowded to parse. The visual appears dense with information while transmitting very little.

Scatter Plots without Scales

A scatter plot whose axes lack a scale or begin at arbitrary points can make a weak relationship look strong, or a strong one look weak, purely through axis compression or expansion.

Practical check: Before accepting a chart's message, verify the axis origin and scale, confirm that the chart type suits the variable type, and ask whether the data range was selectively chosen to support a predetermined conclusion.

Module 2 Recap

Six principles to carry forward:

  • 1A frequency table compresses raw data into a structured summary. It is always the first step before drawing any chart.
  • 2Bar charts (categorical, gaps between bars) and histograms (continuous, touching bars) look similar but belong to different data types. Confusing them is a substantive error, not merely cosmetic.
  • 3Use a grouped bar chart to compare individual subgroup values; use a stacked bar chart (especially 100%) to compare proportional composition.
  • 4A scatter plot is the first tool for investigating a relationship between two quantitative variables. Always assess direction, strength, linearity, and outliers before computing any summary number.
  • 5A box plot condenses an entire distribution into five numbers, identifies outliers via the 1.5 × IQR rule, and enables efficient group comparison when placed side by side.
  • 6Truncated axes, wrong chart types, and selective data ranges are routinely used to mislead. Verification of these three features should precede any chart interpretation.

📝 Module Quiz

10 questions on frequency distributions and data visualization.

1.A researcher records the department of each of 500 students. The most appropriate chart is:

Department is a nominal qualitative variable. A bar chart is the correct choice. A histogram would falsely imply that continuous values exist between the named categories.

2.Why do the bars in a histogram touch each other, unlike in a bar chart?

Continuous variables can take any value within a range, so there are no forbidden values between classes. Touching bars reflect this continuity. Gaps in a bar chart signal that categories are discrete and separate.

3.You want to compare average exam scores for male and female students across three departments. The best chart is:

A grouped bar chart places bars for each gender side by side within each department, enabling direct height comparison within and across departments from a shared baseline. A stacked bar chart would make the non-baseline group hard to compare.

4.A 100% stacked bar chart shows the proportion of students in each BMI category across four departments. What does switching to 100% stacked remove from the display?

Scaling all bars to 100% height removes information about group size, since every department bar now looks equally tall regardless of how many students are in it. What remains is each department's proportional composition, which is exactly what the 100% stacked chart emphasizes.

5.A scatter plot of study hours (x) vs. CGPA (y) shows points clustering tightly around an upward-sloping line. This indicates:

Upward slope = positive direction (as x increases, y tends to increase). Tight clustering = strong relationship. Together: strong positive association. This does not imply causation; students who study more may also differ in other relevant ways.

6.In a box plot, the box spans from Q1 to Q3. What percentage of the data does the box represent?

Q1 is the 25th percentile and Q3 is the 75th percentile by definition. The interval between them therefore contains exactly the middle 50% of all observations in the dataset, regardless of the distribution's shape.

7.For a dataset with Q1 = 40 and Q3 = 60, which value would be flagged as an outlier by the 1.5 × IQR rule?

IQR = 60 − 40 = 20. Upper fence = 60 + 1.5 × 20 = 90. Lower fence = 40 − 30 = 10. Only 95 exceeds the upper fence of 90, so it is the only outlier among the choices.

8.A histogram of income has a long tail to the right. This shape is called:

Skewness is named after the direction of the tail. A right-skewed distribution has its bulk on the left and a long tail reaching toward higher values. Income is a classic example: most earners cluster at moderate levels, but a small number of high earners stretch the distribution far to the right.

9.A bar chart's vertical axis runs from 98 to 106, making the bar for 100 look much shorter than the bar for 106. This is an example of:

Starting the axis at 98 rather than 0 compresses the reference baseline so the visible variation fills the full chart height. A genuine 6-unit difference appears far larger than it is. This is among the most widely exploited techniques for making modest differences look dramatic.

10.You want to investigate whether taller students tend to have higher BMI. The most appropriate chart is:

Height and BMI are both quantitative continuous variables. A scatter plot is the correct tool for visualizing the relationship between two quantitative variables simultaneously. Each student becomes one point, and the pattern of points reveals the direction and strength of the association.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: The Dramatic Sales Chart

A company's annual report includes a bar chart titled "Unprecedented Revenue Growth." The vertical axis runs from 980 to 1,020 crore taka. The bars show 2021 = 990, 2022 = 998, 2023 = 1,005, 2024 = 1,012. The tallest bar appears to be roughly five times the height of the shortest.

What does the data actually show, and why does the visual impression diverge so dramatically from the arithmetic?

Most readers accept the visual impression of dramatic, accelerating growth. The title "Unprecedented" reinforces this, and the tall final bar seems to confirm it.
Revenue grew from 990 to 1,012 crore over four years: an increase of 22 crore on a base of 990, roughly 2.2% total growth (about 0.7% per year, barely above inflation). By starting the axis at 980 rather than 0, the tiny 22-crore variation is stretched across the full chart height. A bar representing 1,012 that should appear 1.022 times as tall as one for 990 instead appears roughly five times as tall. The word "unprecedented" is technically defensible (revenue did reach a new high) while the visual impression of dramatic growth is manufactured entirely by the axis choice.

Case 2: Scatter Plot and Causation

A health researcher publishes a scatter plot with "Hours of sleep per night" on the x-axis and "Reaction time (ms)" on the y-axis. The points show a moderate negative association. The caption reads: "More sleep causes faster reaction times."

Is the caption a valid interpretation of the scatter plot? What does the chart actually demonstrate?

Most readers accept the causal caption because the visual pattern is clear and the direction makes intuitive sense. If sleeping more is associated with faster reactions, it seems natural to say sleep "causes" the improvement.
A scatter plot demonstrates association, not causation. The chart tells us that in this dataset, individuals who reported more sleep tended to have faster reaction times. But participants were not randomly assigned to sleep conditions: people who sleep more may also exercise more, eat better, or experience lower stress. Any of those factors could independently improve reaction time. To establish that sleep itself causes faster reactions, a controlled experiment (randomizing participants into different sleep conditions) is required. The correct caption: "Sleep duration is negatively associated with reaction time in this sample."

Case 3: Comparing Departments with Box Plots

Side-by-side box plots of CGPA by department show: Engineering has a median of 3.20, a box spanning 3.00 to 3.45, and a long lower whisker reaching 2.10. Business has a median of 3.30, a box spanning 3.15 to 3.50, and short symmetric whiskers. A faculty member concludes: "Business students are academically stronger."

Evaluate this conclusion using what the box plots actually show.

Many readers focus only on the median comparison and accept the conclusion. Business's median (3.30) exceeds Engineering's (3.20), which seems to settle the question.
The median comparison is only one part of the story. Engineering's long lower whisker (reaching 2.10) indicates a notable group of lower-performing students pulling the median down, while the upper half of Engineering's distribution may overlap substantially with Business. Engineering's wider box (IQR = 0.45 vs. 0.35) reveals greater variability in academic outcomes. Most importantly, CGPAs across departments are not directly comparable: grading norms, course difficulty, and assessment structures differ systematically. A 3.20 in Engineering may reflect different academic attainment than a 3.30 in Business. The box plots reveal distributional differences but cannot support a simple "stronger" verdict without accounting for structural differences in how grades are awarded across programs.

Case 4: Grouped vs. Stacked — Same Data, Different Insight

A university health report presents two charts of the same BMI category data (Underweight, Normal, Overweight, Obese) across four departments. Chart A is a grouped bar chart. Chart B is a 100% stacked bar chart. A student claims: "These charts show different data because they look completely different."

Is the student correct? What does each chart uniquely reveal that the other cannot?

Many students treat visual difference as a signal of data difference. If two charts look completely different, they assume they must contain different information.
The student is wrong: both charts encode the same underlying numbers. What differs is the question each is designed to answer. The grouped bar chart makes it easy to compare the absolute count of, say, Overweight students across departments side by side. This is the right tool when you want to know: "Which department has the most students in the Obese category?" The 100% stacked bar chart removes absolute count and instead shows each department's proportional breakdown. This is the right tool when you want to know: "In which department does the Overweight category represent the largest share of students?" If a department has three times as many students as another, the grouped chart will visually overemphasize it even if its composition is identical. The 100% stacked chart eliminates this size effect. Choosing between them is a matter of which question matters most to the reader, not which chart contains more data.
