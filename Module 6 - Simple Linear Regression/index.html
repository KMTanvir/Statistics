Simple Linear Regression

Module 6  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Explain what a regression line is and how it differs from correlation
  • Write and interpret the regression equation, including the slope and intercept
  • Calculate the slope and intercept of a fitted line by hand using the least squares formulas
  • Use a fitted line to predict values, and recognize when a prediction is unsafe
  • Interpret R-squared as the proportion of variation explained by the line
  • State the underlying assumptions of the linear regression model, and recognize when they may be violated
  • Describe how multiple linear regression extends the model to several explanatory variables
The question this module answers: Module 5 told us how strongly two variables move together. This module goes one step further: given a value of one variable, what is our best single guess for the other? Regression turns a relationship into a usable prediction tool.

From Correlation to a Line

A scatter plot with a strong correlation suggests the points roughly follow a straight line. Regression finds the single straight line that fits the cloud of points best, called the line of best fit or the regression line.

Explanatory variable (x)
The variable used to make a prediction. Also called the independent or predictor variable. Plotted on the horizontal axis.
Response variable (y)
The variable being predicted. Also called the dependent or outcome variable. Plotted on the vertical axis.
Best fit means: the line is chosen to make the points, on average, as close to the line as possible. The standard method for this is called least squares, which minimizes the sum of squared vertical distances between the points and the line.

The Regression Equation

The fitted line is written as:

\[ \hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x \]

Here \(\hat{y}\) (read "y-hat") is the predicted value of y for a given x. The two numbers that define the line are the intercept and the slope.

Intercept (b₀)
The predicted value of y when x equals zero. It anchors the line on the vertical axis. Sometimes meaningful, sometimes not, depending on whether x = 0 makes sense in context.
Slope (b₁)
The predicted change in y for each one-unit increase in x. The sign of the slope matches the sign of the correlation between x and y.
Connection to Module 5: the slope and Pearson's r always carry the same sign. A positive r means an upward-sloping line; a negative r means a downward-sloping line. The next slide shows exactly how b₀ and b₁ are calculated.

Calculating the Line: The Least Squares Formulas

The slope and intercept are not guessed or drawn by eye. They are calculated directly from the data using the same building blocks introduced for Pearson's r in Module 5.

\[ b_1 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} \]
\[ b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1 \bar{x} \]

\(S_{xy}\) is the sum of the products of deviations, the same quantity used in the numerator of Pearson's r, and it measures how x and y move together. \(S_{xx}\) is the sum of squared deviations of x alone, measuring how spread out the x values are.

Why the slope formula works
The slope scales how strongly x and y move together by how much x itself varies. Dividing by Sxx instead of by the square root of Sxx times Syy, as in Pearson's r, is what converts a unitless correlation into a slope with real units of y per unit of x.
Why the intercept formula works
Once the slope is known, the intercept is chosen so that the line passes exactly through the point of averages, mean x paired with mean y, which keeps the line balanced against the data on both sides.
Good news: if Sxy, Sxx, and the two means have already been computed for Pearson's r, finding the regression line takes only one more division and one more subtraction. No new raw calculation is required.

Worked Example: Fitting the Line

Applying the formulas from the previous slide to the same six students from Module 5 (hours studied and exam score):

From Module 5: mean hours = 5.67, mean score = 68, Sxy = Σ(x−mean x)(y−mean y) = 185.0, Sxx = Σ(x−mean x)² = 33.33.
Slope: b₁ = Sxy ÷ Sxx = 185.0 ÷ 33.33 = 5.55
Intercept: b₀ = mean y − b₁(mean x) = 68 − (5.55 × 5.67) = 36.55
Fitted line: ŷ = 36.55 + 5.55x

In plain English: every extra hour of studying is associated with about 5.55 additional points on the exam, starting from a baseline of about 36.55 points for zero hours studied.

Using the Line to Predict

To predict a score, substitute an hours value into the fitted equation \( \hat{y} = 36.55 + 5.55x \).

A student who studies 7 hours: ŷ = 36.55 + 5.55(7) = 75.4, a safe prediction since 7 falls within the observed range of 2 to 9 hours.
Extrapolation danger: predicting for a student who studies 20 hours gives ŷ = 36.55 + 5.55(20) = 147.5, an impossible exam score since scores are capped at 100. The line was fitted only on data between 2 and 9 hours, and there is no guarantee the same straight-line pattern continues far outside that range.
Interpolation
Predicting for an x value inside the range of the original data. Generally reliable.
Extrapolation
Predicting for an x value outside the range of the original data. Risky, since the relationship may change shape beyond what was observed.

Reading Slope and Intercept in Plain English

Regression output is only useful if it can be translated into a sentence a non-statistician understands.

Slope template: "For each additional [one unit of x], [y] is predicted to change by [b₁] [units of y], on average."
Example: for each additional hour studied, exam score is predicted to rise by about 5.55 points, on average.
Intercept caution: the intercept of 36.55 is the predicted score for zero hours of studying. This may or may not be meaningful. In many real datasets, x = 0 is outside the observed range or does not occur in practice, so the intercept is best treated as a mathematical anchor for the line rather than a literal prediction.

R-Squared: How Well Does the Line Fit?

R-squared, written \(R^2\), is the square of Pearson's r. It is interpreted as the proportion of variation in y that is associated with, or explained by, x.

\[ R^2 = r^2 \]
In the worked example, r = 0.99, so R² = 0.99² = 0.98. About 98 percent of the variation in exam scores across these six students is associated with differences in hours studied; the remaining 2 percent is due to other factors not captured by the line.
R-squaredInterpretation
Close to 0The line explains almost none of the variation in y
Around 0.3 to 0.5The line explains a modest share; many other factors matter
Around 0.6 to 0.8The line explains a substantial share of the variation
Above 0.9The line explains nearly all of the variation, as in the worked example
R-squared is not a grade: a low R² does not automatically mean a model is useless, and a high R² does not automatically mean the model is correctly specified or that x causes y.

Residuals: The Gap Between Actual and Predicted

For any observed point, the residual is the difference between the actual y and the predicted ŷ:

\[ \text{residual} = y - \hat{y} \]
Student 3 studied 5 hours and scored 65. The line predicts ŷ = 36.55 + 5.55(5) = 64.3. The residual is 65 − 64.3 = 0.7, meaning this student scored slightly above what the line predicted.

The least squares method chooses b₀ and b₁ specifically to make the sum of squared residuals as small as possible across all the data points. Large residuals for a particular point indicate that point is poorly described by the line.

Assumptions Behind the Linear Regression Model

The least squares line can always be calculated, but treating it as a trustworthy description of the relationship, and using it for prediction or formal inference, relies on four assumptions about the residuals. These are often remembered by the acronym LINE.

L — Linearity
The true relationship between x and y is actually a straight line. If the real pattern is curved, as with the fertilizer-and-yield example from Module 5, a straight line will systematically misfit parts of the data.
I — Independence
The residuals for different observations are not related to each other. This is commonly violated when data are collected over time or from clustered groups, where one period's or one group's error tends to carry over into the next.
N — Normality of residuals
The residuals are approximately normally distributed. This matters mainly for constructing confidence intervals and hypothesis tests around the slope, topics covered later in this course, rather than for the point prediction itself.
E — Equal variance (homoscedasticity)
The spread of the residuals stays roughly constant across all values of x. If the residuals fan out wider as x increases, a pattern called heteroscedasticity, the line is less reliable at some x values than others.
Practical check: a plot of residuals against x is the simplest diagnostic. A random scatter with no pattern and roughly constant spread supports the assumptions; a curve, a funnel shape, or a trend in the residuals signals a violation.

Beyond One Predictor: Multiple Linear Regression

Simple linear regression uses one explanatory variable. Multiple linear regression extends the same idea to several explanatory variables at once:

\[ \hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x_1 + b_2 x_2 + \dots + b_k x_k \]

Each coefficient \(b_j\) is now a partial slope: the predicted change in y for a one-unit increase in \(x_j\), holding all the other explanatory variables in the model constant.

Example: an agricultural office models rice yield (tons per hectare) using both monsoon rainfall (x₁, millimeters) and fertilizer amount (x₂, kilograms per hectare): ŷ = 0.9 + 0.003x₁ + 0.012x₂. Holding fertilizer fixed, each additional millimeter of rainfall is associated with 0.003 more tons per hectare; holding rainfall fixed, each additional kilogram of fertilizer is associated with 0.012 more tons per hectare.
Why add more predictors
A single predictor often leaves out other factors that matter, and ignoring them can make the one slope you do estimate misleading, since it may partly be standing in for an omitted variable.
Multicollinearity
A complication that arises when two or more explanatory variables are themselves strongly correlated, making it statistically difficult to separate their individual effects on y.
R-squared with multiple predictors: R² still measures the proportion of variation in y explained by the model, now using all predictors together. Adding more variables can only raise or hold R² steady, never lower it, which is why a related measure called adjusted R-squared is used to penalize adding predictors that do not genuinely improve the model.

Limitations of Linear Regression

  • It assumes the relationship is linear; a strongly curved relationship will be fit poorly by a straight line, just as with Pearson's r
  • It is sensitive to outliers, which can pull the entire line toward themselves
  • The LINE assumptions (linearity, independence, normality, equal variance) may not hold, which weakens prediction and invalidates formal inference
  • Extrapolating far beyond the observed range of x is unreliable
  • In multiple regression, correlated predictors create multicollinearity, which can make individual coefficients unstable and hard to interpret
  • A good fit (high R²) still does not prove that x causes y; the same correlation-versus-causation cautions from Module 5 apply directly to regression
Takeaway: regression is a powerful description and prediction tool, but every one of these limitations is a reason to inspect the data and the residuals, not just the final equation, before trusting the model.

Anchor Case: Rainfall and Rice Yield

An agricultural office fits a regression line predicting rice yield (tons per hectare) from monsoon rainfall (millimeters) using ten years of district data, and finds ŷ = 1.2 + 0.004x with R² = 0.71.

Plain-English reading: each additional millimeter of monsoon rainfall is associated with a predicted increase of 0.004 tons per hectare in rice yield, on average, and rainfall accounts for about 71 percent of the year-to-year variation in yield in this district.
Why caution is still needed: the line was fit on rainfall between roughly 1,200 and 2,400 millimeters, the range actually observed over the ten years. Using it to predict yield under a drought of 300 millimeters, or a flood year of 4,000 millimeters, would be extrapolation far outside that range, and the relationship plausibly changes shape at both extremes since too little or too much water both damage yield. The remaining 29 percent of unexplained variation is a reminder that fertilizer, seed variety, and pest pressure also matter, which is exactly the gap that multiple regression is built to close.

Module Recap

  • 1The regression line predicts y from x using \( \hat{y} = b_0 + b_1 x \), with b₁ = Sxy/Sxx and b₀ = mean y − b₁(mean x), fit by least squares
  • 2The slope is the predicted change in y per one-unit increase in x; the intercept anchors the line at x = 0
  • 3Predictions are reliable within the observed range of x (interpolation) and risky outside it (extrapolation)
  • 4R-squared, the square of r, gives the proportion of variation in y associated with x
  • 5Trusting the line for prediction and inference relies on the LINE assumptions: linearity, independence, normality, and equal variance of the residuals
  • 6Multiple linear regression extends the equation to several predictors at once, with each partial slope holding the other predictors constant
  • 7A good fit does not establish causation; regression inherits every caution from correlation

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: Predicting Remittances from Years Abroad

A microfinance office fits a regression line predicting a migrant worker's monthly remittance (in thousand taka) from years spent working abroad, using data from workers who have been abroad between 1 and 12 years. The fitted line is ŷ = 8 + 1.5x.

A loan officer wants to use this line to predict the remittance of a worker who has been abroad for 30 years. Is this a sound use of the model?

Once a formula exists, it is tempting to plug in any number, since the math itself does not stop you from doing so.
Thirty years is far outside the observed range of 1 to 12 years, so this is extrapolation. Remittance patterns plausibly change shape over a career, for instance flattening or declining as a worker nears retirement, a pattern the line was never given a chance to learn. The prediction of 8 + 1.5(30) = 53 thousand taka should not be trusted.

Case 2: School Spending and Test Scores

A regression of average district test scores on per-student school spending finds R² = 0.42, with a positive slope. A newspaper headline reads: "Study proves more spending raises test scores."

Does an R-squared of 0.42 with a positive slope support this headline's causal claim?

A positive slope and a moderate R-squared can look like solid statistical backing for a causal headline, especially when both numbers point the same direction as the claim.
An R-squared of 0.42 means spending is associated with 42 percent of the variation in scores, leaving 58 percent due to other factors, and association is not causation. Wealthier districts likely spend more per student and also have other advantages, such as more educated parents or better nutrition, that independently raise test scores. Spending could matter, but this single-predictor regression alone cannot isolate its effect from these confounding factors; a multiple regression that also controls for household income would be a more honest next step.

Case 3: Forecasting Sales for a New Product Line

A retailer fits ŷ = 50 + 2.3x, predicting weekly sales units (y) from the number of store displays (x), using data from stores with 1 to 8 displays. Management proposes installing 25 displays in their flagship store, expecting ŷ = 50 + 2.3(25) = 107.5 units per week.

What should management be cautious about before acting on this forecast?

A forecast that comes out of a clean formula can feel more certain than it actually is, simply because it is a precise-looking number.
Twenty-five displays is well beyond the observed range of 1 to 8, so this is extrapolation. In reality, the relationship likely flattens at some point, since shelf and floor space are limited and additional displays beyond a certain number bring diminishing or even negative returns by cluttering the store. The forecast of 107.5 units should be treated as unreliable, and a small pilot at a more moderate number of displays would be a safer next step.

Case 4: A High R-Squared with a Hidden Outlier

A regression of district flood damage on rainfall across 15 districts reports R² = 0.88, suggesting rainfall strongly predicts damage. A closer look shows that one coastal district had both extreme rainfall and catastrophic damage that year, while the other 14 districts show only a weak relationship among themselves.

Should the reported R² = 0.88 be trusted as describing the typical district?

A single strong summary number like R² = 0.88 looks objective and final, which makes it easy to overlook that one extreme point can be doing most of the work behind it.
A single extreme outlier can inflate R² well above what the bulk of the data actually supports, since both the line and its fit statistics are sensitive to extreme points. A scatter plot would reveal that 14 of the 15 districts show little relationship between rainfall and damage, and the headline R-squared mostly reflects the one coastal district. Reporting the fit with and without that district would give a far more honest picture.

Case 5: Adding a Second Predictor to the Yield Model

An extension office's simple regression of rice yield on rainfall alone gives R² = 0.71. After also recording fertilizer amount for each plot, they fit a multiple regression: ŷ = 0.9 + 0.003(rainfall) + 0.012(fertilizer), and R² rises to 0.89.

Why did R-squared rise, and is it now safe to say the rainfall coefficient by itself measures rainfall's true effect on yield?

It is tempting to read a jump in R-squared as proof that the new variable's coefficient is now a clean, isolated measure of cause and effect.
R-squared rose because fertilizer amount explains additional variation in yield that rainfall alone could not capture, which is expected whenever a relevant predictor is added. The rainfall coefficient of 0.003 is now a partial slope, the effect of rainfall holding fertilizer fixed, which is a more honest estimate than the simple regression's slope, since it no longer absorbs fertilizer's separate influence. Even so, both coefficients could still be biased by other omitted factors, such as soil quality or pest pressure, and a high R² still does not by itself prove causation.
