Binomial Distribution

Module 9  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Recognize a Bernoulli trial and identify whether a process meets the conditions of a binomial experiment
  • Calculate a binomial probability using the binomial formula and combinations
  • Calculate cumulative binomial probabilities, including "at least" and "at most" questions
  • Calculate and interpret the mean and variance of a binomial distribution
  • Describe how the shape of a binomial distribution changes with n and p
  • Recognize situations where the binomial conditions are violated and the model should not be used
Continuing from Module 8: elementary probability gave the rules for combining probabilities of individual events. The binomial distribution applies those same rules, especially the multiplication rule for independent events, to a very common situation: repeating the same yes/no experiment a fixed number of times and counting how many times "yes" occurs.

The Bernoulli Trial

A Bernoulli trial is a single random experiment with exactly two possible outcomes, conventionally labeled "success" and "failure." Success does not need to mean something good, it simply means the outcome being counted.

\[ P(\text{success}) = p, \qquad P(\text{failure}) = 1 - p = q \]
Examples of success/failure
A mobile banking transaction completes or fails. A loan is repaid on time or defaults. A vaccinated patient does or does not develop a side effect. A manufactured item passes or fails inspection.
p stays constant
In a Bernoulli trial, p is the fixed probability of success for that one trial, determined by the nature of the process being studied, such as a known historical default rate.
Why this matters: the binomial distribution is nothing more than the sum of several independent, identical Bernoulli trials, counting how many resulted in success.

Conditions for a Binomial Experiment

Not every repeated yes/no process is binomial. Four conditions, often remembered with the mnemonic BINS, must all hold:

B — Binary outcomes
Each trial results in exactly one of two outcomes: success or failure.
I — Independent trials
The outcome of one trial does not affect the probability of success on any other trial.
N — Number of trials fixed
The number of trials, n, is set in advance, not determined by when a success first occurs.
S — Same probability of success
The probability of success, p, is identical on every trial.
A frequent violation: drawing several items without replacement from a small population (such as 4 defective items among 20 in a box, sampled 5 at a time) changes the composition of the remaining pool after each draw. This makes the trials dependent and the probability of success shift from draw to draw, so the binomial model does not strictly apply. With a very large population relative to the sample, this effect becomes small enough to ignore in practice.

The Binomial Probability Formula

For a binomial experiment with n independent trials, each with success probability p, the probability of observing exactly x successes is:

\[ P(X = x) = \binom{n}{x} p^{x} (1-p)^{n-x}, \qquad x = 0, 1, 2, \dots, n \]

The term \(\binom{n}{x}\), read "n choose x," counts how many different sequences of n trials contain exactly x successes:

\[ \binom{n}{x} = \frac{n!}{x!\,(n-x)!} \]
Why all three pieces are needed: \(p^x\) accounts for the probability of the x successes happening; \((1-p)^{n-x}\) accounts for the probability of the remaining failures; \(\binom{n}{x}\) accounts for the number of different orders in which those x successes could occur among the n trials.

Worked Example: Exactly x Successes

A microfinance institution's records show that 8% of newly disbursed loans default within the first year (p = 0.08). Among a newly disbursed batch of 10 loans (n = 10), what is the probability that exactly 2 default?

\[ P(X = 2) = \binom{10}{2} (0.08)^2 (0.92)^8 \]
ComponentValue
\(\binom{10}{2} = \frac{10!}{2!\,8!}\)45
\((0.08)^2\)0.0064
\((0.92)^8\)0.5132
P(X = 2) = 45 × 0.0064 × 0.5132 ≈ 0.1478, about a 14.8% chance that exactly 2 of the 10 loans default.
This loan example is used throughout this module. Keep n = 10 and p = 0.08 in mind; later slides build on it to compute cumulative probabilities, the mean, and the variance.

Cumulative Binomial Probabilities

Many real questions ask about a range of outcomes rather than one exact value: "at least," "at most," or "more than." These are answered by summing the relevant individual probabilities.

\[ P(X \le k) = \sum_{x=0}^{k} P(X = x), \qquad P(X \ge k) = 1 - P(X \le k-1) \]
"At most 1 default": P(X ≤ 1) = P(X=0) + P(X=1) = 0.4344 + 0.3777 = 0.8121.

"At least 1 default": P(X ≥ 1) = 1 − P(X=0) = 1 − 0.4344 = 0.5656.

"More than 2 defaults": P(X > 2) = 1 − P(X ≤ 2) = 1 − (0.4344+0.3777+0.1478) = 1 − 0.9599 = 0.0401.
The complement trick from Module 8 returns here: "at least 1" is almost always easiest to compute as 1 minus "exactly 0," since there is only one way to have zero successes but potentially many ways to have one or more.

Mean and Variance of the Binomial Distribution

Unlike most distributions, the binomial mean and variance have remarkably simple formulas, requiring no summation over the full distribution:

\[ \mu = E(X) = np, \qquad \sigma^2 = Var(X) = np(1-p), \qquad \sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)} \]
Loan example (n = 10, p = 0.08):
μ = 10 × 0.08 = 0.8 expected defaults per batch of 10 loans.
σ² = 10 × 0.08 × 0.92 = 0.736.
σ = √0.736 ≈ 0.858 defaults.
Interpreting μ = 0.8: this does not mean any single batch will have exactly 0.8 defaults, which is impossible. It means that if many such batches of 10 loans were disbursed, the long-run average number of defaults per batch would be 0.8, consistent with the full distribution in Slide 6 where 0 or 1 default dominates.

How the Shape Changes with p and n

The shape of a binomial distribution depends entirely on p and n.

p < 0.5
Right-skewed: low values of x are most likely, with a long tail toward larger x. This describes the loan default example, where p = 0.08.
p = 0.5
Perfectly symmetric, regardless of n. A fair coin flipped n times is the classic example.
p > 0.5
Left-skewed: high values of x are most likely, with a long tail toward smaller x.
A connection to look ahead to: as n grows large, regardless of p (as long as np and n(1−p) are both reasonably large, a common rule of thumb being at least 5 each), the binomial distribution becomes closely approximated by the normal distribution covered in Module 12. This is one of the most widely used approximations in applied statistics.

Worked Example: Quality Control

A ceramics workshop's records show that 5% of fired pieces come out of the kiln with a visible defect (p = 0.05). A buyer randomly inspects 20 pieces from a shipment (n = 20). What is the probability that at most 1 piece is defective?

\[ P(X \le 1) = P(X=0) + P(X=1) = \binom{20}{0}(0.05)^0(0.95)^{20} + \binom{20}{1}(0.05)^1(0.95)^{19} \]
TermValue
P(X = 0) = (0.95)200.3585
P(X = 1) = 20 × 0.05 × (0.95)190.3774
P(X ≤ 1) = 0.3585 + 0.3774 = 0.7359, about a 73.6% chance the buyer's sample contains at most one defective piece, and therefore about a 26.4% chance of finding two or more.

Mean defectives per inspected batch: μ = np = 20 × 0.05 = 1.0. Variance: σ² = 20 × 0.05 × 0.95 = 0.95.

When the Binomial Model Does Not Apply

Applying the binomial formula when its conditions are not met produces a number that looks precise but answers the wrong question.

Trials are not independent
Example: sampling 5 fish for a disease test from a pond of only 30 fish, without replacement. Each draw changes the remaining population's disease rate, violating independence.
p is not constant
Example: the probability a customer complaint is resolved on first contact may depend on time of day or agent experience, so a single p does not describe every trial.
n is not fixed in advance
Example: continuing to test patients "until 3 positive cases are found" fixes the number of successes, not the number of trials. This is the negative binomial setting (Module 10), not the binomial.
More than two outcomes
Example: classifying a manufactured part as passing, needing minor rework, or being scrapped has three categories, not two, and requires a multinomial model instead.
The practical fix for small populations: when sampling without replacement from a population that is large relative to the sample (commonly, sample is less than 5–10% of the population), the dependence between draws becomes negligible and the binomial model remains a reasonable approximation.

Anchor Case: Vaccine Side Effect Monitoring

A health directorate monitors a vaccine known from clinical trials to cause a mild fever in 12% of recipients (p = 0.12). At a rural vaccination camp, 15 people are vaccinated on a given day (n = 15), and recipients respond independently of one another.

Question 1: probability that exactly 2 people develop a fever.
P(X=2) = C(15,2)(0.12)2(0.88)13 = 105 × 0.0144 × 0.1845 ≈ 0.279.
Question 2: probability that at least 3 people develop a fever, which would trigger an internal review under the directorate's protocol.
P(X=0) = (0.88)15 ≈ 0.1470. P(X=1) = 15(0.12)(0.88)14 ≈ 0.3006. P(X=2) ≈ 0.279 (above).
P(X ≤ 2) ≈ 0.1470+0.3006+0.279 = 0.7266. P(X ≥ 3) = 1 − 0.7266 = 0.2734, roughly a 27% chance the review threshold is triggered on any given camp day, purely from expected variability, not necessarily a sign that anything is wrong with this particular batch of vaccine.
Why this matters for policy: setting a review threshold without understanding the binomial variability built into small samples risks two costly errors: triggering unnecessary investigations into perfectly normal vaccine batches, or, conversely, setting the threshold so high that a genuinely elevated side-effect rate goes unnoticed.

Module 9 Recap

  • 1A Bernoulli trial has exactly two outcomes, success with probability p and failure with probability 1−p.
  • 2A binomial experiment requires fixed n, independent trials, constant p, and binary outcomes on every trial (BINS).
  • 3The binomial formula, P(X=x) = C(n,x) p^x (1−p)^(n−x), gives the probability of exactly x successes in n trials.
  • 4Cumulative questions ("at least," "at most") are answered by summing individual probabilities, often most easily via the complement.
  • 5The mean and variance have simple closed forms: μ = np and σ² = np(1−p), requiring no summation.
  • 6The distribution is right-skewed when p < 0.5, symmetric when p = 0.5, and left-skewed when p > 0.5.
  • 7Violating independence, a constant p, or a fixed n produces a number that looks like a valid probability but answers the wrong question.
Next module: Module 10 turns to the negative binomial distribution, which answers a closely related but different question: not how many successes occur in a fixed number of trials, but how many trials are needed to reach a fixed number of successes.

The binomial mean is simply the number of trials times the probability of success: μ = np.

7.The variance of a binomial distribution is calculated as:

The binomial variance is σ² = np(1-p), combining the number of trials with both the success and failure probabilities.

When p = 0.5, success and failure are equally likely, making the binomial distribution perfectly symmetric regardless of n.

When success is rare, low values of x dominate and the distribution has a long tail toward higher x, making it right-skewed.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: Overbooking a Flight

An airline knows from years of data that 6% of passengers who book a domestic flight fail to show up (p = 0.06), and no-shows are effectively independent of one another. The airline sells 100 tickets for a 95-seat flight, reasoning that the expected number of no-shows, μ = 100 × 0.06 = 6, comfortably covers the 5-seat overbooking margin.

Is relying on the mean of 6 no-shows alone a safe basis for this overbooking decision?

Since the expected number of no-shows (6) exceeds the overbooking margin (5), it is tempting to conclude the airline is safely covered on average.
The mean describes the long-run average, not the outcome of any single flight. With n=100 and p=0.06, σ = √(100×0.06×0.94) ≈ 2.37, so the actual number of no-shows varies substantially flight to flight. There is a real, calculable probability that fewer than 5 passengers fail to show, which would mean more passengers arrive than there are seats, resulting in involuntary bumping. A responsible overbooking policy should calculate P(no-shows < 5) explicitly from the binomial distribution, not rely on the mean alone, and weigh that probability against the cost of bumping a passenger.

Case 2: A Misapplied "Binomial" Survey

A market research firm surveys 200 randomly selected households in a city of 5 million about whether they have heard of a new mobile banking app, sampling without replacement. An analyst calculates a binomial probability for the number of households that have heard of the app, using p = 0.30 from prior market data.

Is treating this as a binomial experiment a reasonable approximation, or a serious error?

Sampling without replacement technically violates strict independence, leading some to reflexively reject the binomial model here as invalid.
This is a reasonable approximation, not an error. The sample (200) is a tiny fraction (0.004%) of the population (5 million), so removing surveyed households from the population has a negligible effect on the probability of the next household having heard of the app. The common rule of thumb, that the sample should be less than 5 to 10% of the population, is comfortably satisfied here. This is the opposite situation to the pond-of-30-fish example: when the population is large relative to the sample, the binomial model remains a very good approximation despite sampling without replacement.

Case 3: Setting a Defect-Rate Alarm

A factory production manager sets a rule: "if more than 3 defective items appear in a daily sample of 25, stop the line and inspect the machinery." Historical data shows the normal defect rate is 8% (p = 0.08) under healthy operating conditions.

Before adopting this rule, what should the manager calculate, and why?

"More than 3 out of 25" sounds intuitively like a meaningfully elevated defect rate (more than 12%), so the threshold can feel self-evidently reasonable without further calculation.
The manager should calculate P(X > 3) under the normal binomial model with n=25, p=0.08, before adopting the rule. With μ = np = 2.0, getting more than 3 defects by ordinary random variation alone is not as rare as intuition suggests; the binomial distribution has a calculable, often non-trivial, probability of producing 4 or more defects purely by chance even when the process is perfectly healthy. Without this calculation, the factory risks frequent unnecessary line stoppages (a high false-alarm rate) or, if the threshold is set too loosely, missing genuine equipment problems. The threshold should be chosen based on an acceptable false-alarm probability, not intuition about what "sounds like too many."

Case 4: Waiting for the Third Success

A clinical trial coordinator recruits patients one at a time and plans to "keep enrolling patients until exactly 5 patients respond positively to the treatment," then stop enrollment. An assistant proposes using the binomial formula to calculate the probability distribution of the number of patients enrolled.

Is the binomial distribution the correct tool for this question?

Since each patient's response is still a binary success/failure outcome with a roughly constant probability, it is easy to assume the binomial formula automatically applies to any question involving such outcomes.
No, this is not a binomial question. In the binomial setting, the number of trials (n) is fixed in advance and the number of successes is what varies randomly. Here, the situation is reversed: the number of successes is fixed at 5, and the number of trials (patients enrolled) is what varies randomly and is unknown in advance. This is exactly the setting for the negative binomial distribution, covered in Module 10, which answers "how many trials are needed to reach a fixed number of successes," not the binomial question of "how many successes occur in a fixed number of trials."
