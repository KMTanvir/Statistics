Hypothesis Testing

Module 16  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • State the null and alternative hypotheses for a given research question
  • Distinguish one-tailed and two-tailed tests
  • Calculate a test statistic and interpret a p-value correctly
  • Apply a significance level to reach a decision about the null hypothesis
  • Explain Type I and Type II errors, and the concept of statistical power
  • Conduct z-tests and t-tests for means and proportions, and connect the results to confidence intervals
From estimation to decision: Module 15 asked "what is the plausible range for this parameter?" This module asks a sharper question: "does the evidence support or contradict a specific claim about that parameter?" Both rely on the same sampling distribution machinery from Module 14.

The Logic of Hypothesis Testing: A Courtroom Analogy

Hypothesis testing follows the same logical structure as a criminal trial. A defendant is presumed innocent until evidence proves guilt beyond reasonable doubt; the court never directly proves innocence.

Presumption (null hypothesis)
"Innocent" or "no effect" is assumed true unless the evidence strongly contradicts it.
Claim being tested (alternative hypothesis)
"Guilty" or "there is an effect," the claim that requires convincing evidence to accept.
Just as a "not guilty" verdict does not prove innocence, only that guilt was not proven, failing to reject a null hypothesis does not prove the null hypothesis is true. It only means the evidence was not strong enough to reject it.

Null and Alternative Hypotheses

Null hypothesis (H0)
A statement of no effect, no difference, or no change, treated as the default assumption to be tested against the data.
Alternative hypothesis (H1 or Ha)
The claim of interest, stating that there is an effect, a difference, or a change.
Example: a fertilizer company claims its new product increases average rice yield above the historical average of μ=4.2 tons per hectare.
H0: μ = 4.2      H1: μ > 4.2
The null hypothesis always contains the equality; the alternative hypothesis expresses the direction or existence of a difference that the researcher suspects or hopes to demonstrate.

One-Tailed and Two-Tailed Tests

One-tailed test
The alternative hypothesis specifies a direction: H1: μ > 4.2 or H1: μ < 4.2. Evidence in only one direction can lead to rejecting H0.
Two-tailed test
The alternative hypothesis states only that a difference exists, in either direction: H1: μ ≠ 4.2. Evidence far enough in either direction can lead to rejecting H0.
Choose the direction before seeing the data: the choice between one-tailed and two-tailed must be based on the research question itself, decided in advance. Choosing the direction after looking at the sample result, to make a result appear significant, undermines the validity of the test.

The Test Statistic

A test statistic measures how far the sample result is from what the null hypothesis predicts, expressed in standard error units. It plays the same standardizing role as the z-score in Module 12, applied here to a hypothesis rather than a single value.

\[ z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \]

Here μ0 is the value specified by the null hypothesis. A test statistic far from zero (in either direction, or in the direction specified by a one-tailed test) suggests the sample result is unlikely under H0.

Worked Example: A One-Tailed z-Test

Returning to the fertilizer example: H0: μ=4.2, H1: μ>4.2. A trial on n=49 plots using the new fertilizer produces a sample mean yield of x̄=4.35 tons/hectare, with known σ=0.7 tons/hectare.

\[ z = \frac{4.35 - 4.2}{0.7/\sqrt{49}} = \frac{0.15}{0.1} = 1.5 \]
The sample mean yield is 1.5 standard errors above the value claimed under H0. Whether this is convincing evidence depends on the p-value, introduced next.

The P-Value

The p-value is the probability of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true.

Continuing the fertilizer example (one-tailed, H1: μ>4.2):
p-value = P(Z ≥ 1.5) = 1 - Φ(1.5) = 1 - 0.9332 = 0.0668.
What this number means: if the true average yield really were 4.2 tons/hectare (H0 true), a sample mean as high as 4.35 or higher would occur about 6.7% of the time purely from random sampling variability. A small p-value means the observed result would be unusual if H0 were true, casting doubt on H0.

The Significance Level and Decision Rule

Before collecting data, a significance level α is chosen, the threshold p-value below which the null hypothesis will be rejected. Common choices are α=0.05 or α=0.01.

\[ \text{If } p\text{-value} \leq \alpha\text{, reject } H_0\text{.} \qquad \text{If } p\text{-value} > \alpha\text{, fail to reject } H_0\text{.} \]
Fertilizer example, with α=0.05: the p-value of 0.0668 exceeds 0.05, so the decision is to fail to reject H0. The trial does not provide strong enough evidence, at the 5% significance level, to conclude the new fertilizer increases average yield.
"Fail to reject H0" is not the same as "H0 is proven true." It means the evidence was not strong enough to overturn the default assumption, echoing the courtroom analogy from Slide 2.

Worked Example: A Two-Tailed Test

A quality control process expects bags of rice to average μ=25 kg. A factory manager suspects the filling machine may be miscalibrated in either direction: H0: μ=25, H1: μ≠25. A sample of n=36 bags gives x̄=24.7 kg, σ=0.9 kg.

\[ z = \frac{24.7-25}{0.9/\sqrt{36}} = \frac{-0.3}{0.15} = -2.0 \]
For a two-tailed test, the p-value doubles the one-tailed area: p-value = 2 × P(Z ≤ -2.0) = 2(0.0228) = 0.0456.
With α=0.05, since 0.0456 ≤ 0.05, the decision is to reject H0: there is statistically significant evidence the filling machine's average is not 25 kg.

The t-Test: When σ Is Unknown

Exactly as in Module 15's confidence intervals, when σ is unknown and estimated by the sample standard deviation s, the test statistic follows a t-distribution with n-1 degrees of freedom instead of the standard normal distribution.

\[ t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} \]
Worked example: a nutritionist claims average daily salt intake in a community exceeds the recommended 5 g/day. A sample of n=20 finds x̄=5.6 g, s=1.2 g. With H0: μ=5, H1: μ>5:
t = (5.6-5)/(1.2/√20) = 0.6/0.268 ≈ 2.236, with 19 degrees of freedom. Comparing to a t-table or computed p-value (p≈0.019) leads to rejecting H0 at α=0.05.

The z-Test for a Proportion

To test a claim about a population proportion, the test statistic compares the sample proportion to the hypothesized value p0, using the standard error implied by H0 itself.

\[ z = \frac{\hat{p}-p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}} \]
Worked example: a public health campaign aims to raise handwashing compliance above a historical 60% baseline. H0: p=0.60, H1: p>0.60. A post-campaign survey of n=400 finds p̂=0.655.
z = (0.655-0.60)/√(0.60(0.40)/400) = 0.055/0.0245 ≈ 2.245. p-value = P(Z≥2.245)≈0.0124, leading to rejecting H0 at α=0.05: the campaign appears to have raised compliance.

Type I and Type II Errors

Every hypothesis test decision carries a risk of error, since decisions are made from sample data rather than complete knowledge of the population.

Type I error
Rejecting H0 when it is actually true (a "false positive"). The probability of this error is exactly α, the significance level chosen in advance.
Type II error
Failing to reject H0 when it is actually false (a "false negative"). Its probability is denoted β.
The courtroom parallel: Type I error is convicting an innocent defendant; Type II error is acquitting a guilty one. Lowering α reduces the chance of wrongful conviction but, all else equal, increases the chance of letting a guilty defendant go free.

The Trade-Off Between Errors, and Statistical Power

For a fixed sample size, decreasing α (to lower the Type I error risk) increases β (the Type II error risk), and vice versa. The only way to reduce both simultaneously is to increase the sample size.

\[ \text{Power} = 1 - \beta \]

Power is the probability of correctly rejecting H0 when it is actually false, the test's ability to detect a real effect when one truly exists.

Practical implication: a study with low power (a small sample facing a small true effect) may fail to detect a real difference, not because the effect does not exist, but because the test lacked the sensitivity to find it. Researchers calculate required sample size in advance partly to ensure adequate power.

The Connection to Confidence Intervals

Hypothesis tests and confidence intervals, both introduced from the same sampling distribution foundation, are two views of the same evidence and are mathematically linked.

The rule: a two-tailed hypothesis test at significance level α rejects H0: μ=μ0 if and only if μ0 falls outside the corresponding (1-α) confidence interval for μ.
Checking the rice bag example: the 95% CI for the filling machine's mean would be 24.7 ± 1.96(0.15) = (24.41, 24.99). Since the hypothesized value 25 falls just outside this interval, the two-tailed test at α=0.05 rejects H0: μ=25, exactly matching the earlier decision on Slide 9.

Anchor Case: Testing a New Teaching Method

A university department pilots a new teaching method, hoping it raises average exam scores above the historical mean of μ=68 with σ=9. H0: μ=68, H1: μ>68. A pilot cohort of n=81 students taught with the new method scores x̄=70.1.

\[ z = \frac{70.1-68}{9/\sqrt{81}} = \frac{2.1}{1.0} = 2.1 \]
p-value = P(Z≥2.1) = 1-Φ(2.1) ≈ 1-0.9821 = 0.0179. At α=0.05, since 0.0179≤0.05, the department rejects H0, concluding the pilot's improvement is unlikely to be due to chance alone.
Statistical significance is not the whole story: a 2.1-point increase being statistically significant does not automatically mean it is large enough to justify the cost and disruption of department-wide adoption, a separate question of practical significance.

Common Pitfalls

"The p-value is the probability H0 is true"
Incorrect. The p-value is calculated assuming H0 is true; it is the probability of the observed (or more extreme) data given H0, not the probability of H0 itself.
"A small p-value proves a large, important effect"
Incorrect. With a large enough sample size, even a tiny, practically meaningless difference can produce a small p-value. Statistical significance and practical importance are different questions.
Multiple testing: running many hypothesis tests on the same data and reporting only the ones with small p-values (sometimes called "p-hacking") inflates the true Type I error rate well above the stated α, since chance alone guarantees some tests will appear significant when nothing real is happening.

Module 16 Recap

  • 1A null hypothesis states no effect or no difference; the alternative hypothesis states the claim of interest.
  • 2One-tailed tests detect a specified direction of difference; two-tailed tests detect any difference.
  • 3The test statistic measures how far the sample result is from the null hypothesis's claim, in standard error units.
  • 4The p-value is the probability of a result at least as extreme as observed, assuming H0 is true; it is compared to α to reach a decision.
  • 5Type I error (false positive, probability α) and Type II error (false negative, probability β) trade off against each other; power=1-β.
  • 6A two-tailed test's conclusion matches whether the hypothesized value falls inside or outside the corresponding confidence interval.
  • 7Statistical significance does not guarantee practical importance, and multiple testing without correction inflates false-positive risk.

📊 Real Cases

Work through each scenario, then reveal the analysis.

Case 1: "The p-value Was 0.04, So There's a 96% Chance We're Right"
A graduate student reports a hypothesis test result with p=0.04 and tells their supervisor, "there is a 96% chance our hypothesis is correct." Is this statement a valid interpretation of the p-value?
No problem, this is one of the most common misunderstandings in statistics. The reveal panel explains the correct interpretation.
The statement is incorrect. A p-value of 0.04 means: if H0 were true, a result this extreme or more so would occur about 4% of the time. It says nothing about the probability that H1 (or H0) is correct. The p-value is a statement about the data given the hypothesis, not a statement about the hypothesis given the data. The correct phrasing is something like: "the evidence is strong enough to reject H0 at the 5% significance level."
Case 2: Choosing One-Tailed After Seeing the Data
A researcher runs a two-tailed test and gets p=0.08, which is not significant at α=0.05. Noticing the sample mean was above the hypothesized value, the researcher switches to a one-tailed test (H1: μ>μ0), which gives p=0.04, now significant. Is this a valid analysis?
Think about when the test's direction was chosen, before or after seeing which way the data leaned.
This is not valid. The choice of one-tailed versus two-tailed, and the direction of a one-tailed test, must be fixed before examining the data, based on the research question. Switching to a one-tailed test only after seeing that the result leans in a convenient direction inflates the true Type I error rate beyond the stated α, since the researcher is effectively given two chances to find significance.
Case 3: A Microloan Program's Impact Test
A microfinance institution claims its loan program raises average monthly household income above the regional baseline of 9,000 taka. A survey of n=64 client households finds x̄=9,300 taka, with σ=1,200 taka assumed known from prior regional surveys. Using H0: μ=9,000, H1: μ>9,000 and α=0.05, what does the institution conclude?
Standard error = σ/√n = 1200/8 = 150. From there, compute z=(x̄-μ0)/SE.
SE = 1200/√64 = 1200/8 = 150. z = (9300-9000)/150 = 300/150 = 2.0. p-value = P(Z≥2.0) = 1-0.9772 = 0.0228. Since 0.0228≤0.05, the institution rejects H0: the survey provides statistically significant evidence that average household income exceeds the 9,000 taka baseline. As always, this conclusion describes the surveyed clients and does not by itself establish that the loan program caused the increase, since other factors could also explain it.
Case 4: Twenty Tests, One "Significant" Result
A research team tests twenty different unrelated dietary factors against a single health outcome, each at α=0.05, with no true effect actually present for any of them. One of the twenty tests comes back significant (p<0.05), and the team prepares to publish it as a major finding. What is the concern?
Each individual test has a 5% false-positive chance under α=0.05. With twenty independent tests run on factors with no real effect, how many false positives would be expected purely by chance?
With α=0.05 and twenty independent tests where no true effect exists anywhere, roughly one false positive (20 × 0.05 = 1) is expected purely by chance. The single "significant" result is very likely exactly that: a false positive produced by running many tests, not a genuine dietary effect. This is the multiple testing problem; publishing the one significant result while ignoring the other nineteen, without correcting for the number of tests performed, would be a textbook case of p-hacking.
