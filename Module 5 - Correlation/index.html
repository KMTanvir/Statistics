Reading a Relationship From a Scatter Plot

Module 2 introduced the scatter plot as the right chart for two quantitative variables. Before assigning a number to a relationship, it helps to recognize its basic shape by eye.

A relationship has two basic properties: direction (do the variables move together or in opposite directions) and strength (how tightly the points cluster around a clear trend, versus scattering widely). Pearson's r turns both properties into a single number.

Pearson's Correlation Coefficient, r

Pearson's r measures the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two quantitative variables. It is calculated from the same deviations from the mean used in Module 4, but now for two variables at once.

\[ r = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2}} \]
Sign of r
Positive r: as one variable rises, the other tends to rise too. Negative r: as one rises, the other tends to fall.
Magnitude of r
Always between −1 and +1. The closer |r| is to 1, the more tightly the points cluster around a straight line.
r = 0 does not mean "no relationship." It means no linear relationship. Slide 7 shows why this distinction matters.

Worked Example: Study Hours and Exam Score

Six students report hours studied for an exam and their resulting score out of 100.

Mean hours = 5.67, mean score = 68.
Σ(x − mean x)(y − mean y) = 185.0
Σ(x − mean x)² = 33.33, Σ(y − mean y)² = 1050
r = 185.0 ÷ √(33.33 × 1050) = 185.0 ÷ 187.03 = 0.99

An r of 0.99 indicates an almost perfectly straight, strongly positive relationship: in this small dataset, more hours studied is very consistently associated with a higher score.

Interpreting the Strength of r

There is no single official cutoff, but the following rule of thumb is widely used across the social and natural sciences for interpreting the magnitude of r, regardless of sign.

|r| rangeInterpretation
0.00 – 0.10Negligible
0.10 – 0.30Weak
0.30 – 0.50Moderate
0.50 – 0.70Strong
0.70 – 0.90Very strong
0.90 – 1.00Near perfect
These bands are guidelines, not laws. What counts as a "strong" correlation in a controlled physics experiment may be considered weak in social or economic data, where many factors are always at play. Always read r in light of the field and the stakes of the question.

Properties and Limits of r

  • Unitless: r has no units, so it can compare relationships across completely different kinds of variables.
  • Symmetric: the correlation between x and y is identical to the correlation between y and x.
  • Only linear: r measures how close points fall to a straight line. A strong curved relationship can produce a low r.
  • Sensitive to outliers: a single extreme point can pull r noticeably higher or lower than what the bulk of the data would suggest.
  • Says nothing about causation: a high r describes a pattern, not an explanation for why that pattern exists.
Carried forward from Module 4: just as the mean alone hid important information that the standard deviation revealed, r alone can hide important information that only a look at the actual scatter plot reveals. Never compute r without plotting the data first.

When r Misses the Real Relationship

Recall the Module 2 scatter plot of fertilizer amount against crop yield. Suppose the true relationship is not a straight line but an inverted U: yield rises with fertilizer up to a point, then falls as over-fertilization damages the soil.

If r were calculated on this exact data, it would come out close to zero, even though fertilizer and yield are obviously and strongly related. A straight line simply cannot describe a curve that goes up and then down; r can only ask "how close to a line," and the honest answer here is "not close at all."
The lesson: always look at the scatter plot before trusting r. A low r means no straight-line relationship was found. It never proves that no relationship exists at all.

Correlation Is Not Causation

Across many summer seasons, the number of cold drinks sold at roadside stalls and the number of recorded river drowning incidents both rise and fall together, producing a strong positive correlation.

The tempting, wrong conclusion: drinking more cold drinks somehow causes more drownings, or fewer drownings would somehow reduce cold drink sales.
The actual explanation: hot weather independently drives both. Hotter days lead more people to buy cold drinks, and separately lead more people to swim and bathe in rivers and ponds, raising drowning risk. Temperature is the hidden common cause behind a correlation that has nothing to do with cold drinks themselves.
The general principle: observing that two variables move together, no matter how strong the correlation, is never by itself enough to conclude that one causes the other.

Three Explanations Behind Any Correlation

Whenever two variables, X and Y, show a meaningful correlation, there are exactly three possible explanations, and a correlation coefficient alone cannot tell you which one is true.

1. Direct causation
X genuinely causes Y (or Y causes X). Establishing this convincingly usually requires a controlled experiment, as discussed in Module 1.
2. Reverse causation
The assumed direction is backwards: it is actually Y that drives X, not X that drives Y.
3. Confounding variable
A third variable, Z, independently influences both X and Y, creating a correlation between them with no direct link at all.

The cold drinks and drowning example from the previous slide is a confounding variable case, with temperature as Z. The next two slides examine reverse causation and confounding in development data.

Anchor Case: NGOs and Poverty

A district-level dataset shows that districts with a higher density of NGO field offices also tend to have higher poverty rates, a moderately strong positive correlation.

A careless headline: "NGO presence linked to higher poverty," implying NGOs are making poverty worse, or are at least ineffective.
The more plausible explanation: reverse causation. NGOs do not select districts at random. They deliberately concentrate their offices in the districts that already have the greatest need, precisely because poverty is high there. The poverty did not result from the NGO presence; the NGO presence resulted from the poverty.
Why this matters for policy: mistaking the direction of this relationship could lead to the damaging conclusion that NGO programs should be reduced. A careful reading instead asks whether outcomes in NGO-served districts are improving faster than comparable districts started at the same poverty level, a much harder but far more meaningful question than reading the raw correlation alone.

Module 5 Recap

Five principles to carry forward:

  • 1A scatter plot reveals the direction and strength of a relationship before any number is calculated.
  • 2Pearson's r summarizes the strength and direction of a linear relationship in a single number between −1 and +1.
  • 3r is unitless, symmetric, sensitive to outliers, and blind to nonlinear patterns; always plot the data before trusting it.
  • 4Correlation alone never establishes causation. Every correlation has three possible explanations: direct causation, reverse causation, or a confounding variable.
  • 5Spotting which explanation is plausible requires real-world reasoning about the variables involved, not just a larger or smaller r.

1.A scatter plot shows that as advertising spend increases, monthly sales also consistently increase. This pattern describes:

When both variables tend to rise together, the relationship is a positive correlation.

2.Pearson's correlation coefficient r can take which range of values?

Pearson's r is always between −1 and +1, with the sign showing direction and the magnitude showing strength.

3.Two variables have a correlation of r = −0.85. What does this indicate?

The negative sign indicates the variables move in opposite directions, and a magnitude of 0.85 falls in the very strong range.

4.A dataset has a true relationship shaped like an inverted U (rising, then falling). Pearson's r calculated on this data is likely to be:

r only detects straight-line patterns. A symmetric rise-then-fall pattern produces an r near zero even though fertilizer and yield are clearly related.

5.Which of the following is a genuine property of Pearson's r?

r is unitless (it does not change if units are converted) and symmetric (the correlation of x with y equals the correlation of y with x).

6.Adding a single extreme outlier to an otherwise weakly correlated dataset can:

Because r is built from squared-style deviation products, a single extreme point can pull its value noticeably higher or lower than what the bulk of the data supports.

7.Cold drink sales and river drowning incidents both rise and fall together across the year, producing a strong positive correlation. The best explanation is:

Temperature independently increases both cold drink purchases and swimming-related drowning risk, making it a classic confounding variable.

8.Districts with more NGO field offices tend to have higher poverty rates. The most plausible explanation is:

NGOs target high-need districts, so existing poverty drives NGO placement, not the other way around. This is reverse causation.

9.In a small dataset, every time x increases, y consistently decreases by a roughly proportional amount. The sign of Pearson's r for this data would be:

A consistent decrease in y as x increases is the definition of a negative linear relationship, so r would be negative.

10.Why is observing a strong correlation between two variables never sufficient, by itself, to prove causation?

A correlation coefficient cannot distinguish among the three possible explanations behind it; ruling out reverse causation and confounding requires additional reasoning or, ideally, a controlled experiment.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: Cold Drinks and Drowning

A district health office notices that, month by month over several years, sales of cold drinks at roadside stalls and the number of river and pond drowning incidents rise and fall together with a strong positive correlation.

Should the office conclude that cold drinks somehow contribute to drowning risk, or that reducing drink sales would reduce drownings?

A strong, consistent correlation feels like solid evidence of a direct link between the two variables, especially when it holds up year after year.
Hot weather is the hidden common cause. Hotter months independently increase cold drink purchases and independently increase swimming and bathing in open water, which raises drowning risk. Neither variable causes the other; both respond to the season. Reducing cold drink sales would do nothing to make rivers safer.

Case 2: NGO Presence and Poverty

A national dataset shows that districts with more NGO field offices per capita also have higher poverty rates than districts with fewer NGO offices.

Does this mean NGO activity is making poverty worse, or that NGO programs are not working?

It is easy to assume the correlation runs from NGO presence to poverty, since that is the order the variables are usually listed in.
The causal arrow almost certainly runs the other way. NGOs choose where to work based on where need is greatest, so high poverty draws in NGOs rather than NGOs creating poverty. This is reverse causation. A fair evaluation of NGO effectiveness would need to compare how poverty changes over time in served districts against similarly poor districts that were not served, not simply compare poverty levels across districts at one point in time.

Case 3: Mobile Financial Service Agents and Household Savings

Across upazilas, the number of mobile financial service agent points per 1,000 people correlates strongly with the average household savings rate. Areas with more agent points also tend to have noticeably higher savings.

Can the office conclude that placing more agent points in an area would directly raise household savings there?

Because mobile financial services are designed to encourage saving, it feels natural to read the correlation as direct proof that the agents themselves are causing higher savings.
Urbanization and income level are plausible confounding variables. Areas that are more urbanized and have higher average income tend to attract more agent points as a business decision, and separately tend to have higher savings simply because residents have more disposable income. Without accounting for income and urbanization, the raw correlation overstates what installing more agent points alone would achieve in a low-income, low-density area.

Case 4: Fertilizer and Crop Yield, Revisited

An agricultural extension office collects fertilizer-amount and yield data across 40 plots. The true underlying relationship rises with fertilizer up to a point and then falls as overuse damages the soil, but the office only reports that "the correlation between fertilizer and yield was weak (r = 0.08), so fertilizer amount does not matter much for yield."

Is this conclusion justified by the data described?

A low r is often read as final proof that two variables are unrelated, since r is the standard number used to summarize a relationship.
A low r only rules out a straight-line relationship; it says nothing about curved ones. If the true pattern is an inverted U, fertilizer matters enormously for yield, but in a way that increases yield up to an optimal amount and then decreases it, a pattern that nearly cancels out in a linear correlation calculation. The office's conclusion is not justified; a scatter plot, not r alone, is the only way to catch this.
