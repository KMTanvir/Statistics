Poisson Distribution

Module 11  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Recognize the conditions of a Poisson process and identify when it applies to a counting situation
  • Calculate a Poisson probability using the Poisson formula
  • Calculate cumulative Poisson probabilities, including "at least" and "at most" questions
  • Calculate and interpret the mean and variance of a Poisson distribution
  • Rescale a Poisson rate across different time or space intervals
  • Use the Poisson distribution as an approximation to the binomial, and recognize when the Poisson model itself breaks down
Continuing from Modules 9 and 10: the binomial distribution counted successes in a fixed number of discrete trials, and the negative binomial counted trials needed to reach a fixed number of successes. The Poisson distribution removes the idea of discrete trials altogether, counting how many times a rare event occurs over a continuous span of time or space.

The Poisson Process

A Poisson process describes events that occur randomly and independently over a continuous interval of time or space, at a constant average rate. Three conditions must hold:

Constant average rate
Events occur at a steady average rate, denoted λ (lambda), over the interval being studied. λ does not change within the interval.
Independence
The occurrence of one event does not make another event more or less likely to occur elsewhere in the interval.
No simultaneous events
Two events cannot occur at exactly the same instant or point; in any sufficiently small sub-interval, the probability of more than one event is negligible.
Contrast with the binomial: the binomial distribution counts successes among a fixed, countable number of discrete trials (n). The Poisson distribution has no such n: it counts events that could in principle happen any number of times across a continuous interval, with only the average rate λ fixed in advance.

The Poisson Probability Formula

If X is the number of events occurring in an interval with average rate λ, the probability of observing exactly x events is:

\[ P(X = x) = \frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^{x}}{x!}, \qquad x = 0, 1, 2, \dots \]

Here e ≈ 2.71828 is the base of the natural logarithm, and x! is the familiar factorial. Unlike the binomial formula, there is no upper limit on x: in principle, any non-negative whole number of events is possible, though probabilities shrink rapidly once x is far from λ.

Only one parameter: the entire Poisson distribution is determined by λ alone, the average number of events per interval. This is simpler than the binomial, which requires both n and p.

Worked Example: Snakebite Cases at a Rural Clinic

During monsoon season, a rural health clinic receives an average of 3 snakebite patients per week (λ = 3). Assuming arrivals are independent and occur at a steady average rate, what is the probability the clinic receives exactly 5 snakebite patients in a given week?

\[ P(X = 5) = \frac{e^{-3} \, 3^{5}}{5!} \]

Shape: like the binomial with small p, the Poisson distribution is right-skewed for small λ, with a peak near λ and a long tail toward higher counts. As λ grows large, the Poisson distribution becomes increasingly symmetric and bell-shaped.

Cumulative Poisson Probabilities

As with the binomial and negative binomial distributions, the complement rule from Module 8 is the fastest route to "at least" questions.

"At most 2 cases in a week" (λ=3):
P(X ≤ 2) = P(0) + P(1) + P(2) = 0.0498 + 0.1494 + 0.2240 = 0.4232.
"At least 3 cases in a week":
P(X ≥ 3) = 1 − P(X ≤ 2) = 1 − 0.4232 = 0.5768, meaning the clinic should expect 3 or more snakebite cases in more than half of all weeks during monsoon season.
This complement-rule shortcut matters even more for the Poisson distribution than for the binomial, since x has no upper limit, and summing every individual term beyond a handful of values quickly becomes impractical by hand.

Mean and Variance of the Poisson Distribution

The Poisson distribution has an unusually simple and memorable property: its mean and variance are both equal to λ.

\[ \mu = E(X) = \lambda, \qquad \sigma^2 = Var(X) = \lambda \]
Snakebite clinic example (λ = 3):
μ = 3 cases per week, on average.
σ² = 3, so σ = √3 ≈ 1.73 cases.
Why this is notable: for the binomial distribution, mean (np) and variance (np(1-p)) are generally different. For the Poisson distribution they are always identical. This gives a useful diagnostic: if real count data has a sample variance much larger than its sample mean ("overdispersion"), the data likely violates the Poisson assumptions, often because the event rate is not actually constant.

The Poisson Approximation to the Binomial

When a binomial experiment has a large number of trials n and a small probability of success p, such that the product λ = np stays moderate, the binomial distribution is closely approximated by a Poisson distribution with that same λ.

\[ \text{Binomial}(n, p) \approx \text{Poisson}(\lambda = np), \quad \text{when } n \text{ is large and } p \text{ is small} \]
Worked example: a rare genetic condition affects 0.03% of newborns (p = 0.0003) in a region with 10,000 births per year (n = 10,000). The exact binomial calculation of P(X=5) is cumbersome by hand, but with λ = np = 10,000 × 0.0003 = 3, the Poisson formula from Slide 3 gives P(X=5) ≈ 0.1008, the same value computed for the snakebite clinic.
Practical rule of thumb: the Poisson approximation works well when n ≥ 20 and p ≤ 0.05, and especially well when n is very large and p is very small, exactly the regime where binomial calculations by hand or by combinations become impractical.

Rescaling the Rate Across Intervals

Because λ is an average rate, it scales proportionally with the size of the interval. If the average rate over one interval is known, the rate over a longer or shorter interval is found by simple proportion.

From weeks to days: if the snakebite clinic averages λ = 3 cases per week, the average daily rate is λday = 3 / 7 ≈ 0.4286 cases per day.

From weeks to months: over a 4-week month, λmonth = 3 × 4 = 12 cases per month.
A common error: using the weekly λ = 3 directly in a question that asks about a single day, or vice versa, without rescaling, produces a probability for the wrong interval entirely. Always match λ to the interval the question actually asks about before applying the Poisson formula.

Combining Independent Poisson Processes

If two independent Poisson processes occur over the same interval, with rates λ1 and λ2, the total number of combined events is itself Poisson distributed, with rate λ1 + λ2.

\[ X_1 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1), \ X_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_2), \ \text{independent} \implies X_1 + X_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2) \]
Worked example: two rural clinics in neighboring upazilas each see snakebite cases independently, Clinic A at λA = 3 per week and Clinic B at λB = 2 per week. The district's total weekly snakebite caseload across both clinics follows a Poisson distribution with λ = 3 + 2 = 5 cases per week.
Use in practice: this property lets a district health office aggregate independently monitored facilities into a single regional rate without re-deriving the distribution from scratch.

Anchor Case: Accidents at a Dhaka Intersection

A busy intersection records an average of 2.5 traffic accidents per month (λ = 2.5), based on several years of data. A traffic safety committee proposes a rule: "if more than 5 accidents occur in a month, flag the intersection for an emergency engineering review."

Question: how often would this rule trigger purely by chance, even if nothing about the intersection has changed?
Summing P(0) through P(5) for λ=2.5 gives P(X ≤ 5) ≈ 0.9580.
P(X > 5) = 1 − 0.9580 ≈ 0.0420, about a 4.2% chance in any given month.
Interpreting the 4.2%: over a year, a 4.2% monthly chance means the rule will likely trigger a review roughly once every two years even with no real change in safety, a "false alarm" rate the committee should weigh deliberately, exactly as the factory manager in Module 9 had to weigh a defect-rate alarm threshold.

When the Poisson Conditions Fail

Non-constant rate
If accidents cluster heavily during rush hours and are rare overnight, a single daily λ averaged across the whole day misrepresents risk at any specific hour.
Non-independent events
If one flood damages upstream embankments and makes a second flood more likely soon after, the independence assumption is violated.
Overdispersion as a warning sign: when real count data shows a sample variance much larger than the sample mean, contradicting the Poisson property that μ = σ², this is strong evidence that the constant-rate or independence assumptions do not hold, and a more flexible model (such as the negative binomial used for overdispersed counts) may be more appropriate.
Practical fix: splitting a non-constant-rate interval into smaller, more homogeneous sub-intervals (such as separate rates for rush hour versus overnight) often restores a reasonable approximation to the Poisson model within each sub-interval.

Choosing Among Binomial, Negative Binomial, and Poisson

The common thread across all three: every model in this family rests on the same foundation laid in Module 8, independent events with a constant probability or rate. What differs is what the study design or process holds fixed: the number of trials, the number of successes, or neither, only an average rate.

Module 11 Recap

  • 1The Poisson distribution counts rare events over a continuous interval of time or space, governed by a single parameter λ, the average rate.
  • 2A Poisson process requires a constant average rate, independent events, and no two events at exactly the same instant.
  • 3The Poisson formula, P(X=x) = e^(−λ) λ^x / x!, has no upper limit on x.
  • 4Mean and variance are both equal to λ, an unusually simple and diagnostically useful property.
  • 5The Poisson distribution approximates the binomial well when n is large and p is small, with λ = np.
  • 6λ scales proportionally with interval length, and independent Poisson rates over the same interval add together.
  • 7Overdispersion (sample variance much larger than sample mean) signals that the constant-rate or independence assumptions likely fail.

📝 Module Quiz

14 questions on the Poisson process, the Poisson formula, mean and variance, rate scaling, and the binomial approximation.

1.Which of the following is NOT a required condition of a Poisson process?

Unlike the binomial distribution, the Poisson distribution has no fixed number of discrete trials; it counts events over a continuous interval governed only by the rate λ.

2.The Poisson distribution is fully determined by:

The entire Poisson distribution is determined by a single parameter, λ, the average rate of events.

3.A clinic averages λ=3 snakebite cases per week. What is P(X=5)?

P(X=5) = e^(-3)(3^5)/5! = (0.04979 × 243)/120 ≈ 0.1008.

4.For a Poisson distribution with λ = 3, P(X ≤ 2) = 0.4232. What is P(X ≥ 3)?

By the complement rule, P(X ≥ 3) = 1 - P(X ≤ 2) = 1 - 0.4232 = 0.5768.

5.For any Poisson distribution, the relationship between the mean and the variance is:

For the Poisson distribution, μ = σ² = λ, always.

6.The Poisson distribution provides a good approximation to the binomial distribution when:

The Poisson approximation works well for large n and small p, holding λ=np at a moderate value.

7.A rare condition affects 0.03% of 10,000 newborns. What λ should be used for a Poisson approximation?

λ = np = 10,000 × 0.0003 = 3.

8.If a process has λ=3 cases per week, what is the average daily rate?

λ scales proportionally with the interval length: 3 per week / 7 days per week = 0.4286 per day.

9.Clinic A sees cases at λA=3/week and Clinic B independently at λB=2/week. What is the distribution of their combined weekly caseload?

The sum of two independent Poisson variables is Poisson with rate equal to the sum: λ=3+2=5.

10.An intersection averages λ=2.5 accidents per month, and P(X ≤ 5) ≈ 0.9580. What is the probability of more than 5 accidents in a month?

P(X > 5) = 1 - P(X ≤ 5) = 1 - 0.9580 = 0.0420.

11.Why might using a single daily λ for traffic accidents be misleading if accidents cluster heavily during rush hour?

A single daily average masks the fact that the rate is much higher during rush hour and much lower overnight, violating the constant-rate assumption within the day.

12."Overdispersion" in count data refers to:

Overdispersion describes data where the variance substantially exceeds the mean, signaling that the Poisson assumptions of constant rate or independence likely do not hold.

13.A flood-prone river basin averages λ=1.2 floods per year. What is P(zero floods in a year)?

P(X=0) = e^(-λ) λ^0 / 0! = e^(-1.2) ≈ 0.3012, since λ^0=1 and 0!=1.

14.A flash flood damages an upstream embankment, making a second flood more likely in the weeks that follow. Why does this violate the Poisson model?

The Poisson model requires that events be independent; if one flood physically increases the likelihood of a subsequent flood, that condition is violated.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: Staffing a Rural Clinic for Peak Demand

A rural clinic averages λ=3 snakebite cases per week during monsoon season and staffs exactly enough antivenom doses for 3 cases per week, reasoning that this matches the average demand.

Is stocking exactly the average (3 doses) a safe staffing policy?

Stocking exactly the average demand feels intuitively sufficient, since "on average" the supply should match the need.
Matching the mean ignores the considerable week-to-week variability. With λ=3, σ=√3≈1.73, and P(X ≥ 3)≈0.5768, meaning the clinic will need 3 or more doses in well over half of all weeks, and will sometimes need 5, 6, or more. Stocking only the mean guarantees frequent shortages; the clinic should calculate a higher stock level, such as the 90th or 95th percentile of the Poisson distribution, to cover demand in nearly all weeks.

Case 2: A Rare-Disease Risk Calculation

A health analyst needs to compute the probability that exactly 4 newborns out of 10,000 (p=0.0003 each) develop a rare congenital condition, and worries that the exact binomial formula, involving C(10000,4), is too unwieldy to compute by hand.

Is there a practical shortcut available?

With n this large, it seems the only path forward is computing the unwieldy combination C(10000,4) directly, or giving up on an exact answer.
Since n=10,000 is large and p=0.0003 is small, the Poisson approximation with λ=np=3 applies, replacing the unwieldy binomial calculation with the simple formula P(X=4)=e^(-3)(3^4)/4! ≈ 0.168. This is far easier to compute and very close to the exact binomial probability, illustrating exactly why the Poisson approximation exists.

Case 3: Comparing Two "Rates" Across Reports

One district report states "the river basin floods at a rate of 1.2 per year," while a partner agency's report on the same basin states "0.1 floods per month," and a reviewer assumes one of the two figures must be wrong.

Can both figures be correct descriptions of the same flooding pattern?

1.2 per year and 0.1 per month look like different numbers describing the same basin, inviting suspicion that one report contains an error.
Both figures describe the same underlying rate, just rescaled across different intervals: 1.2 floods per year / 12 months per year = 0.1 floods per month exactly. Because λ scales proportionally with interval length, neither figure is wrong; they are consistent once converted to a common timeframe. The lesson is to always check the stated interval before comparing two reported rates.

Case 4: A Misapplied Poisson Model After Embankment Failure

A planning office uses a Poisson model with λ=1.2 floods per year to estimate flood risk for an upcoming year, even though a major embankment failed during the most recent flood and has not yet been repaired, substantially raising the basin's vulnerability to a follow-on flood in the near term.

Is continuing to apply the same Poisson model with λ=1.2 appropriate going forward?

Since λ=1.2 is based on solid historical data, it can seem reasonable to keep using the same established rate going forward.
No, the historical λ=1.2 was estimated under conditions where the independence assumption held. With the embankment damaged, a flood is now more likely to be followed by another flood in the near term, violating independence and likely raising the effective near-term rate above 1.2. The planning office should not mechanically apply the historical Poisson model immediately after such a structural change; the rate needs re-estimation, or a different model entirely, until normal independent conditions are restored.
