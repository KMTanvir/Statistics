Dispersion, Shape, and Spread

Module 4  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Calculate and interpret the range and interquartile range as basic measures of spread
  • Calculate variance and standard deviation for raw and grouped data
  • Explain why sample variance divides by n minus 1 rather than n
  • Use the coefficient of variation to compare variability across different units or scales
  • Apply the empirical rule to interpret a standard deviation in roughly bell-shaped data
  • Measure skewness and kurtosis numerically and connect them to distribution shape
The question this module answers: Module 3 found the center of a distribution. But two distributions can share the exact same center and still behave completely differently. This module measures that difference.

Range and Interquartile Range

The simplest measure of spread is the range: the distance between the largest and smallest value in a dataset.

\[ \text{Range} = \text{Maximum} - \text{Minimum} \]
Batsman A's last 10 innings: 35, 38, 40, 42, 44, 36, 39, 41, 43, 42.
Range = 44 − 35 = 9 runs.
The range's weakness: it depends on only two values, the maximum and the minimum, and ignores everything in between. A single unusually high or low value can make the range badly misleading, exactly the same vulnerability the mean has to outliers.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

Recall from Module 2 that the IQR measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data, the distance between the first quartile (Q1) and the third quartile (Q3).

\[ IQR = Q_3 - Q_1 \]

Because the IQR excludes the most extreme 25% on each end, it is far more resistant to outliers than the range, the same property that makes the median resistant compared with the mean.

Variance and Standard Deviation

Variance measures the average squared distance of each value from the mean. Standard deviation is the square root of variance, which returns the measure to the original units of the data.

\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1} \qquad s = \sqrt{s^2} \]
Worked example: Batsman A, mean = 40

Sum of squared deviations = 25+4+0+4+16+16+1+1+9+4 = 80.

Treating these 10 innings as the population of interest: Variance = 80 ÷ 10 = 8. Standard deviation = √8 = 2.83 runs.

Reading the standard deviation: a typical innings for Batsman A lands about 2.83 runs away from his average of 40. A small standard deviation relative to the mean signals a consistent performer.

Population Variance vs. Sample Variance

The formula for variance changes slightly depending on whether the data represents an entire population or a sample drawn from a larger population.

Population variance
\[ \sigma^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \mu)^2}{N} \]Divides by N, the full population size. Used when the data already represents every unit of interest.
Sample variance
\[ s^2 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1} \]Divides by n − 1 rather than n. Used when the data is a sample meant to estimate a larger population.

Why n minus 1?

A sample mean is calculated from the same data used to measure spread around it, so the sample tends to be slightly closer to its own mean than the true population is to the population mean. Dividing by a smaller number, n − 1 instead of n, inflates the result just enough to correct for this tendency. This adjustment is called Bessel's correction, and n − 1 is referred to as the degrees of freedom.

In practice: almost all real datasets are treated as samples, since they are rarely a complete census of every unit that could exist. For this reason, the n − 1 sample formula is the one used by default throughout the rest of this course.

The Coefficient of Variation

Standard deviation is expressed in the original units of the data, which makes it impossible to compare variability across variables measured on different scales. The coefficient of variation (CV) solves this by expressing standard deviation as a percentage of the mean.

\[ CV = \frac{s}{\bar{x}} \times 100\% \]
Introducing Batsman B, also averaging 40 across the same 10 innings: 5, 10, 90, 100, 5, 15, 95, 8, 12, 60.
BatsmanMeanStd. DeviationCoefficient of Variation
A402.837.1%
B4039.1397.8%

Both batsmen average exactly 40 runs. Batsman A's standard deviation is only 7.1% of his mean, an extremely consistent record. Batsman B's standard deviation is 97.8% of his mean, almost as large as the average itself, indicating wild, unpredictable swings between very low and very high scores.

Why CV matters: comparing raw standard deviations across different units, say, runs scored versus balls faced, or BDT versus USD, is meaningless. The CV strips away the units, allowing fair comparison of relative variability.

The Empirical Rule

For data that is roughly bell-shaped (approximately symmetric, unimodal, and not heavily skewed), the empirical rule, also called the 68-95-99.7 rule, describes how observations cluster around the mean in terms of standard deviations.

  • About 68% of observations fall within 1 standard deviation of the mean
  • About 95% of observations fall within 2 standard deviations of the mean
  • About 99.7% of observations fall within 3 standard deviations of the mean
Applying it to the BMI data (mean = 26.06, SD = 4.52):
68% of students roughly between 21.5 and 30.6
95% of students roughly between 17.0 and 35.1
99.7% of students roughly between 12.5 and 39.6
Caution: the empirical rule assumes a roughly bell-shaped distribution. Since the BMI data is mildly right-skewed, these percentages are useful approximations, not exact figures.

Measuring Skewness Numerically

Module 3 described skewness visually and through the ordering of mean, median, and mode. Skewness can also be measured with a single number using Pearson's coefficient of skewness.

\[ SK = \frac{3(\bar{x} - \text{Median})}{s} \]
Applying it to the BMI data: mean = 26.06, median = 25.68, SD = 4.52.
SK = 3(26.06 − 25.68) / 4.52 = 3(0.38) / 4.52 = 1.14 / 4.52 = 0.25
SK < 0
Left-skewed (negative skew). Mean is pulled below the median by a long left tail.
SK ≈ 0
Approximately symmetric. Mean and median are close together.
SK > 0
Right-skewed (positive skew). Mean is pulled above the median by a long right tail.

A skewness value of 0.25 is small but positive, confirming a mild right skew, consistent with the slightly longer right tail visible in the Module 2 BMI histogram.

Kurtosis: Peakedness and Tail Weight

Kurtosis measures how concentrated a distribution is around its center, and how heavy its tails are, compared with a normal (bell-shaped) distribution.

Leptokurtic
Sharper peak and heavier tails than normal. More extreme values occur than a bell curve would predict.
Mesokurtic
Kurtosis close to that of a normal distribution. Used as the baseline for comparison.
Platykurtic
Flatter peak and lighter tails than normal. Extreme values are rarer than a bell curve would predict.
Why it matters beyond the classroom: in finance, a leptokurtic ("fat-tailed") return distribution means extreme gains and losses happen more often than a normal-curve model would suggest, a key reason naive risk models have historically underestimated market crashes.

Two Batsmen With the Same Average

This module opened with a promise: two players who average exactly the same can be entirely different players. The full picture is now in front of us.

MeasureBatsman ABatsman B
Mean (average)4040
Range9 (35–44)95 (5–100)
Standard deviation2.8339.13
Coefficient of variation7.1%97.8%

A selector deciding between these two batsmen using the average alone would see no difference at all. Batsman A scores close to 40 in almost every innings: a dependable contributor, unlikely to either fail badly or score a match-winning century. Batsman B alternates between single-digit failures and explosive innings past 90: a high-risk, high-reward player whose contribution on any given day is nearly unpredictable.

The selection depends on context. A team chasing a small target steadily might prefer Batsman A's reliability. A team needing an explosive innings to win from a difficult position might gamble on Batsman B. Neither choice is "wrong," but neither choice can be made responsibly without looking past the average to the spread.
This is the central lesson of the module: the mean answers "what is typical." The standard deviation and CV answer "how much can you trust that typical value to actually occur." Reporting one without the other is reporting half the story.

Module 4 Recap

Six principles to carry forward:

  • 1The range uses only the maximum and minimum and is highly sensitive to outliers; the IQR, using only the middle 50%, is far more resistant.
  • 2Variance is the average squared deviation from the mean; standard deviation returns that measure to the original units.
  • 3Sample variance divides by n − 1, not n, to correct for the tendency of a sample to underestimate true population spread.
  • 4The coefficient of variation expresses spread as a percentage of the mean, allowing fair comparisons across different units or scales.
  • 5The empirical rule (68-95-99.7) describes how data clusters around the mean for roughly bell-shaped distributions.
  • 6Skewness and kurtosis give numeric descriptions of a distribution's asymmetry and tail weight, extending the visual judgments made in Module 3.
Next: Module 5 turns to probability, the mathematical foundation that explains why these patterns of center and spread arise in the first place, and how we can reason about uncertainty itself.

📝 Module Quiz

10 questions on range, variance, standard deviation, CV, the empirical rule, skewness, and kurtosis.

1.A dataset has minimum 12 and maximum 47. What is the range?

Range = Maximum − Minimum = 47 − 12 = 35.

2.Why is the IQR generally preferred over the range as a measure of spread?

The range depends only on the two most extreme values, so a single outlier distorts it. The IQR uses only the middle 50% of the data, making it far more resistant to extreme values.

3.Why does sample variance divide by n − 1 instead of n?

Because the sample mean is calculated from the same data, the sample tends to sit slightly closer to its own mean than the population does to its true mean. Dividing by n − 1 (Bessel's correction) compensates for this and gives a less biased estimate.

4.Two investments have very different average annual returns and are measured in different currencies. What is the best way to compare their relative variability?

The coefficient of variation expresses standard deviation as a percentage of the mean, removing the units entirely. This makes it the appropriate tool for comparing relative variability across different scales or currencies.

5.A roughly bell-shaped dataset has mean 50 and standard deviation 5. According to the empirical rule, about what percentage of values fall between 40 and 60?

40 and 60 are each 2 standard deviations from the mean of 50 (50 ± 2×5). The empirical rule states about 95% of values fall within 2 standard deviations of the mean.

6.A distribution has Pearson's coefficient of skewness equal to −0.6. This indicates:

A negative skewness coefficient indicates the mean lies below the median, the signature of a left-skewed (negatively skewed) distribution with a long left tail.

7.A stock's return distribution is leptokurtic. This means:

Leptokurtic distributions have a sharper peak and heavier tails than the normal distribution, meaning extreme outcomes (both very good and very bad) occur more frequently than a bell curve would suggest.

8.Batsman A averages 40 with SD 2.83. Batsman B averages 40 with SD 39.13. Which statement is correct?

Equal means say nothing about spread. Batsman A's small SD relative to his mean indicates consistent scores near 40; Batsman B's very large SD indicates wide swings between low and high scores.

9.In the grouped variance formula \( s^2 = \frac{\sum f_i m_i^2}{n} - \bar{x}^2 \), what does \(m_i\) represent?

m_i is the midpoint of class i, used as a stand-in for every value within that class, the same approximation used for the grouped mean in Module 3.

10.Why is reporting only the mean of a dataset, without any measure of spread, often an incomplete picture?

Two datasets can share an identical mean while behaving completely differently, as the two-batsmen example shows. A measure of spread is needed to know whether the mean is a trustworthy summary of any individual observation.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: Two Mutual Funds, Same Return

Two mutual funds both advertise an average annual return of 10% over the past five years. Fund X's yearly returns were 9%, 11%, 10%, 10%, 10%. Fund Y's yearly returns were −15%, 35%, 5%, 20%, 5%.

Are these two funds equally good investments? What is missing from the advertised "average return" figure?

Many investors compare funds using the headline average return alone, treating "same average" as "same investment quality."
Both funds do average 10%, but Fund X's returns barely deviate from year to year, while Fund Y experienced a 15% loss in one year and a 35% gain in another. Fund Y is far riskier: an investor who needed to withdraw money during its −15% year would have suffered a real loss despite the fund's attractive long-run average. The standard deviation and coefficient of variation of the yearly returns, not the average alone, reveal which fund is actually more suitable for a risk-averse investor.

Case 2: Comparing Two Production Lines

A factory manager compares two production lines making the same product. Both lines report an average of 3 defective units per 100 produced. Line 1's daily defect counts barely move from day to day. Line 2's daily defect counts swing between 0 defects on some days and 12 or more on others, despite averaging the same 3 per day.

Should the manager treat both lines as equally reliable, since they share the same average defect rate?

A matching average defect rate looks like equal quality control on paper, so it is tempting to conclude the two lines perform identically.
A high standard deviation in Line 2's defect counts signals an unpredictable process, even though it averages the same as Line 1. Unpredictability creates real costs: on bad days, Line 2 may produce defect spikes large enough to disrupt delivery schedules or trigger costly quality failures, even though its long-run average looks fine. Line 1's low variability makes it the more dependable process to plan around, regardless of the matching average.

Case 3: Comparing Two Exam Sections by Mean Alone

Following the bimodal exam discussion from Module 3, suppose both Section A and Section B of a course report a mean exam score of 70. Section A's scores cluster tightly between 65 and 75. Section B's scores split into two distinct clusters, roughly half near 50 and half near 90.

Does reporting "both sections averaged 70" fairly describe both classes?

An identical mean of 70 suggests, at first glance, that the two sections performed at a similar level overall.
Section A's low spread means 70 genuinely describes a typical student. Section B's high spread (and underlying bimodal shape) means almost no student in that section actually scored near 70: the figure describes neither the struggling half nor the strong half. A complete report would include the standard deviation (or note the bimodal shape directly) alongside the mean. Without it, "both sections averaged 70" creates a false impression of similar performance.

Case 4: Fat Tails in Investment Risk

A financial advisor presents two investment options as "equally risky," since both have similar standard deviations of monthly returns. Option P's monthly returns are well described by a normal (bell-shaped) curve. Option Q has the same standard deviation but is leptokurtic, meaning extreme monthly losses and gains occur noticeably more often than a normal curve would predict.

Are Option P and Option Q really equally risky, given that they share the same standard deviation?

Standard deviation is often treated as a complete measure of risk on its own, since it is the most commonly reported spread statistic in finance.
Standard deviation alone does not capture tail risk. Option Q's leptokurtic shape means severe losses (and severe gains) happen more frequently than the standard deviation alone would suggest, since a normal-curve model underestimates the chance of extreme events in fat-tailed distributions. Two investments can share an identical standard deviation while having very different chances of a severe loss month. This is precisely the kind of risk that purely mean-and-SD-based models have historically underestimated in real financial markets.
