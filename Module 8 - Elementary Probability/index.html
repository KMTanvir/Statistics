Elementary Probability

Module 8  ·  By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Define a sample space and an event, and distinguish simple from compound events
  • Assign probabilities using the classical, empirical, and subjective approaches
  • Apply the complement rule and the addition rule, including when events are not mutually exclusive
  • Distinguish independent from dependent events, and apply the multiplication rule to each
  • Calculate and interpret a conditional probability
  • Apply Bayes' theorem to update a probability in light of new evidence
  • Recognize common probability misconceptions, including base rate neglect and the gambler's fallacy
The question this module answers: every method covered so far in this course described data that had already been collected. Probability is the formal language for describing uncertainty about what has not yet happened, or what is not yet known, and it is the foundation underneath every distribution and every inference method covered later in this course.

Sample Space and Events

Any process with an uncertain outcome, called a random experiment, has a full list of possible outcomes called the sample space, denoted S.

Sample space (S)
The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. Example: rolling a six-sided die has sample space S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.
Event
Any subset of the sample space, a collection of outcomes we are interested in. Example: "rolling an even number" is the event {2, 4, 6}.
Simple event
An event consisting of exactly one outcome, such as "rolling a 4."
Compound event
An event consisting of two or more outcomes, such as "rolling an even number."
Example: a microcredit officer visiting a household records whether the household has electricity access (yes/no) and whether it has a mobile financial account (yes/no). The sample space has four outcomes: (yes,yes), (yes,no), (no,yes), (no,no).

What a Probability Must Satisfy

A probability is a number assigned to an event describing how likely it is. Every valid probability assignment must follow three basic rules:

  • The probability of any event A is between 0 and 1: \( 0 \le P(A) \le 1 \)
  • The probability of the entire sample space is 1: \( P(S) = 1 \), since something in the sample space must happen
  • The probabilities of all the simple events in the sample space sum to 1
\[ 0 \le P(A) \le 1, \qquad P(S) = 1 \]
A probability of 0 or 1 is still meaningful: P(A) = 0 means A essentially cannot happen; P(A) = 1 means A is essentially certain. Neither means the question was answered incorrectly.

Three Ways to Assign a Probability

Classical probability
Used when all outcomes in the sample space are equally likely. P(A) = (number of outcomes in A) divided by (total number of outcomes in S). Example: rolling a fair die, P(even) = 3/6 = 0.5.
Empirical probability
Based on observed relative frequency from past data. Example: if 18 of the last 120 intercity bus departures left more than 15 minutes late, the empirical probability of a late departure is 18/120 = 0.15.
Subjective probability
A degree of belief based on judgment and available information, used when neither equally likely outcomes nor extensive historical data are available. Example: an economist estimates a 30 percent probability that the central bank cuts its policy rate at the next meeting.
All three are legitimate: the right approach depends on what is actually known about the situation, not on which formula looks the most rigorous.

The Complement Rule

The complement of event A, written A′ (or "not A"), consists of every outcome in the sample space that is not in A. Since A and A′ together cover the whole sample space:

\[ P(A') = 1 - P(A) \]
If the probability that a randomly selected garment factory shipment passes quality inspection on the first try is 0.82, the probability that it does not pass on the first try is 1 − 0.82 = 0.18.
Useful trick: when "at least one" or "none" appears in a probability question, it is often far easier to calculate the complement first and subtract from 1, rather than calculating the event directly.

The Addition Rule: Mutually Exclusive Events

Two events are mutually exclusive if they cannot both happen at the same time, that is, they share no outcomes. For mutually exclusive events:

\[ P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) \]
A retailer's daily sales fall into exactly one of three categories: low, medium, or high. P(low) = 0.25, P(medium) = 0.45, P(high) = 0.30. Since a day cannot be both low and medium, P(low or medium) = 0.25 + 0.45 = 0.70.
Check before adding: simply adding probabilities only works when the two events truly cannot occur together. Adding probabilities for events that can overlap double-counts the overlap and gives a number that is too large.

The Addition Rule: General Case

When two events can occur together, the general addition rule subtracts the overlap once, so it is not counted twice:

\[ P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \text{ and } B) \]
During the monsoon season, P(rain on a given day) = 0.60, P(flood warning issued) = 0.15, and P(both rain and a flood warning) = 0.10. P(rain or flood warning) = 0.60 + 0.15 − 0.10 = 0.65.

Independent and Dependent Events

Two events are independent if the occurrence of one does not change the probability of the other. Formally, A and B are independent if and only if:

\[ P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B) \]
Checking the rain and flood-warning example from the previous slide: if rain and flood warnings were independent, we would expect P(rain) × P(flood) = 0.60 × 0.15 = 0.09. The actual joint probability was given as 0.10, close to but not exactly 0.09, so these events are not quite independent, which makes sense since flood warnings are issued partly because of rainfall.
Independent events
Knowing one event occurred gives no information about the other. Example: the outcome of one coin flip and the outcome of a separate, later coin flip.
Dependent events
Knowing one event occurred changes the probability of the other. Example: drawing two cards from a deck without replacement.

The Multiplication Rule: Independent Events

For independent events, the probability that both occur is simply the product of their individual probabilities, the same formula used to define independence:

\[ P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B) \]
A factory has two independently operating backup generators, each with a 5 percent chance of failing on a given day. The probability that both fail on the same day is 0.05 × 0.05 = 0.0025, just one-quarter of one percent, which is exactly why redundant independent backups are used: the chance of total failure shrinks dramatically.
This extends to more than two events: for any number of mutually independent events, the probability that all of them occur is the product of all their individual probabilities.

Conditional Probability

The conditional probability of B given A, written P(B|A), is the probability that B occurs given that A is known to have occurred. It is defined as:

\[ P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \text{ and } B)}{P(A)} \]
Among a bank's loan applicants, 40 percent have a co-signer, and 12 percent both have a co-signer and are approved. The probability that an applicant is approved given that they have a co-signer is P(approved | co-signer) = 0.12 ÷ 0.40 = 0.30, or 30 percent.
Independence revisited: A and B are independent exactly when P(B|A) = P(B), meaning learning that A occurred does not change how likely B is.

The Multiplication Rule: Dependent Events

Rearranging the conditional probability formula gives the general multiplication rule, valid whether or not A and B are independent:

\[ P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B \mid A) \]
A class project group is formed by selecting two students at random, without replacement, from a class of 12 students, 5 of whom are statistics majors. P(first student is a statistics major) = 5/12. Given the first was a statistics major, P(second is also a statistics major) = 4/11, since one statistics major and one total student have been removed. P(both statistics majors) = (5/12) × (4/11) = 20/132 ≈ 0.152.
Without replacement changes the probabilities: sampling without replacement makes each successive draw depend on what was already drawn, which is exactly why the second fraction in the example is not also 5/12.

Tree Diagrams for Sequential Events

A tree diagram organizes a sequence of dependent events into branches, with each path's probability equal to the product of the probabilities along that path, exactly as in the multiplication rule.

Each path from the starting point to an end branch represents one possible sequence of outcomes, and its probability is the product of the fractions along that path. The four end-branch probabilities in this diagram sum to 132/132 = 1, confirming the full sample space is accounted for.

Building Toward Bayes' Theorem

Conditional probability lets us calculate P(positive test | has disease), since medical tests are validated by giving them to people whose true status is already known. But the question a patient actually wants answered is the reverse: P(has disease | positive test). These are not the same number, and confusing them is one of the most consequential probability mistakes in practice.

Sensitivity
P(positive test | has disease). How well the test catches true cases. A highly sensitive test rarely misses someone who is actually sick.
Specificity
P(negative test | does not have disease). How well the test clears true non-cases. A highly specific test rarely flags someone who is actually healthy.
The missing ingredient: sensitivity and specificity alone are not enough. Going from "P(positive | disease)" to "P(disease | positive)" also requires knowing how common the disease is in the population being tested, called the base rate or prevalence.

Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem formally reverses the direction of a conditional probability, combining the base rate with the test's known properties:

\[ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \, P(A)}{P(B)} \]

Applied to a diagnostic test, with D for "has disease" and Pos for "tests positive":

\[ P(D \mid \text{Pos}) = \frac{P(\text{Pos} \mid D)\,P(D)}{P(\text{Pos} \mid D)\,P(D) + P(\text{Pos} \mid D')\,P(D')} \]
Prior probability
P(D), the base rate or prevalence of the disease before any test result is known.
Posterior probability
P(D | Pos), the updated probability of disease after the positive test result has been taken into account.

Worked Example: The Medical Test Paradox

A rapid diagnostic test for a disease has 95 percent sensitivity and 90 percent specificity. In the population being screened, the disease has a 1 percent prevalence. A patient tests positive. What is the probability they actually have the disease?

QuantityValue
P(D), prevalence0.01
P(D′), no disease0.99
P(Pos | D), sensitivity0.95
P(Pos | D′), false positive rate0.10
P(Pos) = P(Pos|D)P(D) + P(Pos|D′)P(D′) = (0.95)(0.01) + (0.10)(0.99) = 0.0095 + 0.099 = 0.1085
P(D | Pos) = 0.0095 ÷ 0.1085 = 0.0876, about 8.8 percent.
The paradox: a test that is correct 95 percent of the time on sick patients still leaves a patient who tests positive with under a 9 percent chance of actually being sick. This happens because the disease is rare, so the much larger pool of healthy people, even at a low 10 percent false-positive rate, produces more false positives in absolute terms than the small pool of truly sick people produces true positives.

Common Probability Misconceptions

Base rate neglect
Ignoring how common or rare an event is in the overall population, exactly the mistake that makes the medical test result feel more conclusive than it is.
Gambler's fallacy
Believing that independent random events "owe" a different outcome after a streak, such as expecting tails to be more likely after five consecutive heads on a fair coin. Each flip remains independent, with probability 0.5 regardless of history.
Confusing P(A|B) with P(B|A)
Also called the prosecutor's fallacy in legal settings: the probability of evidence given innocence is not the same number as the probability of innocence given the evidence.
Treating dependent events as independent
Multiplying P(A) by P(B) when A and B are actually related, such as assuming two nearby rivers flood independently when both respond to the same regional rainfall.
Why this matters: every one of these mistakes has the same shape: substituting an easier, intuitive-feeling number for the actual quantity the question asked for.

Anchor Case: A Disease Screening Program

A public health office is deciding whether to roll out the rapid test from the worked example, sensitivity 95 percent and specificity 90 percent, as a mass screening tool in a population where prevalence is 1 percent. A junior official argues: "since the test is 95 percent accurate, anyone who tests positive should be treated as a confirmed case."

The flaw in this reasoning: as the worked example showed, a positive result here corresponds to only about an 8.8 percent chance of actually having the disease, not 95 percent. Treating every positive as confirmed would mean roughly 91 percent of those flagged are healthy people who would be needlessly treated or isolated.
A better policy: use the inexpensive rapid test as a first-stage filter, then confirm every positive result with a second, more specific test before acting on it. Combining two test results, using the multiplication rule for the (approximately independent) tests, drives the false-positive problem down sharply, since a person would need to be incorrectly flagged by both tests.

Module Recap

  • 1A sample space lists every possible outcome of a random experiment; an event is any subset of that sample space
  • 2Probabilities can be assigned classically, empirically from data, or subjectively, and every valid assignment stays between 0 and 1 and sums to 1 over the sample space
  • 3The complement rule, P(A′) = 1 − P(A), is often the easiest path to "at least one" type questions
  • 4The addition rule adds probabilities directly for mutually exclusive events, but must subtract the overlap, P(A and B), otherwise
  • 5Independent events satisfy P(A and B) = P(A)P(B); dependent events require the conditional probability P(B|A) instead
  • 6Conditional probability, P(B|A) = P(A and B)/P(A), measures how a known event changes the probability of another
  • 7Bayes' theorem reverses a conditional probability, combining sensitivity, specificity, and the base rate to find P(disease | positive test)
  • 8Ignoring the base rate, as in the medical test paradox, is one of the most common and consequential probability mistakes in practice

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: A Mass Screening Decision

A health office plans to screen an entire upazila for a disease with 1 percent prevalence, using a test with 95 percent sensitivity and 90 percent specificity. An official proposes treating every positive result as a confirmed case and beginning treatment immediately, citing the test's "95 percent accuracy."

What is wrong with using the headline 95 percent figure to justify this policy?

A single accuracy-sounding number, 95 percent, feels like it should answer the question a patient or official actually cares about.
95 percent is the sensitivity, P(positive | disease), not the chance a positive result is correct, P(disease | positive). Applying Bayes' theorem with the 1 percent prevalence, as in the worked example, gives a positive predictive value of only about 8.8 percent. Treating every positive as confirmed would mean roughly 9 in 10 flagged people are actually healthy. A confirmatory second test before acting on any single positive result would substantially reduce this problem.

Case 2: The "Hot Hand" at the Lottery Counter

A lottery ticket seller notices that a particular number combination has not won in the last 40 draws and tells customers it is "due" to win soon, since by their reasoning every number must eventually balance out.

Is the seller's reasoning a valid use of probability?

A long losing streak feels like it should build up pressure toward a win, the same intuition behind "the longer it takes, the more it owes you."
If each lottery draw is an independent random event, the probability of any specific combination winning is exactly the same on every draw, regardless of how many times it has failed to win before. This is the gambler's fallacy: independent draws have no memory, and "due" is not a property that probability assigns to anything.

Case 3: Adding Probabilities That Overlap

A quality control report states: "12 percent of units have a cosmetic defect, and 8 percent have a functional defect, so 20 percent of units have some defect." Records show that 3 percent of units have both defects.

Is 20 percent the correct figure for "some defect"?

Adding two percentages to get a combined rate feels natural and is the first thing most people reach for.
Since some units have both defects, simply adding 12 percent and 8 percent double-counts those units. The general addition rule applies: P(some defect) = 0.12 + 0.08 − 0.03 = 0.17, or 17 percent, not 20 percent. The 20 percent figure overstates the true defect rate by exactly the size of the overlap.

Case 4: Assuming Two Rivers Flood Independently

A flood insurance model assumes that flooding at two river gauges in the same regional watershed are independent events, each with a 10 percent annual probability of exceeding a critical level. Based on this, the model estimates P(both gauges flood in the same year) = 0.10 × 0.10 = 0.01, or 1 percent, and prices policies accordingly.

What is the risk in assuming independence here?

Multiplying two probabilities together is a quick, clean calculation, and it is tempting to use it whenever two events seem physically separate.
Two rivers in the same regional watershed are both driven by the same regional rainfall, so flooding at one gauge is informative about flooding at the other: these events are very plausibly dependent, not independent. If they are positively dependent, the true P(both flood) is higher than the 1 percent the independence assumption produces, meaning the insurer is underpricing the risk of a single extreme rainfall event causing both rivers to flood at once.

Case 5: A Courtroom Probability Argument

In a trial, a forensic expert testifies that a particular piece of evidence would occur in only 1 in 10,000 innocent people, that is, P(evidence | innocent) = 1/10,000. A prosecutor tells the jury this means the defendant is innocent with probability only 1 in 10,000, that is, P(innocent | evidence) = 1/10,000.

Is the prosecutor's restatement a valid use of the expert's number?

Swapping the order of the two events in a conditional probability sentence feels like a harmless rewording, since both versions use the same two events and the same number.
P(evidence | innocent) and P(innocent | evidence) are generally different numbers, related through Bayes' theorem, which also requires the base rate, here the probability that a random person matching the suspect description is actually guilty before any evidence is considered. This substitution is the prosecutor's fallacy, the courtroom version of the same mistake as the medical test paradox, and it can make weak evidence sound far more conclusive of guilt than it actually is.
