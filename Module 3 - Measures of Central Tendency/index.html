Continuing from Module 2: You already organized the 2,000-student BMI dataset into a frequency table and several charts. This module asks the next natural question: if you could report just one number to describe this distribution, which number, and why?

The Mean

The arithmetic mean (commonly called the "average") is the sum of all values divided by the number of values. It is the balancing point of the data: the point where the data would balance if each value were a weight placed on a number line.

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n} \quad \text{(sample)} \qquad \mu = \frac{\sum x_i}{N} \quad \text{(population)} \]
Worked example: Nine students' midterm scores: 65, 68, 72, 72, 72, 75, 78, 90, 95.
Sum = 65+68+72+72+72+75+78+90+95 = 687.
Mean = 687 ÷ 9 = 76.33.

Properties of the mean

  • Uses every value in the dataset, so it incorporates all available information.
  • Is the algebraic balancing point: the sum of deviations from the mean always equals zero.
  • Is sensitive to extreme values. A single very large or very small observation can pull the mean noticeably in its direction.
  • Applies only to quantitative data, since it requires arithmetic operations on the values.

The Median

The median is the middle value of an ordered dataset: the point that splits the data into two equal halves, with 50% of observations below it and 50% above.

Odd number of observations
Median is the single middle value after sorting. Position = (n+1)/2.

Scores: 65, 68, 72, 72, 72, 75, 78, 90, 95 (n = 9)
Position = (9+1)/2 = 5th value → Median = 72
Even number of observations
Median is the average of the two middle values.

Add a 10th score of 60: 60, 65, 68, 72, 72, 72, 75, 78, 90, 95
Middle two are 72 and 72 → Median = (72+72)/2 = 72

Properties of the median

  • Depends only on the rank (position) of values, not their exact magnitude.
  • Resistant to outliers: changing the highest score from 95 to 9,500 would not change the median at all.
  • Appropriate for ordinal data as well as quantitative data, since it requires only that values can be ranked.

✏ Quick Check

A dataset has 12 observations sorted in order. Which positions hold the two values averaged to find the median?

The Mode

The mode is the value (or category) that occurs most frequently in the dataset. It is the only measure of central tendency that can be applied to nominal (qualitative) data, since it requires only counting, not ordering or arithmetic.

From the scores example: 65, 68, 72, 72, 72, 75, 78, 90, 95. The value 72 appears three times, more than any other value. Mode = 72.
Unimodal
Exactly one value occurs most frequently. Most real-world quantitative distributions are unimodal.
Bimodal
Two values tie for the highest frequency, or the distribution has two distinct peaks, often signaling two underlying subgroups.
Multimodal
More than two peaks. Often a sign that the data combines several distinct populations that should be examined separately.
A bimodal exam score distribution is a warning sign. If a class's scores cluster around 90 and 50 with few scores in between, it usually means two distinct groups exist within the class: students who understood the material and students who did not. Reporting a single mean (perhaps 70) would describe neither group accurately.

Mean, Median, and Mode Under Skewness

When a distribution is symmetric, the mean, median, and mode coincide at the same point. When a distribution is skewed, they separate, and the direction of separation reveals the direction of the skew.

The mean is always pulled in the direction of the longer tail because it accounts for the magnitude of every value, including extreme ones. The median, depending only on rank, stays closer to the bulk of the data.

From the Module 1–2 BMI data: The histogram was right-skewed. As you will verify on Slide 8, Mean (26.06) > Median (25.68) > Mode (25.0), exactly the pattern shown above.

Choosing the Right Measure of Center

The "correct" measure of central tendency is not a matter of preference. It depends on the variable's measurement level and the shape of its distribution.

A practical habit: Whenever you encounter a reported "average," ask two questions: what kind of variable is this, and is the distribution likely to be skewed? Income, wealth, house prices, and city population are routinely skewed; for these, the median is almost always the more honest summary.

The Mean for Grouped Data

When data has already been organized into a frequency distribution (as in Module 2), individual values are no longer available, only class intervals and frequencies. The mean is then estimated using the midpoint of each class as a stand-in for every value inside it.

\[ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum f_i m_i}{n} \quad \text{where } m_i \text{ is the midpoint of class } i \]

The Median and Mode for Grouped Data

Both the median and the mode can be estimated for grouped data using interpolation formulas that locate the value within the relevant class.

Median for grouped data

\[ \text{Median} = L + \left( \frac{\frac{n}{2} - CF}{f} \right) h \]

where \(L\) is the lower boundary of the median class, \(CF\) is the cumulative frequency before the median class, \(f\) is the frequency of the median class, and \(h\) is the class width.

Locating the median class: n/2 = 1,000. The cumulative frequency reaches 698 by the end of class 21.0–23.9 and 1,238 by the end of class 24.0–26.9, so the median class is 24.0–26.9 (L = 24.0, CF = 698, f = 540, h = 3).

Median = 24.0 + [(1,000 − 698) / 540] × 3 = 24.0 + 1.68 = 25.68

Mode for grouped data

\[ \text{Mode} = L + \left( \frac{f_1 - f_0}{2f_1 - f_0 - f_2} \right) h \]

where \(f_1\) is the frequency of the modal class (the class with the highest frequency), \(f_0\) is the frequency of the class before it, and \(f_2\) is the frequency of the class after it.

Modal class is 24.0–26.9 (highest frequency, 540). L = 24.0, \(f_0\) = 460, \(f_1\) = 540, \(f_2\) = 380, h = 3.

Mode = 24.0 + [(540 − 460) / (2(540) − 460 − 380)] × 3 = 24.0 + 1.0 = 25.0
Putting it together: Mean = 26.06, Median = 25.68, Mode = 25.0. Since Mean > Median > Mode, this confirms the right-skewed shape already visible in the Module 2 histogram.

The Weighted Mean

A simple mean treats every value as equally important. A weighted mean assigns different weights to different values, appropriate whenever some observations should count more than others.

\[ \bar{x}_w = \frac{\sum w_i x_i}{\sum w_i} \]
Worked example: Semester GPA
CourseCredit Hours (w)Grade Point (x)w × x
Statistics33.7511.25
Calculus33.5010.50
English24.008.00
Programming43.2513.00
Total1242.75

Weighted mean (true GPA) = 42.75 ÷ 12 = 3.56

Simple (unweighted) mean of the four grade points = (3.75+3.50+4.00+3.25) ÷ 4 = 14.50 ÷ 4 = 3.63

The two answers differ. The unweighted mean incorrectly treats a 2-credit course the same as a 4-credit course. Since Programming carries the most credit hours and the lowest grade point, ignoring credit weight overstates the GPA. Whenever observations represent different amounts (credit hours, population sizes, transaction volumes), the weighted mean is the correct calculation.

When "Average" Misleads

A reported average is a single number standing in for an entire distribution. When the underlying distribution is highly skewed, that single number can create a badly distorted impression of what is typical.

A small garment factory reports an "average monthly salary" of 20,762 taka across its 21 employees. Nineteen sewing-machine operators earn 12,000 taka each, one line supervisor earns 28,000 taka, and the factory manager earns 1,80,000 taka.

Mean salary = 436,000 ÷ 21 = 20,762 taka. Median salary (the 11th value among 21 sorted salaries, which falls among the 19 operators) = 12,000 taka.

The mean is technically correct and deeply unrepresentative. No employee earns close to 20,762 taka: 19 of the 21 employees (90% of staff) earn exactly 12,000. The mean is dragged upward by a single very high salary. The median of 12,000 is a far more honest description of what a typical employee at this factory actually earns.
This pattern recurs constantly: national income statistics, house prices, company salaries, and city-level wealth figures are all commonly skewed by a small number of very high values. Whenever you read "the average is X," it is worth asking whether the median would tell a different story.

Module 3 Recap

Six principles to carry forward:

  • 1The mean uses every value and is the algebraic balancing point of the data, but it is sensitive to outliers and skewness.
  • 2The median depends only on rank, splits the data exactly in half, and is resistant to extreme values.
  • 3The mode is the only measure usable for nominal data and identifies the single most common value or category.
  • 4In a right-skewed distribution, Mean > Median > Mode. In a left-skewed distribution, the order reverses. In a symmetric distribution, all three coincide.
  • 5For grouped data, the mean uses class midpoints, while the median and mode use interpolation formulas that locate a value within the relevant class.
  • 6A weighted mean is required whenever observations carry different importance. Skewed real-world quantities like income are best summarized by the median, not the mean.

📝 Module Quiz

10 questions on mean, median, mode, and their use in grouped and weighted data.

1.Five exam scores: 55, 60, 60, 85, 90. What is the mean?

Sum = 55+60+60+85+90 = 350. Mean = 350 / 5 = 70.

2.Using the same five scores (55, 60, 60, 85, 90), what is the median?

Already sorted: 55, 60, 60, 85, 90 (n=5, odd). The middle (3rd) value is 60.

3.A dataset has 8 sorted values. The median is calculated as:

For an even n, the median is the average of the two middle values, here in positions n/2 = 4 and n/2 + 1 = 5.

4.Which measure of central tendency can be used for nominal data such as favorite color?

The mode requires only counting frequencies, not ranking or arithmetic, so it is the only measure of central tendency valid for nominal categories.

5.A dataset of household incomes contains a few extremely wealthy households. Which measure best describes the "typical" household income?

Income distributions are typically right-skewed due to a small number of very high earners. The median, being resistant to extreme values, better represents the typical household than the mean, which is pulled upward.

6.If Mean > Median > Mode for a distribution, the distribution is:

A long right tail pulls the mean above the median, which in turn sits above the mode. This ordering, Mean > Median > Mode, is the signature of a right-skewed (positively skewed) distribution.

7.In the grouped-data median formula, what does \(CF\) represent?

\(CF\) is the cumulative frequency accumulated in all classes before the median class begins. It tells us how many observations must already be accounted for before reaching the class that contains the median.

8.A student computes their GPA by simply averaging grade points across courses, ignoring credit hours. This is incorrect because:

A 4-credit course represents more academic work than a 2-credit course and should contribute proportionally more to the overall GPA. A weighted mean using credit hours as weights is the correct calculation.

9.An exam's score distribution shows two distinct peaks, one near 50 and one near 90, with few scores in between. This pattern is called:

Two distinct peaks define a bimodal distribution, often indicating that the data combines two different subgroups, for example, students who grasped the material and students who did not.

10.A factory's mean salary is much higher than its median salary. The most likely explanation is:

When the mean substantially exceeds the median, the distribution is right-skewed: a small number of unusually high values (such as a manager's much larger salary) pull the mean upward while the median, being resistant to outliers, remains close to what most employees actually earn.

🎯 Real Cases

Read each scenario, write your interpretation, then reveal what most people miss and what the careful reading looks like.

Case 1: The Startup's "Average" Salary

A 12-person startup advertises itself as a great place to work, citing an "average employee salary of 95,000 taka per month." In reality, ten junior employees earn 35,000 taka each, one senior engineer earns 90,000, and the founder/CEO draws a salary of 7,75,000 taka per month.

Is the advertised figure accurate? What does it conceal?

Most readers accept the figure at face value: a six-figure average salary sounds attractive, and the arithmetic is not actually wrong.
Mean = (10 × 35,000 + 90,000 + 775,000) / 12 = (350,000 + 90,000 + 775,000) / 12 = 1,215,000 / 12 = 101,250, close to the stated figure. But the median, the 6th and 7th values among 12 sorted salaries (both 35,000), is just 35,000 taka. Ten of twelve employees, more than 80% of staff, earn 35,000, nowhere near the advertised "average." The CEO's single salary inflates the mean dramatically. The advertised number is arithmetically correct and practically misleading: it describes no actual employee's pay.

Case 2: National Income Headlines

A news article reports: "Average per-capita income has risen to 3,200 USD, signaling rising prosperity for citizens." A reader who works a typical job notices their own income, and the incomes of most people they know, are well below this figure.

How can "average" income rise while most people's income stays flat or grows much more slowly?

Many readers assume that a rising national average implies that a typical citizen's income is also rising at a similar pace.
National income distributions are almost always strongly right-skewed: a relatively small share of very high earners can raise the mean substantially even while the income of a typical (median) citizen barely moves. This is precisely why economists distinguish "mean per-capita income" from "median household income," and why the latter is usually the more meaningful figure for assessing the experience of a typical citizen. The headline is not false, but reporting the mean alone, without the median for comparison, gives an incomplete and often overly optimistic picture.

Case 3: The Bimodal Midterm

An instructor reports a class midterm mean of 68 out of 100 and concludes that "students performed adequately, with room for improvement." A closer look at the score distribution reveals two distinct clusters: about 40% of students scored between 85 and 95, and about 45% scored between 40 and 55, with very few scores between 60 and 80.

Does the mean of 68 accurately describe this class's performance?

Most readers interpret 68 as describing a class of students who performed moderately, picturing scores clustered somewhere near that value.
The score of 68 falls in a near-empty region of the actual distribution: almost no student actually scored close to 68. The mean here is a mathematical artifact of averaging two separated clusters, not a description of any real subgroup. This bimodal pattern usually signals that two distinct populations exist within the class, perhaps students who mastered a prerequisite concept and students who did not. A single mean obscures this entirely. The more honest summary acknowledges two groups and their separate centers (roughly 90 and 47) rather than reporting one number that represents neither.

Case 4: Comparing Section Averages Without Weights

A department head wants the overall average exam score across three sections of the same course. Section A (15 students) averaged 72, Section B (60 students) averaged 80, and Section C (25 students) averaged 65. The department head computes (72 + 80 + 65) / 3 = 72.3 and reports this as the course-wide average.

Is this calculation correct? If not, what should be done instead?

Averaging the three section means directly seems like the natural shortcut, since it requires no further data and produces a clean, plausible-looking number.
This is incorrect because the sections have very different sizes. Simply averaging the three means treats each section as equally important, even though Section B (60 students) represents far more students than Section A (15 students). The correct approach is a weighted mean using section size as weights: (15 × 72 + 60 × 80 + 25 × 65) / (15+60+25) = (1,080 + 4,800 + 1,625) / 100 = 7,505 / 100 = 75.05. The properly weighted course-wide average (75.05) is noticeably higher than the naive calculation (72.3), because it correctly gives Section B's strong performance more influence, proportional to its larger enrollment.
